# Vivify Flat Characters

**Why it matters.** Flat characters read as *dead, lifeless, lackluster* — cardboard cutouts on the page rather than flesh-and-blood people. Readers don't bond with cardboard, and a cast of them sinks a book even when the plot works. This is a seven-step pass Fox assembled from seven different craft authors; each step adds one dimension that a flat character is missing. Run a thin character through all seven in order: the first five *establish* who the character is, and the last two *complicate* it.

> Fox's framing: steps 1–5 show the reader who the character is; steps 6–7 are different — only once identity is established can you tweak and challenge it.

## The seven steps

### 1. Exaggerate the personality
**Rule.** Don't write a normal everyday Joe or Jane. List the character's main personality traits, rank each 0–10, then push the defining ones from a 5–6 up toward a 9–10 (Fox's joke: "dial it up to 11," from *This Is Spinal Tap*). A breakout main character needs a *big* personality.
**Why it works.** A larger-than-life central character is one of the levers that makes a book a "breakout" — bigger-than-normal personalities are more memorable and carry a story further. Mild, average personalities give the reader nothing to grab onto.
**Examples.**
- *The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo* (Stieg Larsson), Lisbeth Salander — could have been "merely smart"; instead Larsson pushed it to a photographic memory and a genius hacker who can get any information she wants. Could have been "frustrated and resentful"; instead pushed to a near-boundless capacity for revenge.
- *A Confederacy of Dunces* (John Kennedy Toole), Ignatius J. Reilly — "possibly the most exaggerated character in all of American literature": grandiose self-regard, simultaneously lazier than you can imagine, and brimming with moral outrage aimed in entirely the wrong directions.
**Failure mode it prevents.** The average, unmemorable protagonist who is competent but never vivid.

### 2. Add character contrast
**Rule.** After exaggerating a character, build a *second* character who is that personality's opposite. If the lead is suspicious of everyone, make the counterpoint warm and trusting.
**Why it works.** Two reasons, both load-bearing. (1) Contrast *highlights* the first character's traits — someone doesn't read as weird until you stand them next to a normal person, and then they're unmistakably weird by comparison. (2) Opposed personalities generate friction, which is fuel for conflict throughout the storyline.
**Examples.**
- Hermione (rigid rule-follower) vs. Luna Lovegood (unconventional dreamer).
- Walter White (cold, canny calculator) vs. Jesse Pinkman (a bundle of emotional chaos).
- Elphaba in *Wicked* (serious, passionate, principled, blunt) vs. Glinda (frivolous, people-pleasing, vain, but charming).
**Failure mode it prevents.** A lead whose traits never register because nothing on the page sets them off; a cast with no built-in friction.

### 3. Give appearance depth
**Rule.** Describe the character early (don't leave readers picturing the wrong hair halfway through), and make the appearance *mean something* — connect it to who they are. Three options: play *into* type, play *against* type, or do both at once.
**Why it works.** Appearance is otherwise inert detail. Tying it to character turns description into characterization: it either confirms or productively contradicts what the reader expects, and both create depth.
**Examples.**
- *Into type* — Jack Reacher (Lee Child), the 6'5", 230 lb ex-military bruiser whose body matches the physical-combat life. (Fox's aside: this is why casting Tom Cruise drew anger; Alan Ritchson fits the type.)
- *Against type* — Tyrion Lannister (*Game of Thrones*): a dwarf — diminutive — yet a giant of intellect, canny and strategic, his body at odds with his mind.
- *Both at once* — Beast (X-Men): embodies "beast" (hairy, muscular, blue) while also playing against it (the suit, cultured and refined).
**Failure mode it prevents.** Meaningless physical description that does no characterization work; late or contradictory appearance that breaks the reader's mental image.

### 4. Show moral attempts *and* moral mistakes
**Rule.** The character needs both. A **moral attempt**: give them agency, make them active, have them trying to solve a problem and do something good — so readers identify with and like them. A **moral mistake**: even a good hero must be fallible and err in a way that hurts.
**Why it works.** The moral-mistake half comes from John Truby (*The Anatomy of Story*): a hero who is too perfect is unrealistic, and a flaw that causes them to act badly toward others keeps them human. The attempt earns the reader's allegiance; the mistake earns belief. Readers *should* be sympathetic to the failing — so don't make it so heinous or unforgivable that it turns them against the character. The target reaction: "That was a mistake — I understand, and I forgive you, but it was certainly a mistake."
**Example.** *The Queen's Gambit* (Walter Tevis). Moral attempt: Beth Harmon claws out of the orphanage and the valley of her life by learning chess and playing it honestly — no cheating, no cut corners. Moral mistakes: she gets addicted to tranquilizers and booze; after Mrs. Wheatley's death she isolates instead of reaching out; she shows up hungover to a major match; and she discards the people closest to her — Jolene (her orphanage friend), Benny (peer and mentor), and Mr. Shaibel the janitor (who taught her chess).
**Failure mode it prevents.** The passive saint — either a flawless hero the reader can't believe in, or a do-nothing protagonist with no agency.

### 5. Give the character a wound
**Rule.** Give the character a **backstory wound** — and understand precisely what that is. *A sad backstory is not a wound* (the most common mistake here). A wound is something that *keeps happening* in the character's life because it distorts how they act in the present.
**Why it works.** The wound is the mechanism that makes backstory operative rather than decorative: it bends present-day behavior, relationships, and choices, and is the route to a universal truth the reader recognizes. The point isn't the past event; it's the ongoing distortion. Wounds need not be enormous — quiet wounds (an emotionally distant father, a friendship that ended over a misunderstanding) feel big to the character and shape the present just as surely. Mix large and quiet; just make sure there *is* one.
**Example.** *Little Fires Everywhere* (Celeste Ng). Mia agreed to be a surrogate for the wealthy Ryan family, but after her brother's death and her parents disowning her, she couldn't give up the baby — she took Pearl and ran, and they've been on the run for years. That's the backstory; here is the *wound* (present-day distortion): she is deeply distrustful and wary; she and Pearl never stay long anywhere, so all their relationships stay shallow; and the secret she keeps poisons her relationship with her daughter.
**Failure mode it prevents.** A "tragic backstory" that sits inert in chapter one and never touches the character's present behavior; reflexively reaching for only huge traumas (molestation, witnessed murder) when a quiet wound would do.

### 6. Apply the force field of character change
**Rule.** Characters must change — but real change is reached only by pressing *inward*. James Scott Bell (*Plot & Structure*) pictures the self as concentric layers: outer = opinions, then attitudes/values, then the core = self-image and core beliefs. Outside pressure (challenges, obstacles) drives the change; to make it real you must penetrate past the outer layers to the center. Ask of your own story: *how deep does it penetrate the character's sense of self?*
**Why it works.** Change only comes through challenge, because characters — like real people — don't *want* to change; changing is difficult and it hurts, so it takes external pressure to force it. The common authorial failure is to change only the outer layer — opinions and attitudes — which is superficial. Real, satisfying change happens at the core: self-image and core beliefs.
**Example.** Scrooge in *A Christmas Carol* (Dickens), where each ghost penetrates one layer deeper. Ghost 1 shifts his *opinions* — seeing old Fezziwig, he thinks maybe he should be nicer to Cratchit. Ghost 2 reaches his *attitudes and values* — confronted with the children sent to prisons and workhouses, he sees his values aren't healthy. Ghost 3 reaches the *core* — at his own gravestone he grasps how despised he'll be, and his hand in Tiny Tim's death, which shakes his essential understanding of himself and triggers the real transformation into a generous man.
**Failure mode it prevents.** The superficial arc — a character who merely updates an opinion or attitude and is mistaken for one who has genuinely changed.

### 7. Invert the character
**Rule.** Find the point where the character acts *unlike themselves*. This works only because steps 1–5 established who they are: the reader must know the baseline to feel the violation. Then give them a moment of "Whoa — I didn't know you were capable of that." Without characters who can act against their own type and surprise the reader, you don't have flesh-and-blood people; you have cardboard cutouts.
**Why it works.** Inversion is the proof of dimensionality. A character who can only ever do the predictable thing is, by definition, flat; the capacity to surprise *against an established pattern* is what makes them feel real and unpredictable like a person. (Drawn from Debra Spark, *And Then Something Happened*.)
**Examples.**
- From Spark — a paired inversion. In Lore Segal's *Half the Kingdom*, a white woman watches a Black woman jump from a roof to her death and does *nothing* — calls no one, reaches out to no one — and the reader recoils: who is this callous person? Its opposite (from *This American Life*): a cranky, cantankerous man *saves* someone from a suicidal jump, running up to pull them back. The "good" figure fails to act; the apparent antagonist does the saving — both doing the opposite of what we'd predict.
- *Red Rising* (Pierce Brown). The hero lives in a Roman-style, might-makes-right world where only power matters — which is exactly why it's shocking when he upends that philosophy and volunteers to be whipped in front of his team, not for his own wrongdoing but for someone else's. A Judeo-Christian value of self-sacrifice erupting into a "might makes right" worldview — one of the book's most shocking beats, inverting what we thought we knew about him.
**Failure mode it prevents.** The wholly predictable character — perfectly consistent and therefore lifeless. (Caveat implicit in the rule: invert only *after* the baseline is set, or the moment reads as inconsistency, not surprise.)
