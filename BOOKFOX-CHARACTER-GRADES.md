## The nine techniques (Bookfox's framework)

1. **Laugh in the face of cannibals** — a counterintuitive reaction that reveals personality. Used sparingly; must stay believable.
2. **Make your character carry something** — possessions, physical or metaphorical, that reveal character.
3. **Contradictory characters** — one surprising element against the stereotype.
4. **Three things on introduction** — have them *say* something, *do* something, and *show their problem* — fast.
5. **Make them likable** — relatable flaws plus any of: morality, save-the-cat, suffering, competence. (Likable ≠ flawless.)
6. **Odd habit + the WHY** — a strange tic *and* the deeper reason for it.
7. **Widen the perception gap** — distance between how a character sees themselves and how everyone else sees them.
8. **Create foils** — antagonist = inverted protagonist; sidekick = complement; mentor = older version of the protagonist.
9. **Create a 3-time character (most important)** — *haunted* by the past, *wrestling* in the present (two layers of problem), *wary* of the future.

## The headline

Three findings, in order of leverage:

1. **Your book already lives or dies on Bookfox's #1 most-important technique, and it passes at the top of the class.** Technique 9 — the 3-time character — *is* the architecture of *Chasing the Sun*. William is a textbook 3-time character in the psychological sense (haunted by Dublin, wrestling Oregon, wary of every grave to come), and that is exactly why he grades A−. **The pattern of every weak character is the same single failure: they are given a present and sometimes a future, but no past *wound of their own*.** That is the master gap, and it has cheap fixes.

2. **Technique 2 — carry something — is your superpower.** The folded paper of names, Alex's worn compass, Teresa's feathers, Mary Margaret's travelling brooch, Lottie's pencil ledger, Doyle's fused apron. The book's best characterization is almost always an object. **Every cheap lift below routes through this:** the fastest way to lift a thin character is one carried object, not a paragraph of description.

3. **William never loses a contested exchange to a human being — not once in 81,000 words.** This is the real flaw your "belittling antagonist" instinct is circling. The fix is *not* a recurring villain (forced, melodramatic, anachronistic for this record). It is letting him lose **one** existing scene. See the Antagonist Verdict below.

## The three cross-cutting gaps (the real diagnosis)

### Gap 1 — Every weak character is missing the same leg of technique #9: a *past wound of their own*

The book is generous with *present* (everyone is doing something) and decent with *future* (the daughters head to convents, the sons to early graves). What thins the cast is the **past**. Bookfox: *go short on information, long on angst — the past must spill into the present.* Lottie's Kansas, Mary Margaret's anything, Doyle's hardness, Alex's twenty silent years — these characters serve William's past instead of carrying their own. **William grades A on #9 precisely because his past is a live wound; the cast grades down exactly where theirs is decoration.** Every lever marked "#9" below is the same move: give the character one past beat that hurts *them*.

### Gap 2 — The plot's stated center, Alex, is a name and a dirt-pile for 2,600 lines

*"If I stay here, Alex will die"* (l. 2216) is the justification for the entire Oregon wager — built on a child the reader never hears until he walks in as a stranger to sign papers (l. 2680). The continent moves for a boy we cannot lose because we never had him. The compass (the book's single best object, l. 2248 → l. 2690) is already planted to fix this — it just needs the boy to touch it once, young.

### Gap 3 — William wins every human exchange, so he never breaks in front of us

Diagnosed in your own line: *"He wanted to hit something that could hit back. He wanted an enemy he could see"* (l. 1424). He out-charms the customer, out-works the foreman, out-curses the landlord, and *leaves* every city before anyone can best him. The antagonist of the novel is correctly death and his own grief-logic — but a man who never loses to a person flattens. See below.

## The Antagonist Verdict — your "belittling character who shows up later"

**The instinct is right; the form is wrong.** A nemesis who *returns* to degrade William across an ocean and a continent would be melodrama and is untrue to this record — a working-class immigrant who moved cities every few years never stayed long enough to grow a personal enemy, and the book's own logic (he out-works and leaves) makes a sustained rivalry impossible without bending the history.

**The natural, high-leverage version already exists on the page.** The most overtly degrading human in the book is the Boston landlord (ch. 16): *"You ungrateful Paddy cur... your brood of potato-eating brats... worthless Fenian trash"* (l. 1029). But as staged, **William wins it** — he hits first and harder (*"May the devil use your backbone as a ladder!"* l. 1028) and *"The landlord left, muttering."* The degrader is a one-scene weather event, not a foil.

**The move (Bookfox #8 — antagonist = inverted protagonist):** re-stage that single existing exchange so William **loses it once**. Let the nativist line land *unanswered* — not because he's out-argued, but because four of his children are buried in that city and he cannot pay. He is made small in front of Mary and the children, and they move *because he lost*, not because he chose to. That is the inverted foil he's missing: the nativist is the dark mirror of William's whole *"make the name mean something"* project, telling him to his face that the name means *Fenian trash*.

- **One appearance, never recurring.** A belittler who returns is forced; a belittler William *loses to once and never sees again* is the natural Bookfox move.
- **Costs no new scaffolding** — the scene and the man already exist (l. 1024–1030); Boston rent-hikes and nativism are already corroborated in the text (l. 669, l. 1055).
- **Cost: half a scene** (re-pointing an existing exchange).

## The five levers, in priority order

If you only touch five things — chosen for leverage × cheapness, each tied to a Bookfox technique:

1. **Give Alex one childhood line + the compass touch before William leaves** *(#4 / #9)* — makes the plot's silent center a person, seeds the twenty-year carry, and turns the final scene from a stranger's introduction into a callback. Use the boy's plain register, metaphor from the needle and north, not psychology. Do **not** expand to a scene — his power is scarcity. **Beat.**

2. **Let William lose the landlord scene once** *(#8)* — the missing human defeat and the inverted foil, answering your antagonist instinct correctly without a recurring villain. **Half-scene.**

3. **Make Hollis's desk photo his daughter; two glances** *(#2 / #4)* — the framed picture is already on his desk in the opening (l. 11). Define it as the nine-year-old (l. 1011) and let his eye return to it twice, at the "Six" exchange and as the paper crosses the desk. A father of a living child listening to a father of seven dead ones. One planted detail lifts four of his weak grades at once, and the living-child photo *answers* William's dead-children paper. Keep it to **two** glances, not three. **Beat.**

4. **Stop sanding Mary's flaw** *(#5)* — pure subtraction. Her temper is real (*she wrings sheets "as though the sheet had personally wronged her,"* l. 150) but the manuscript keeps retracting it with a reconciling tag ("but she was smiling," l. 352, l. 411). Cut those retractions in the prosperity years and the saint becomes a wife again. A flawless dead wife is harder to grieve than a real one. **Beat–half-scene.**

5. **Give Mary Margaret one want that is not service and not her dead mother** *(#9)* — she has the most page-time of the daughters and the least interior; the text promises "the fierce Mary Margaret" (l. 1950) and delivers dutiful exhaustion. One small private appetite, planted early and paid before she dies at 24, converts the most-seen daughter from a vessel into a person. Can *replace* a redundant "she helped" beat rather than add length. **Beat.**

**Two more nearly free:** root William's counting habit once in ledger language while cutting a duplicate commercial count *(#6, beat)*; surface Lottie's pencil-journal thesis once in the Oregon reunion *(#9, beat)*.
