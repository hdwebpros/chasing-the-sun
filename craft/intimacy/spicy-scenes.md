# Intimate Scenes — Nine Common Mistakes

**Why it matters.** The intimate scene is where craft discipline most often collapses: writers who are perfectly controlled everywhere else suddenly reach for purple synonyms, runaway similes, and anatomical play-by-play. Almost every failure here is the same failure — the author becomes *visible* (straining, euphemizing, gawking) and the reader is yanked out of the characters and into an awareness of the writing. The cure is the same one that runs through the rest of this KB: keep the prose plain, keep whole people on the page, and serve the emotional register the scene actually needs. Many of the cautionary examples below are real winners or shortlistees of the *Literary Review* Bad Sex in Fiction Award — a useful external calibration of where the floor is.

> Sister video: Fox's *nine ways to write* intimate scenes (positive technique) is the companion to this *nine mistakes* list. This file is the avoid-list.

## The nine mistakes

### 1. Thesaurus abuse
**Mistake.** Reaching for exotic synonyms in the intimate scene that you would never use anywhere else in the book — treating "normal language" as somehow forbidden once clothes come off.
**Why it fails.** In ~99% of intimate scenes there is no reason to use a strained phrase when a plain, normal word does the job. The exotic word doesn't elevate the moment; it spotlights the author hunting through a thesaurus, which is the opposite of immersion. One word in particular — **"tumescent"** — is singled out as the worst offender in the entire register: it reads like a medical diagnosis, not desire.
**Examples (clinical, as cautionary specimens).**
- Matthew Reynolds, *The World Was All Before Them*: "She seemed to be surfing the waves of neuromuscular euphoria." (There is already a plain word for that.)
- Michel Houellebecq, *Serotonin*: "offer her moist parts to my triumphant phallus" — "triumphant phallus" is deeply awkward; the passage was a Bad Sex Award **shortlistee** (see Provenance — Fox misremembers it as a *winner*).
- Meredith Duran, *A Lady's Code of Misconduct*: a character's manhood described as a **"tumescent swelling"** — reads like a terminal diagnosis.
**Better approach.** Use the same plain, accessible diction you trust in the rest of the manuscript. If a word would feel show-offy in a dialogue tag, it is show-offy here too. (See [[voice/sound-like-yourself]] on plain Anglo-Saxon word choice.)

### 2. Similes attack
**Mistake.** Letting a simile run wild — a comparison that's meant to clarify or charge the moment but lands grotesque, absurd, or unintentionally comic.
**Why it fails.** A simile in an intimate scene is high-risk: when it works it conveys exactly what's happening, but when it misfires it drags a *worse* image into the reader's head than plain description would have. The reader stops on the image, examines it, and is gone. (Fox's caveat: similes are *not* banned, and aren't a problem at all if you're deliberately going for humor — only if you intend romance and the comparison sabotages it.)
**Examples.**
- Norman Mailer, *The Castle in the Forest*: a limp male member as "a coil of excrement." A simile that could have gone anywhere went the maximally wrong direction. (2007 Bad Sex Award **winner** — verified.)
- Rowan Somerville, *The Shape of Her*: "Like a lepidopterist mounting a tough-skinned insect with a too blunt pin…" — a Kafka-doing-erotica image nobody asked for. (2010 Bad Sex Award **winner** — verified.)
- Sean Thomas, *Kissing England*: "She is so small and compact and yet she has all of the necessary features" — invites the reader to compare a lover to a Sony Walkman. Wacky rather than romantic.
**Better approach.** Pressure-test the image: read it cold and ask what picture it actually puts in the reader's mind. If you're not writing comedy, hold back on the wilder comparisons. (See [[sentence/power]] on figurative-language control.)

### 3. Impossible biology
**Mistake.** Choreography that is physically impossible or wildly improbable — moves the body cannot actually make.
**Why it fails.** This may be the worst failure of all, because a biology error makes the reader acutely aware of *the author making a mistake* instead of the characters doing anything. Immersion depends on the reader trusting that the writer has, in some sense, "done this before"; impossible mechanics break that trust instantly.
**Exception.** Deliberate non-realism is fine. In Neil Gaiman's *American Gods*, a man is wholly enveloped and swallowed by a woman's body — but it's overt fantasy (the goddess Bilquis), signposted as unreal, so there's no broken contract.
**Examples.** Fox cites Peter Høeg's *Smilla's Sense of Snow* as a passage he reads as not physically possible, and A.A. Gill's *Starcross* as merely improbable — a man reaching down and lifting his partner in a way that "beggars belief." (Specific lines paraphrased in the video, not re-quoted here.)
**Better approach.** Write the choreography so it convinces the reader you understand how bodies work. If you must defy realism, mark it as fantasy on purpose.

### 4. God-awful dialogue
**Mistake.** A scene building nicely, and then a character opens their mouth and says something stupid, overwrought, or baffling.
**Why it fails.** Bad dialogue here is a register break: the spoken line exposes the author's strain more nakedly than narration does, and it triggers eye-rolling or outright DNF. The reader's emotional buy-in, painstakingly built, evaporates in one line.
**Examples.**
- Laurent Binet, *The Seventh Function of Language*: "I desire the landscape that is enveloped in this woman. A landscape I do not know but that I can feel. And until I have unfolded that landscape, I will not be happy." — opaque "landscape" talk that signals nothing real. (Bad Sex Award **nominee** — verified.)
- Sebastian Faulks, *Charlotte Gray*: "This is so wonderful. I feel I might disintegrate. I might break into a million fragments." — not the worst imaginable, but over-the-top enough to induce eye-rolling.
**Better approach.** Hold spoken lines to the same plausibility and restraint as the rest of the book's dialogue; under-state rather than over-state. (See [[dialogue/worst-lines]] on lines that betray the scene.)

### 5. Unsexy "sexy" scenes
**Mistake.** Intending an erotic, sensual scene and *accidentally* producing something ridiculous, off-putting, or blind to what sensuality even is.
**Why it fails.** When a scene has "complete and utter disregard for anything like reality" and ignores the essence of sensuality, the reader recoils rather than warms — the scene becomes a poster child for how *not* to do it.
**Important distinction.** This applies only when you're *aiming* for sexy and missing. If the scene depicts coercion, assault, or anything that should *not* read as sexy, then being unsexy is correct, not a mistake.
**Example.** Jade West, *Artless* (title unverified — see Provenance): a couple coupling right next to a dead body, plus painful-to-read mechanics with zero preparation. The mismatch between the staging and any plausible eroticism is the failure.
**Better approach.** Write toward sensuality with some grounding in reality; the litmus test is simply "no scene of yours should be able to win a Bad Sex award."

### 6. Believing it has to be erotic
**Mistake.** A one-dimensional assumption that *every* intimate scene must be erotic and sensual.
**Why it fails.** It throws away most of the scene's range. In romance, yes, the scene will usually be erotic — but in other genres an intimate scene can do far more dramatic work. Locking into "erotic only" flattens the moment and wastes its plot potential.
**The fuller register (keep all four options).**
1. **Funny.** Intimate scenes can be comic. Steve Almond's craft essay *"Hard Up for a Hard On"* (in *The Writer's Notebook*, Tin House) is the model — the title alone announces the comic intent. (Verified.)
2. **Horrifying.** They can be terrifying. Fox cites the ending of Mary Doria Russell's *The Sparrow* as one of the most horrific scenes he's read — and the perfect ending for that book. (Verified: the ending depicts captivity and sexual violation; genuinely harrowing.)
3. **Depressing / sad.** They can be sorrowful rather than joyful when the plot requires it.
4. **Epiphany-bearing.** They can deliver a character realization — e.g. *I'm not supposed to be with this person*, revealed by how they're treated in the bedroom.
**Better approach.** If you only want "stimulating," that's a legitimate choice — but choose it knowingly, aware that comedy, horror, sorrow, and revelation are all on the menu. (See [[emotion/resonance]] on matching emotional register to plot function.)

### 7. Diving into the less-elegant realities
**Mistake.** Dwelling on the awkward, sticky biological facts of intercourse — fluids of all types, gas, mess.
**Why it fails.** When the characters are clearly enjoying themselves but the reader is "ewed out," the prose is working against its own aim. The gross detail makes the reader wonder whether the author is trying to repel them, which is at cross-purposes with the intended experience.
**Better approach.** It is perfectly legitimate to skip the less-elegant biological realities in service of atmosphere and of the feeling you want the reader to have. Selection is not dishonesty; it's craft. (Note the boundary with #5: don't sanitize a scene whose *point* is ugliness.)

### 8. Forgetting about anticipation
**Mistake.** Insta-romance — characters meet and are "doing it" two seconds later, with no build.
**Why it fails.** The most romantically charged part of a book is often *not the act itself* but the lead-up: the small gestures, the flirting, the tension, the *will-they / won't-they* wondering. Skip it and you skip the best material.
**Examples.**
- Bernhard Schlink, *The Reader*: a woman tells a much younger man to take a bath, says she won't look as he undresses — then she does look; friction crackles in the air over *what will happen*. (Verified scene; Fox paraphrases.)
- A.M. Homes, *Music for Torching*: a simple kissing scene carrying wonderful romantic tension. (Verified novel.)
**Better approach.** Don't rush to the explicit material. Give the lead-up real page-time and focus — it is "just as important." (See [[tension/tension]] on sustaining anticipation, and [[scene/chapter-hooks]] on the open-door/withheld-payoff move.)

### 9. The rise of Frankenstein writing
**Mistake.** "Frankenstein writing" — lumbering through the scene as nothing but jerky physical movement, a blow-by-blow of body mechanics like a sports announcer.
**Why it fails.** It reduces people to disembodied parts. The diagnostic question is: **are body parts the protagonist of your scene?** ("His penis did this. Her breast did that.") When parts are the actors — "caressing and stabbing and doing all sorts of crazy verbs" — the human beings disappear, and so does the emotion. The best intimate scenes have a few physical details but *layer them with* thoughts, emotions, dialogue, and metaphor.
**Better approach.** Make **whole people** the subject of your sentences ("This character did this," "This woman did this"), not their anatomy. Treating people as whole people, not parts, is what keeps the scene human. (See [[sentence/power]] on subject choice and agency in the sentence.)
