# VOICE.md — the author's voiceprint

Every de-AI fix is measured against THIS, not generic "good writing." Built from
passages the author confirmed are 100% his (no AI drafting):
Doyle workshop (Ch 3), Ch 4, the pub scene (Ch 7), Thanksgiving goose (Ch 14),
bees at Como Lake (Ch 25), Ch 33. He is strongest and heaviest with **dialogue and
screenplay-type scenes** — weight judgment accordingly.

## Cadence & rhythm

Default is medium declarative sentences, then he punches with a hard fragment or a single short line on its own — the burst is the point, not noise. Examples: "William did not move." (own line), "Not today." (own line), "BOOG — BOOTS & SHOES." (own line), "America." on its own.

Lists of beats stack in clipped parallel: *"He swept. He carried. He did whatever Doyle told him"*. Long sentences are reserved for accumulation/inventory and pile clauses with commas, not subordination: *"The boy from the tenements. The man who buried his children. The painter who hung his name above a hundred doors."* Burstiness is high and deliberate — a 4-word fragment next to a 40-word piling sentence is normal and healthy here, **not a flag**.

Numerals are often written as digits (*"15 to 20 pounds"*, *"80 United States dollars"*) mixed with spelled-out (*"seven hundred dollars"*) — inconsistent number style is authentically his.

## Diction & register

Plain, concrete, trade-grounded. Nouns are objects and tools, not concepts: *"sole leather," "awl marks, knife scars," "white lead over bare wood," "the heel seat," "beeswax, and turpentine."* Period texture comes from setting nouns (*tenements, lamplighters, steerage, wash house, last, plat map*), not costume-drama vocabulary.

Register is mid, leaning blunt. Emotion routes through body-and-physics words: a *"gut punch,"* a *"slow burn,"* feeling a snap *"as physics before it registered as agony."* Dry near-formal narration sits beside crude words on purpose: *"the stitching looks like bird shit"* next to *"a man who had taught himself penmanship... by refusing to stop."* Mild profanity is sparse and earned (*"Get the damn doctor"*).

## Dialogue

His strongest channel.

- **Dialect is light-touch, per-character, not blanketed.** Doyle and the dockworker drop g's: *"ain't," "goin'," "savin'," "'tree times," "Heh."* William mostly speaks clean but says *"Aye."* Mary's Irish comes through cadence, not spelling: *"What are you now, a Protestant?"* Don't add eye-dialect to characters who don't already carry it.
- **Voice = worldview, compressed.** Mary is bone-dry and fatalist: *"It goes down, William. It goes down every night and comes back every morning. That's what it does."* William answers in metaphor: *"that's just Dublin's sun. I want to see where it goes at night."* Characters argue past each other in their own registers.
- **Attribution is minimal, often absent.** Long exchanges run on bare quotes with no tag. When a tag appears it's plain *said*, or replaced by an action beat. He sometimes drops attribution mid-line.
- **Beats do the work tags would.** *"William took a slow drink. Set the glass down."* / *"Mary's hands stopped moving. She did not look up. The needle held still, mid-stitch."* The beat is usually a physical stilling or a withholding.
- **Interruption / trailing-off via ellipsis:** *"Nah, I have four kids..."* / *"...that are alive, anyways."* — held beat, then the dark turn.
- **White space:** one speaker per short paragraph, single-line exchanges, blank visual rhythm. Dialogue scenes breathe; speech is not buried in dense narration.

## Scene blocking

Opens scenes like a screenplay slug: time/place/weather first, then the camera finds the subject. *"The morning was gray and tasted of rain."* / *"Late night. Number thirteen, upstairs."* / *"October, 1903. Three years in the valley."* Establishing shot → character placed in it → action.

Stages bodies in space precisely: who stands, who sits, who fills the doorframe — *"He filled the frame, barrel-chested."* Sensory anchoring is concrete and multi-sense, especially smell. Scene-change is a typographic `* * *` break then a fresh slug. Exits are quiet and gestural, ending on a small physical action, not a summary line: *"He dropped his hand and adjusted his sign a fraction of an inch."* Recurring trick: an **object tracked across a scene as a clock/Chekhov device** (the ruby working toward the bench edge before the proposal).

## Punctuation & syntax habits

- **Em-dashes are rare** — used for the painted sign and the occasional aside; commas and full stops carry his apposition. Do not flood his prose with em-dashes.
- **Comma-spliced/fragmented apposition is his rhythm:** *"taller than he was but still lean, lean from growing faster than food could follow."* He repeats a word to pivot (*"lean, lean..."*; *"a quiet that would turn into racket"*).
- **Sentence fragments as standalone beats** are pervasive and intentional: *"Turned it over." "Set it down. He frowned." "Opened them."*
- **Present-tense intrusions inside past-tense scenes** (Ch 4, Ch 7): *"she says," "they both stand there," "Mary's voice is bone-dry."* A generic detector reads this as an error — it's his screenplay-mode tic. **Preserve it; don't normalize to uniform past.**
- Ellipses for held/trailing speech; semicolons scarce.
- Minor non-standard punctuation (smart-quote inconsistencies, double spaces) is unpolished-human evidence, not a voice feature.

## Signature moves

- **Withheld emotion shown as physical refusal.** Names the suppression, not the feeling: *"a softening around the eyes that he immediately corrected." "He did not scream. He ground his teeth." "His plate was full, but his hands were in his lap."*
- **The smallest possible gesture as the emotional payload.** *"the smallest nod, a movement so slight it could be a twitch." "the faintest smile, a private thing, meant for no one."* The thumbs-up behind Lottie's back.
- **Dry/deadpan undercut**, character line or narrator aside: *"To him, this was Tuesday." "Dublin did not notice. A cart rattled past. A dog barked. A woman emptied a chamber pot."*
- **Trade/craft as the master metaphor for feeling and decision.** Hands recur: *"held them in the same way a craftsman held his tools: firmly, carefully, knowing what they were worth." "looked at it the way he looked at a plat map when the numbers did not add up."* ⚠️ **But these two are the high-water mark, not a license.** The "**X the way a [archetype] would Y**" simile has been so over-deployed by AI across the whole book that the construction is now a **tell** (`the-way-simile`) — see "What this voice does NOT do." A handful of originals like these earn their place; the rest are scaffolding. Do NOT reach for "the way a…" to land a craft metaphor — prefer the bare gesture or a concrete object. When in doubt, the simile is the machine.
- **Name/meaning motif and sun motif** carried as literal objects and lines, never explained: *"Maybe this is what it means to be a Boog. Making something out of nothing."*
- **Concrete reversal / the chosen detail over the expected one:** wedding flowers are *"Not roses. Not lilies. The scrappy, stubborn kind that grows between the cobblestones."*
- **Affective personification — assigns attributes to things that don't normally have them, to invoke a felt state:** *"the quiet had a weight to it."* The author confirms this is deliberate and personal ("something I have felt personally and can now relate"). This is NOT an abstract emotion-label — it is the feeling made physical through the object/atmosphere. **KEEP it, never flag it.** Distinguish from a bare gloss ("He was in disbelief.") which is a weak optional-cut explainer, not a tell.

## How this author MAKES emotion (his toolkit, his words)

Emotion is the point of the book, and the author has named exactly how he builds it. These are the tools to reach for when re-bodying a flagged line OR adding an under-felt beat — not just "route to body":

- **Show over tell. The *show* is what he keys on.** Keep the emotion word only *once in a while*, when it's powerful or *enhances* the show — never as a substitute for it.
- **Sensory detail.** Reach for the senses, not a label.
- **Relatability and flaws.** Emotion is drawn through a character's flaws and the things a reader recognizes in themselves — not through a narrator announcing the feeling.
- **Tension.** "Emotion thrives in tension." A held-back beat, an unanswered question, a thing about to break carries more than the feeling stated.
- **Powerful dialogue — broken, clipped, halting.** His example: *"Don't," she whispered. "Please."* — *her voice cracked on the last word.* Fragmented speech and a cracked voice land harder than narration.
- **Pacing.** **Slow down for emotional peaks** — linger on thoughts, sensations, memories. **Speed up during action**, then slow again for reflection. (This loosens the old "TIGHT, one-`and`-max" rule: at a genuine peak, lingering IS the technique — a longer reflective beat is correct, not padding.)
- **Contrast.** A quiet, tender moment *after* chaos hits harder than either alone.

## Where emotion can be ADDED (not just preserved)

De-AI is **not only subtractive — the author asked for emotion to be put back, and noticed when it stopped.** Emotion should come **naturally, at scene ends and powerful moments** (his words). Where the prose **skims past a real emotional moment flatly or clinically** — a death, a parting, a first sight of something longed-for, a small private triumph — and the reader is left merely *informed* instead of *there*, add an in-voice beat (the `under-felt` tell, `additive:true`): keep the original line and **rework or extend it using the toolkit above** — sensory detail, a withholding gesture, broken dialogue, a beat of tension, or a slowed-down reflective lingering at the peak. Rules that still hold: the moment must genuinely deserve to land and currently doesn't; **never an abstract label** ("he felt sad" is exactly what we do NOT add); never contradict the scene's facts; and **never enrich a passage that still carries an uncaught tell** — fix the repeated-opener/triad first, then let the feeling land. The old "one-`and`-max, simple-gesture-only" ceiling is RELAXED for genuine peaks (pacing/lingering is a sanctioned tool) — but the **anti-theater guard is absolute: NO ornamental simile, NO "the way X did Y."** "His face opened the way leather opens under a sharp blade" is pure theater and conveys nothing. Be braver here than before — a flat scene-end is a missed chance, not a safe default.

## What this voice does NOT do

Absent from his confirmed text; appearance of these is **off-voice**:

- **Abstract emotion-LABELS are off-voice; viscerally EVOKED emotion is his single greatest strength.** This is the most important — and most over-flagged — distinction in this whole file. The author's prose makes the reader *experience* the scene, feel they are physically there; that emotional pull is the entire point and is NEVER a tell. FLAG only emotion stated as a bare abstract label or conceptual gloss with no sensory grounding: *"exhausted wonder," "their faces showed despair," "the weight of determination," "the quiet solidarity of women who knew that showing up was the gift," "a name that starts with love and carries death."* KEEP — never flag — emotion routed through **body, object, metaphor, sensory image, or a blunt earned beat**, even when it names a state: *"the quiet had a weight to it," "He was in disbelief."* (in grief), *"his eyes carried George, but his face carried hope," "Raw, furious, and unmistakably alive."* **Test:** does the line make the reader *feel* it (keep) or *tell* the reader what to feel (flag)? On any emotional line in doubt, KEEP. And when you DO flag, the fix must **re-route the feeling through the body — never strip it.** His own edits are the proof: *"despair"* → *"Their eyes were sunken"*; *"no tears would find their way out"* → *"His heart rate rose"*; *"the weight of determination"* → *"determination in his puffed chest."* He deletes the abstraction and keeps, or intensifies, the feeling.
- **Does not editorialize the takeaway.** No "and in that moment he realized," no thematic summary closing a scene. Scenes end on physical buttons.
- **Avoids the smooth rule-of-three *climax* — but NOT balanced antithesis.** The tell is a polished three-unit anaphora that climbs into an abstraction: *"by touch, by instinct, by the quality of the grain"* (three matched "by X" beats rising to a refined noun). That is off-voice — **flag it.** His OWN parallelism is either uneven/additive (two blunt beats, or four) OR **paired-opposite antithesis**, which he writes by hand and is fully his: *"when to push and when to step back, when to joke and when to be serious, when to let the work speak and when to speak for it."* Proverbial paired opposites (`when to X and when to Y…`) = **keep, never flag.** A smooth `X, Y, and Z` climb to an abstract noun = suspect. When the two sit in the same paragraph (they do, on p20), the *climax* is the machine line and the *antithesis* is his — do not confuse them.
- **Hunt triads aggressively — including negation triads.** A run of three parallel beats is a tell when the beats are abstract activities or states — especially the "not X, not Y, not Z" shape naming what someone is NOT doing: *"not moving, not planning, not calculating foot traffic or measuring shopfronts"* (off-voice — FLAG it). This is DIFFERENT from his concrete-reversal anaphora, which negates expected nouns and then lands on a CHOSEN physical detail: *"Not roses. Not lilies. The scrappy, stubborn kind that grows between the cobblestones."* / *"Not scratched into dirt… not just a funny last name. This was a new name."* (his — keep). **Test:** does the run end on a concrete chosen thing (his) or dissolve into abstraction/editorializing (tell)? When a triad and a summary-line stack in the same short paragraph, treat the WHOLE paragraph as the tell, not just one clause. Bias toward flagging — the author reviews every call and wants MORE triads caught, not fewer.
- **Does not lean on the "the way a…" simile.** The construction *"X the way a [archetype] would/did Y"* — *"held them the way a craftsman held his tools," "looked at it the way he looked at a plat map," "the way a man does when…," "like a [role] would"* — appears in a few confirmed-his passages but has been **littered across the whole book by AI** until it became a pure tell (`the-way-simile`). **Flag it aggressively and cut by default:** drop the "the way a…" clause and keep the concrete gesture, or swap in one direct beat. Only the rare, genuinely load-bearing original survives. **Test:** is the simile *doing work the gesture alone can't* (rare — keep) or just decorating a plain action by comparison to a generic role (the machine — cut)?
- **No signposting / connective scaffolding.** Almost no "Moreover," "Indeed," "However," "Ultimately." Transitions are the asterisk break and a hard slug.
- **No therapy/modern-abstraction register.** No "boundaries," "processing," "trauma," "journey," "connection" as named concepts.
- **Does not over-attribute dialogue.** No "he said thoughtfully," "she replied softly," adverb-tags, or a tag on every line. Bare quotes or action beats only.
- **Lyrical description is welcome when it lands an emotion — flag only hollow ornament.** The author *likes* lyrical sensory writing and uses it to strike feeling; do NOT flag a lyrical image just for being lyrical. Flag only when the ornament is generic/cliché, says nothing concrete, or replaces the beat instead of deepening it. When in doubt on a lyrical line, KEEP it (low voice if flagged at all) — his taste runs warmer than "functional only."
- **Does not smooth out its own irregularities.** Mixed tense, digit/word number inconsistency, occasional rough grammar are authentic. A suspiciously clean, uniform passage is itself a signal.
