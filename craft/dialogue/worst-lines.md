# Worst Dialogue Lines — and Fixes

**Why it matters.** A single bad line of dialogue is the kind of thing a reader throws the book across the room for — it breaks the spell instantly, because dialogue is the place where the author's hand is most visible. When a line is wrong, the reader stops hearing a *character* and starts hearing the *author* working the marionette strings. The cure is the same one as voice (see [[voice/sound-like-yourself]]): stop asking "what do I need this line to do for me?" and ask "what would this specific human, in this specific circumstance, plausibly say?" Below are the ten recurring failure modes, worst-to-least, each with the mechanism, Fox's actual example, and the fix.

> Master principle (Ted Tally, screenwriter of *The Silence of the Lambs*): **"What's important is not the emotion they're playing, but the emotion they're trying to conceal."** Most bad dialogue has only one level — bare information delivery. Good dialogue has a second level underneath: what is the character trying to *prevent* you from knowing? Where are they being dishonest? Subtext is the difference between a person and a cardboard cutout.

## The ten failure modes

### 10. The Department of Redundancy Department
**The bad pattern.** Saying the same beat two or three times in a row — the action, then the sound, then the verb.
**Why it fails.** Each element already communicates the whole thing; stacking them insults the reader's comprehension and adds nothing but bulk. The doubled-up dialogue tag is the everyday version (an adverb tag that re-states what the line already proves).
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: "Aro started to laugh. 'Ha ha ha,' he chuckled." (Stephenie Meyer, *New Moon*)
- Fixed: keep **one** of the three. *"Aro started to laugh."* — or just the sound, *"Ha ha ha,"* — or just *"he chuckled."* Any one carries it; never all three.
- Everyday version (bad): *"I'd just love to be shot in the face with a shotgun," she said sarcastically.* The sarcasm is already on the page; the tag is a stage direction telling the reader how to feel.
**The fix principle.** Pick the single element that does the work and cut the rest. Trust the line to land without a tag narrating it.

### 9. Melodrama
**The bad pattern.** A character suddenly speaks in an inflated, near-Shakespearean register the moment emotion rises — language no real person would use.
**Why it fails.** It is over the top and cringe, and you can *feel the author hovering over the scene*, pulling the strings of the marionette. The character is saying what the author thinks they *should* say in a Big Emotional Moment, not what a human would actually say.
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: "I'm haunted by the kiss that you should never have given me. My heart is beating... hoping that kiss will not become a scar." (Anakin to Padmé, *Star Wars: Attack of the Clones*) — Anakin speaks normally everywhere else, then switches to this affectation only here.
- Fix direction: ask what *this human, in this circumstance,* would plausibly say. Go for plausibility over poetry.
**The fix principle.** Make the character sound like themselves, not like you reaching for grandeur. Realism beats elevated diction.

### 8. Naked Dialogue
**The bad pattern.** A character announces, in dialogue, exactly who they are — a tidy thesis statement of their own personality.
**Why it fails.** People do not deliver neat summaries of themselves out loud; it does not happen. So it reads as unintentionally overwrought. It is telling, in the worst place to tell. It also collapses to one level — pure self-declaration, no concealment.
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: "I'm weird. I'm a weirdo. I don't fit in and I don't want to fit in. Have you ever seen me without this stupid hat on? That's weird." (Jughead, *Riverdale*)
- Fix direction: **show** the character being weird through action and behavior instead of having them label it. If you want genuine introspection, put it in their *thoughts*, not their mouth — and consider letting them get it *wrong* about themselves (more revealing than an accurate self-summary).
**The fix principle.** Show who they are; don't let them announce it. Self-knowledge spoken aloud is almost always false-sounding.

### 7. Dialect Gone Wrong
**The bad pattern.** Heavy, scattershot phonetic misspelling meant to convey an accent — so mangled the reader can't even tell which accent it is.
**Why it fails.** Too many misspellings make dialogue genuinely hard to read. And random mistakes don't represent any real language — every language interferes with English in its *own* specific way. Throwing a generic pile of errors at the page does not produce a believable speaker. (Fox's example was also partly **expositional** — the speaker tells the listener facts the listener already knows.)
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: the Eastern-European phone-call dialogue in Dan Brown, *Inferno* — so botched Fox couldn't decide if it was meant to be Eastern European or Asian, and wouldn't read it aloud.
- Fix direction: **a little misspelling goes a long way** — e.g. swapping "e" for "is" once is plenty. Then use the *right* mistakes for that specific dialect: does this language drop verbs? omit articles? invert adjectives? Match the interference pattern to the real language.
**The fix principle.** Light touch, correct mistakes. Render dialect through grammar and word order more than spelling. (See [[dialogue/dialect-gradient]] for measuring a dialect slope across a book.)

### 6. Absurd Swerves
**The bad pattern.** A jarring, unmotivated tonal lurch mid-scene — dead-serious one second, flippant the next — often combined with implausible staging.
**Why it fails.** Several mechanisms at once: (1) the character is essentially talking to himself out loud, which makes no sense; (2) redundancy of the bad kind — the same thing said three times; (3) the abrupt switch from intense concentration to casual greeting is unmotivated; (4) believability — he would have noticed the other person long before.
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: "I did not hit her. It's not true. It's bullshit. I did not hit her. I did not. Oh hi, Mark." (Johnny, Tommy Wiseau, *The Room*)
- Fixed (Fox's rewrite direction): make it a real conversation with Mark, not a soliloquy. Give it subtext — Johnny tries to convince Mark, but the reader doubts him. *"I could have hit her, and I wanted to hit her, but I didn't do it."* Then Mark agrees in a way that signals he doesn't quite believe it: *"Yeah, I believe you. Sure, man."*
**The fix principle.** Ground the line in a real exchange with stable motivation, and let the characters say the opposite of what they mean. Subtext turns cardboard into people.

### 5. Forced Backstory
**The bad pattern.** Two characters rehash shared history in dialogue purely so the *reader* learns it — the "As you know, Bob" maneuver.
**Why it fails.** Ask: *who is this dialogue for?* Not the listener — they already lived it. It's aimed past the characters at the audience, which is why it rings false. (Even strong literary authors do this.)
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: the wife in Paul Auster, *Sunset Park*, walking her husband through all the times he taught their son baseball — i.e. *"As you know, Morris, you used to be close. You used to teach him baseball."*
- Fixed: let the dialogue be a **spark**, not the delivery truck. One line — *"Remember how much time you spent in the park teaching him baseball?"* — then drop into the character's *thoughts* and let him reminisce, surfacing a particular memory. The line lights the fuse; interiority does the heavy lifting.
**The fix principle.** Use a single line of dialogue to trigger memory, then carry the backstory in thought. Never make characters narrate what they both already know.

### 4. Getting a Little Lectury
**The bad pattern.** A character mounts a soapbox and stays there — a monologue that runs pages, with no back-and-forth.
**Why it fails.** A little grandstanding is fine; a 75-page speech is not. It isn't even dialogue (no exchange) — it's monologuing. And the character degrades into a *mouthpiece* for the author's ideas rather than a human being. There should be space between the character's beliefs and yours.
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: John Galt's marathon radio speech in Ayn Rand, *Atlas Shrugged* (commonly cited as ~70–90 pages), straight advocacy for Objectivism. *(Note: the page count is famously large; Fox cites it as the archetype of the over-long speech.)*
- Fixed (Nathan Englander's method, per Fox's interview with him): write out everything the character thinks about the topic — gush a couple of pages onto the page — then **condense**, sometimes down to a paragraph, sometimes to a single line. Spill all the beans, then keep only what is essential to the book.
**The fix principle.** You earn your ideas by compression. Draft the whole rant, then cut to the one thing that must be said, so the reader isn't slogging.

### 3. Robotic Dialogue
**The bad pattern.** Every line is the blandest, most expected response possible — you can predict the next line as you read.
**Why it fails.** Two problems. (1) *Content:* the dialogue exists only for **information delivery** — it's a UPS truck dropping plot packages, with nothing about characterization, no playfulness, no surprise. Unpredictability is what keeps a reader from getting bored; if you can guess every reply, the tension dies. (2) *Rhythm:* the lines are clunky, manufactured-in-a-lab, lacking the swoop and swerve of real speech.
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: James Redfield, *The Celestine Prophecy* — *"It's certainly lovely, isn't it?" "It certainly is. I've never seen anything quite like it." "Is this your first visit here?" "Yes, it is. I don't know much about the place." ... "You're a scientist, then?" "I teach physics at a small college in Maine."* — for pages.
- Fix direction: let dialogue do work beyond plot — reveal *who someone is*; let a character crack a joke, say something unexpected, change the topic, refuse to be earnest.
**The fix principle.** Make the next line unguessable and let each line characterize. Two craft tools: transcribe real conversations to learn how speech actually moves, and read your dialogue aloud to hear whether it sounds real or manufactured.

### 2. Too Punny
**The bad pattern.** A character whose lines are wall-to-wall puns, all clustered on one theme (usually their own name or power).
**Why it fails.** Puns aren't inherently bad, but a few go a long way — and the real trouble is that every pun is themed around his abilities and name, which is too on the nose.
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: Mr. Freeze in *Batman & Robin* — "Allow me to break the ice. My name is Freeze. Learn it well, for it is the chilling sound of your doom." / "Let's kick some ice!" / "Tonight's forecast: a freeze is coming."
- Good (Terry Pratchett, *Discworld* — how to do puns *well*): Magrat says a broomstick is "one of them sexual metaphor thingies," with the footnote "Although this is a fallacy. Fallacy. Fallacy." And: a man in shiny gold armor feels like a class traitor — "guilt by association," then guilt as in *guilt*, guilt as in *gilt* (golden). *(Pratchett wording is paraphrased from the video.)*
**The fix principle.** Don't go overboard, and don't theme every pun on one obvious peg. Wit that surprises (Pratchett) beats wit that telegraphs (Mr. Freeze).

### 1. Diction-Level Change-Ups
**The bad pattern.** A single character speaks (or thinks) at two incompatible diction levels — lofty thesaurus words in one breath, very casual filler in the next.
**Why it fails.** A character should hold one diction level consistent with who they are. When a narrator's *interior* voice is high-flown and their *spoken* voice is low, that's not a person — it's a construct stitched together by the author. (Corollary "axe to grind": a PhD character who speaks like an average person is unbelievable — get a real expert onto their subject and their diction *spikes* immediately.)
**Example (bad → fixed).**
- Bad: E. L. James, *Fifty Shades of Grey* — narration reaches for "somnambulant," "subaqueous," "primal carnal rhythm," while the spoken/thought voice runs on "Jeez," "Holy crap" (≈100×), "Oh my" (≈50×), "Holy cow." Thesaurus abuse in the head; very low diction out of the mouth.
- Bad: *Morbius* — a Nobel-nominated PhD scientist says "It's lethal to bats, but deadly to humans," as if *lethal* and *deadly* weren't synonyms. (No expert talks like that.) *(This is the widely-memed phrasing; the literal film line is reported variously, e.g. "deadly to bats, fatal to humans" — the craft point stands either way: redundant near-synonyms in an expert's mouth.)*
**The fix principle.** Keep one consistent diction level per character, matched to their education and world; when an expert is on their home turf, let the vocabulary rise to match. Mismatch reads as authorial puppetry.
