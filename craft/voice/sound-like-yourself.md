# Sound Like Yourself

**Why it matters.** Agents reject on the first paragraph and readers DNF on the first page when prose reads *robotic / mechanical / dutiful* — the tell that there's no human behind it (today: that it reads as AI). The unit of practice is one paragraph (3–5 sentences) that sounds fully like you, or fully like the character. Voice is the cure for mechanical prose.

> Diagnostic test (Elmore Leonard, *10 Rules of Writing*, NYT July 2001, rule 10 — **verified**): **"If it sounds like writing, I rewrite it."** Prose that "sounds like writing" is stiff and over-proper — the opposite of voice.

## The nine steps

**Rule.** Drop the schoolteacher baggage. Fragments — yes (most bestsellers use them). Open with a conjunction — yes (the anti-conjunction "rule" is a ~300-year-old invention). Misspell or coin words — yes, *if you can pull it off*. "Respectable" language — not required. **One rule only: trust your quirkiness.**
**Why it works.** The inherited rules make you write to satisfy an imagined authority (the English teacher in your head) instead of yourself — and writing-to-satisfy-an-authority is the exact source of the "fulfilling a duty to the page" deadness from the intro. Voice can't coexist with permission-seeking; the energy has to transfer unfiltered.
**Failure mode it prevents.** Correct, joyless prose that breaks no rules and has no pulse — the kind an agent rejects in two paragraphs.
> House note: this is permission, not license. The manuscript's VOICE.md already bans certain shapes (e.g. dependent-clause openers). VOICE.md overrides "you can do anything."

### 2. Know what voice *is* (learn to recognize it)
Train the eye on before/after pairs — mechanical vs. alive:

| Mechanical (no voice) | Alive (voice) | What changed |
|---|---|---|
| "Sarah was tired as she drove to work on a Monday." | "Another Monday, another caffeine emergency." | verve, compression, attitude |
| "On her way she stopped at the coffee shop and it was a mess." | "Sarah shuffled into Been There Done That — whoever named this place deserved both a raise and a stern talking-to — and nearly turned right back around." | active verb (*shuffled*), specificity (named shop), a pun, a parenthetical holding two feelings at once |
| "The line was so long she worried she might not have time." | "The line snaked through the shop like a line to get into a funeral: slow-moving and full of people who'd rather be anywhere else." | simile, fresh image, sentence variety against the prior two |

Build recognition by reading books with strong voice until it seeps into your unconscious.

### 3. Discover your *natural* voice (the unskippable step)
Record yourself talking for 1–2 minutes on a prompt ("I'll tell you what doesn't make any sense…" / "I remember when you could…"). Play it back, ideally **transcribe it**. How you sound aloud — rushed-and-smashed vs. deliberate-and-measured — is the best clue for how you should sound on the page. (Fox ran exactly this exercise teaching at USC/Chapman: when a student's page didn't match their speech, they were faking it on the page.)

### 4. Describe your voice
Pin it down with **three adjectives** (per book, if your voice shifts between books).
- *Forest Gump* (Winston Groom): colloquial, folksy, down-to-earth — and unmistakably unique; you hear Tom Hanks through the spelling and syntax.
- *The Devil Wears Prada* (Lauren Weisberger): energetic, stream-of-consciousness, with staccato bursts.

### 5. Check your voice against the character
Decide how far the character's voice sits from yours (close if autobiographical; worlds apart otherwise). **POV governs how much of you bleeds through:** first person → channel the character; omniscient → your voice carries the narration, switching to the character only in dialogue. **Create characters voice-first:** before hair/eye/height, write the single line that character would say that encapsulates them. Once you can hear them, the rest follows.

### 6. Choose a tone
Decide the attitude the reader will feel — calm, serious, sarcastic, depressed, nonchalant. Holding an attitude in mind makes word choice and pacing fall into place subconsciously. (Vonnegut, *Slaughterhouse-Five*: cynical, world-weary, "so it goes," dark humor. Douglas Adams / Terry Pratchett: every paragraph asks "what would make this funny?")

**Rule.** Make each sentence's rhythm contrast with its neighbors, and **vary sentence length**.
**Why it works.** Voice is heard, not seen — and the ear registers *change*. A run of same-length, same-shape sentences flattens into a drone the reader stops hearing (this is the mechanical/AI tell). Contrast re-engages the ear sentence by sentence; the variation itself is a signature.
> *Devil Wears Prada*: first sentence = 35 words, **zero** commas (flowing, uninterrupted); next = 5 words, **four** commas (staccato, jerky); last = 6 words.
**The subtler lever — internal segmentation.** Length isn't the only knob. *Forest Gump*'s four sentences look roughly equal in length yet break into 2 / 3-short / 3-long / 4 parts — the rhythm lives in *how* each sentence is chopped, not just how long it is. So "vary your sentences" is really "vary their internal architecture."
**When to do it.** Mostly in **revision**, not drafting — in the draft, let your natural speaking self carry the rhythm; tune later. (Forcing rhythm mid-draft is what produces self-conscious, "writerly" prose — see Leonard's rule at top.)

### 8. Deliberate word choice
The same meaning in high-falutin words reads pretentious; in second-grader words, accessible.
> Quiz (Barbara Kingsolver, *Demon Copperhead*): "The company put the ___ on any choice other than going into the mines." Options: *kibosh* / *chokehold* / *shut hole* / *clamp down*. She chose **"shut hole"** — a coined word (you have the writer's license to invent), symbolically apt (a mine is a hole; the only open hole is the one you're forced into), and plainly Anglo-Saxon/accessible. Note: her right word is not necessarily *your* right word.

### 9. Read it aloud (last)
Your **ear is smarter than your eye**. Take a paragraph (yesterday's or last year's) and read it aloud — the ear catches whether voice actually landed. Last step because it's the final arbiter; loop back to Leonard's rule (see top): if it sounds like writing, rewrite it.
