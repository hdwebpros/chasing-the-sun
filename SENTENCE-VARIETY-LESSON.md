## Part 1 — What Bookfox actually says

**Source:** [*How to Write a Sentence / 16 Ways to Write Sentences that Dazzle*](https://thejohnfox.com/2020/07/how-to-write-a-sentence/),
thejohnfox.com.

His core claim about monotony, verbatim:

> *"If you just write SVO sentences, they get very, very boring, and you will put all of your
> readers to sleep."*

SVO = Subject-Verb-Object. *"He looked at the apron. He stood in the doorway. He set down the
crate."* are all SVO. That is the pattern you flagged about yourself. So your instinct matches
his named problem — good. **But note what he does *not* say:** he doesn't put a number on it
("three in a row"), and elsewhere he's explicit that SVO repeated on purpose has "a kind of
music to them." His complaint is **unrelieved** SVO, not SVO itself.

His prescribed fixes (his three named techniques, with his own examples):

| Bookfox's fix | His example | On-voice for you? |
|---|---|---|
| **Preposition opening** | *"In just a moment, he'd realize he should have obeyed the sign."* | ✅ usable — you already do this ("On winter evenings…", "Outside, the valley was dark") |
| **Adverb + comma opening** | *"Carefully, he put the meat inside the crocodile's mouth."* | ⚠️ use rarely — a single adverb-comma is fine, but it's not a strong part of your voiceprint; don't lean on it |
| **Dependent-clause opening** | *"When the zookeeper said she could have what she wanted, he didn't expect…"* | ❌ **OFF-VOICE.** VOICE.md: thin density *"not by adding subordinate-clause openers, which are off-voice."* Do **not** use this one. |

His summary line, verbatim:

> *"Little tiny variations like this don't seem to matter much, but they have a tremendous
> affect on the reader."*

**`[my synthesis]`** So Bookfox gives you the *diagnosis* (unrelieved SVO dulls the reader) and
one clean *fix* you can use (lead with a prepositional/time phrase), one to use sparingly
(adverb-comma), and one to **ignore** (dependent clauses). His other on-voice escape hatches are
the ones already in VOICE.md — fold the look into the object, change the grammatical subject,
comma-splice two beats — but those are *yours*, not his; I'm not going to attribute them to him.

## Part 2 — What Alyssa Matesic actually says

She has two relevant, separately-sourced points. Both map directly onto your Gap-8 bullets
about `something` / `small` / `quiet` and the repeated lamp ending.

### 2a. Pet phrases

**Source:** [*8 Mistakes in Writing That Sabotage Your Book*](https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/writing-mistakes-sabotaging-your-story)
(Mistake #2), plus her [pet-phrase post on X](https://x.com/AlyssaMatesic/status/2060029190712512720).

Her definition, verbatim from the X post:

> *"A pet phrase is a unique, descriptive turn of phrase that YOU love to use — and don't
> realize you're repeating."*

Her real example: an author described *"nearly every character"* with the construction
**"they [did X] with the [Y] of someone who [Z]…"** — and it appeared **13 times in the first
few chapters.** Her warning:

> *"You won't catch these on a read-through because they sound natural to you, but your reader
> will notice… once they do, you've broken their immersion."*

From the article, her threshold and fix, verbatim:

> *"If you say 'gold-flecked eyes' three times on one page, you need to vary the prose."*

> **Fix:** *ask beta readers to flag repeated phrases, then search your manuscript for every
> instance and reword the variations substantially* — or cut them.

**`[my synthesis]`** This *is* your `something` ×134, `small` ×115, `quiet` ×97 problem, named
by a professional editor. Her method is exactly the deterministic search-every-instance pass
your `/tic` tool already does. And her threshold ("three times on one page") tells you the bar
isn't "never use the word" — it's "don't let it cluster."

### 2b. Stock / overused gestures

**Source:** same article, Mistake #6.

The clichés she names, verbatim:

> *"letting out a breath they didn't know they were holding,"* feet *"padding down a hall,"*
> smiles that *"didn't reach their eyes."*

Her suggested alternatives, verbatim:

> Instead of a held breath: *"the air hissed behind her teeth,"* or show the response through
> *"shoulders relaxing"* — describe the gesture *"in another way"* using fresher language.

Her verdict:

> *"These make prose sound amateurish and seasoned readers will pick up on it."*

**`[my synthesis]`** Note her fix is **not** "delete the gesture." It's **re-body it with a
fresher, more specific physical detail** — which is identical to your VOICE.md instruction
("re-route the feeling through the body, never strip it"). She and your own voiceprint agree.

### 2c. Don't do this pass yet (her sequencing rule)

**Source:** [her "repainting a car" note](https://substack.com/@chapterbreak/note/c-240176170) /
*7 Harsh Truths About Revising*.

> *"Pretty sentences won't fix a broken story… making every sentence sparkle is like repainting
> a car that has no engine."*

This is why CRAFT-REVIEW puts Gap 8 in **Phase 3**, after structure. Worth remembering before
you spend a weekend on sentence openers: per Matesic herself, this is the *last* pass, not the
first.

## Part 3 — Applying their advice to your four Gap-8 bullets

Each fix below is tagged with whose verified advice it rests on.

### Bullet A — the clipped "He [verb]" openers
**Rests on:** Bookfox's SVO-monotony point + his *preposition-opening* fix (the one on-voice
technique of his three).

Per Bookfox, the problem is **unrelieved** SVO, not SVO itself — so only fix the runs that
aren't doing a job. **Real before, line 2399** (a low-stakes business paragraph):

> He started in town. The first lot was on the southwest edge of downtown… He walked the raw
> clay with his stick and studied the survey stakes. He stood on the corner for twenty minutes
> watching foot traffic before he made up his mind.

Applying **Bookfox's preposition/time-phrase opening** to break the run (and folding two SVO
beats into one, which is your own comma-splice habit, not his):

> He started in town. The first lot sat on the southwest edge of downtown, the streets still
> being graded. **On the corner, he stood for twenty minutes** watching foot traffic, pricing
> it in his head, before he made up his mind.

**Do NOT apply** Bookfox's dependent-clause fix here (*"Having studied the stakes, he…"*) — that's
the off-voice one.

**`[my synthesis]` — the keep test:** Bookfox says SVO has "music" when intentional. The
six-in-a-row after Mary Margaret's death (line 1989: *"He stood up. He closed his daughter's
eyes…"*) is that music — a grief ritual. Leave peaks alone; only vary the errand prose.

### Bullet B — "something" (the emotion shim)
**Rests on:** Matesic's pet-phrase rule ("vary the prose" when a phrase repeats; reword
substantially).

Her rule says vary the *repeated* ones, not all of them. **Keep** the instances where
"something" is paid off in the same breath (line 102: *"something shifted in his expression, a
softening around the eyes"* — the concrete image lands immediately). **Reword** the ones left
abstract. Real before, **line 2014**:

> …and something happened to his face that he could not control.

Per Matesic (search every instance, reword substantially) — and the concrete version is sitting
in your very next line, so promote it:

> …and his face moved before he could stop it. His cheeks pushed up toward his eyes.

### Bullet C — "small" / "quiet"
**Rests on:** Matesic's pet-phrase threshold ("three times on one page = vary it").

Her bar is **clustering**, not total count — so you don't touch all 212. **Keep** the ones doing
real work (VOICE.md cites *"the quiet had a weight to it"* as a strength; Matesic would agree —
it's specific, not a crutch). **Vary** only where "small/quiet" is the reflexive default. Her
own method: replace the vague word with the **fresher, concrete cause** underneath it
(her *"the air hissed behind her teeth"* logic).

- Reflexive: *the room went quiet*
- Matesic-style re-body: *the scrape of chairs stopped* / *no one reached for the bread*

### Bullet D — "the lamp burned low" ×6
**Rests on:** Matesic's stock-gesture rule (Mistake #6) — a repeated phrase the reader clocks;
re-body it in another way rather than deleting.

This is her exact category: a phrase you love, repeated until the reader notices (six scene-
endings between l.1515–2559). Per her advice — keep the strongest, reword the rest with a fresh
concrete image (not a held breath but *"the air hissed behind her teeth"*; not a sixth lamp but
a new object):

- **Keep** the strongest (l.1544: *"He looked at it until the lamp burned low"* — haunting).
- **l.2392 reword:** *He sat at the desk until the ink dried on the nib, then went outside…*
- **l.2443 reword:** cut the lamp entirely — *The rain continued. William sat with his face in
  his hands and he shook.* (the rain + the shaking are the stronger image)
- **l.2559 (Lottie) reword:** *The coffee had gone cold at her elbow.* (same "been here too
  long" beat, fresh object)

## Part 4 — Where their advice and your voice COLLIDE (read this twice)

This is the part generic advice gets wrong, and it's why you've been "getting in trouble."
**Bookfox and Matesic are writing for a general audience. You have an established voiceprint
that overrides them on three specific points:**

1. **Bookfox's dependent-clause opener — REJECT.** His *"When the zookeeper said…"* fix is
   off-voice (VOICE.md, the "Resist" note). Use his *preposition/time-phrase* opener instead;
   it does the same de-monotony job and is already in your book.

2. **Neither of them sanctions cutting your intentional repetition.** Bookfox himself says
   repeated SVO has "music." Your grief-ritual runs (line 1989), your refusal beats (*"He did
   not pour whiskey or sing or weep"*), your screenplay-clipped parallel — those are signature,
   not the mistake. A naive reading of "vary your sentences" would sand those off. Don't.

3. **Matesic's "reword substantially" ≠ "delete the feeling."** Her fix re-bodies a stale phrase
   with a *fresher physical one* ("the air hissed behind her teeth"). It is **never** "replace
   the gesture with an abstract label." That matches VOICE.md exactly: re-route through the body,
   never strip. If a fix ever leaves you with *less* concrete physicality, you've misapplied her.

**`[my synthesis]` bottom line:** Take from Bookfox the *diagnosis* (unrelieved SVO dulls) and
his *one* on-voice fix (lead with a prepositional/time phrase). Take from Matesic the *method*
(search every instance of a repeated phrase; re-body, don't delete; don't cluster three on a
page) and her *threshold* (clustering, not raw count). Run it as a Phase-3 `/tic` pass, after
structure — which is also her advice. Reject everything that would flatten the repetition you
use on purpose.

## Part 5 — Fix-traps (mistakes actually made while doing this pass)

**TRAP 1 — fixing an opener-pile-up by creating a triad.** The most common self-inflicted
wound. When you collapse three clipped parallel sentences (*"She told him X. She told him Y.
She told him Z."*) into one comma list, you can turn them into a polished **X, Y, and Z** —
which is the rule-of-three tell VOICE.md says to hunt. You traded one tell for another.

- ❌ *…telling him about Charlotte and the books, the snow, and Alex's questions.* (matched
  three-beat list — a triad)
- ✅ *…telling him Charlotte had taken over the books and Alex was starting to ask questions.*
  (**two** beats — uneven/additive, his actual parallelism)

**Rule:** your real parallelism is **two blunt beats, or four** — never the smooth matched
three. When a fix leaves you with exactly three parallel items, cut to two (drop the weakest)
or build to four. The original anaphora often gets away with three *positives* only because it
lands on a **fourth withheld beat** (*"She did not tell him to come home"*) — that's
concrete-reversal, not a triad. If your rewrite drops that fourth turn, the surviving three
become a naked triad. **After every opener fix, recount the parallel beats.**

**TRAP 2 — protecting "signature" repetition the author's ear rejects.** Don't hide behind
"it's an anaphora, keep it." If the author says a passage still metronomes, that overrides the
keep-default. The anaphora's power is the *withheld turn*, not the repeated opener — you can
fold the repetition into comma-piled accumulation (his) and keep the turn as a hard short burst.

**TRAP 3 — a stack of one-word fragments is *also* a triad, and *also* staccato.** `Dry.
Clean. No mold.` reads like "signature burstiness" but it's three matched single-word
fragments — both a rule-of-three (TRAP 1) and choppy staccato. **Two** clipped fragments next
to a long sentence is the voice; **three matched** clipped fragments is the tic. When in doubt,
dissolve them into the flowing action that produced them (*"pressing a few between her thumb and
forefinger to find them dry and free of mold"*) rather than leaving them stacked.

**TRAP 4 — removing the tic but never varying an opener.** This is the one that makes a "fix"
feel surgical instead of better. Cutting "She / She / She" down to one "She" is *subtraction*;
it doesn't make the prose move. **A fix isn't done when the tic is gone — it's done when an
opener actually varies.** Use the one Bookfox opener that's on-voice (the prepositional /
time-place phrase: *"In her palm she counted the coins"*, *"On the walk back she passed
St. Stephen's"*) and drop it precisely on the worst SVO beat. Do **not** reach for the
dependent-clause opener (*"Before she paid, she…"*) to do this — that's the off-voice one
(Part 4, point 1). After every fix, check: did at least one sentence now *open* differently, or
did I only delete?
