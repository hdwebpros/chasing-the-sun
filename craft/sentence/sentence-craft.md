# Sentence Craft — Levels of Mastery

**Why it matters.** Anyone can write a grammatical sentence; there are *levels* above that (Fox's analogy: knowing how to shoot a basketball vs. shooting it like Steph Curry). This is a six-step build for one sentence at a time — variety against its neighbor, verb selection, a power word, an internal story arc, a figure of speech, and a final cut. It pairs with [[sentence/variety]] (the monotony diagnosis) and [[sentence/power]] (the scored rubric); this file is the *constructive* counterpart — how to make one sentence land, not just how to detect a flat one.

> **House gate.** Several steps below are generic "make it beautiful" advice (power word every sentence, always reach for a fresh verb, always include a figure of speech). These are **permission, not quota** — three governors bind them:
>
> - **The author's ear is the gate.** The hand-authored VOICE.md was retired (it canonized AI patterns); the craft research here is the ruler and the author is the veto, with RYAN-HOUSE-STYLE.md for triad-fix mechanics. Where the house voice is deliberately plain, restrained, or period-flat, *do not* gild a sentence just because a technique exists. Fox himself repeatedly caps his own advice ("not every sentence needs this") — honor that.
> - **Spend the budget at the peaks.** A cherry-picked verb, a power word, a figure of speech are *expensive* — they draw the eye. Spend them at the emotional and structural high points; keep the valleys (errand prose, stage business, transitions) deliberately plain. **Over-elevation is its own AI tell** — a "sunflowers weeping" dropped into a throwaway line reads as manufactured exactly the way a flat line does. The plainness of the valleys is what lets the peaks land.
> - **No single shape, and no shortcut.** Do *all* of the craft on *every* sentence, each weighed against its neighbors — never reverse-engineer one surface feature (short, long, fronted clause, fragment) and force it across the book. There is **no winning shape**: *"Ice broke on the rivers."* is elite SVO (*broke* is the cherry-picked verb and the power word at once), and a long accumulating line is elite for a different reason (its power word, its arc). Length and cadence are not the ruler; the craft, applied per-sentence-in-context, is. Trying to find the one pattern the author's ear approves and mass-applying it is the exact move he has flagged as counterproductive.

> **House diagnostics (from the Ch. 28 page pilot).** Failure-readings I keep getting wrong:
>
> - **Rewording an abstraction is not crafting it — convert the state-claim into a *seen image*. (Show, don't *re-tell*.)** The trap: a flat assertion (*"The business was strong"*) gets "fixed" by swapping synonyms (*"the season's books had closed"* / *"the spring work was already spoken for"*) — same dead skeleton, an abstract tell with no image and no cherry-picked verb, just a new coat. 
> - **A power verb *conveys action, emotion, or imagery with maximum impact and efficiency, and catches the reader off guard not predicatable yet still not nonsensical it has to be a word humans use naturally*, and its strength is *conferred by context* — never intrinsic.** So don't hunt an impressive verb in isolation — choose the one the rest of the sentence, and its neighbors, make inevitable, then let the surrounding words pay it off. This is why Step 1 (relative to its neighbors) is not optional polish but *crucial*: a verb's power and a sentence's meaning both live in the context, not the chunk.
> - **"5th grade / sophomoric" means *no craft*, not *simple words*.** When the author calls a line 5th-grade, he means primer-cadence — flat verbs, no power word, mechanical declaratives or sentimental filler fragments. It is **not** a complaint about plain diction; plain is often right. The diagnosis is the *absence of craft*.
> - **Fix the verb, don't amputate the beat.** 
> - **You can break down a sentence to multiple if it starts to get overengineered**
> **House diagnostics (from the prologue first-page pilot, June 2026).** 
> - **The verb is the whole game; a default verb is an automatic fail.** This is the single most common miss. *open, push, take, move, hang, go, get* arrive first precisely because they are the *predicted* word for the slot — they are never the answer on their own. The author's verdict on a batch of my fixes: "the biggest issues overall are your verb selections." Spend the *most* reasoning here. Survey real candidates and choose the one that does work the flat verb cannot (sensory, conferred by context).
> - **If no real person would say it and no real writer would write it, it is not a fix — it is manufactured texture.** Test every offering against living speech and real prose: would a person actually say this, would a good writer actually write it? If not, cut it — do not embellish it. (Cross: [[voice/ai-fingerprints]] A2.)
> - **A fix may not contradict the story it is fixing.** Keep load-bearing world- and scene-building and never invert established blocking. A fix that breaks continuity or staging has failed before its prose is judged. (Cross: [[voice/ai-fingerprints]] A7.)
> - **"Better word" ≠ fancy word — but it is *never* the 2nd-grade default either.** The cherry-picked verb and the power word are *ordinary words doing surprising work* (*broke, weeping, slithered, bitter, naked*) — not $10 thesaurus picks, and not *open / take / move* either. The failure I keep making is reaching *down* to the flattest word and calling it restraint. Restraint means a plain valley beat not a peak left uncrafted with a primer verb.
> - **Serve the paragraph's focal point; do not compete with it.** CRITICAL Before fixing any sentence, trace the paragraph's *causal chain* and find the pivot — the beat that triggers the next action. **(a)** don't charge a runway detail — it robs the peak of its payoff; **(b)** never write a setup that **contradicts** the payoff. Find the peak by **logic** (what does this detail *cause*?), then aim every runway sentence at it.

## The fix gate — show the work, or you haven't done it

**The bar is *splendid*, not clean.** The whole point of this craft is *writing splendid sentences* — making a line dazzle, not making it error-free. A fix that only removes a flaw (a tense slip, a verb echo, a clunk) and leaves an *ordinary* sentence behind has **failed**: subtraction is table stakes, not the goal. Every item in the gate below serves one question — *is this sentence now splendid?* If the honest answer is "no, but it's correct now," you are not finished.

**The failure this prevents (the one that keeps recurring): the self-certifying autopilot fix.** You produce a plain Subject–Verb–Object sentence with the first verb that comes to mind, *name* the six steps, and present it as crafted — without having done them. That is the AI default-verb tell turned on your own editing: stopping at the first locally-plausible output and self-certifying. **Naming a step is not doing it.**

**"Plain" is not the escape hatch. Plain-crafted ≠ sophomoric.**

**The gate — do this in the open, for every sentence-level fix, before presenting it. Work it in order; a later step can send you back to redo an earlier one.**

0. **Classify the beat: peak or valley.** Decide *before* you fix whether this is an emotional/structural high point or an errand/transition beat. This classification governs steps 3 and 4 below, and you do **not** get to reclassify a peak as a valley afterward to excuse a flat fix. When in doubt, treat it as a peak.
1. **Opener class** of the fix *and* of its neighbor — confirm they differ; if not, does the writing genuinely call for two same-shape sentences in a row? Sometimes is fine, a habit is not.
2. **Survey whole sentences, not bare verbs.** Write the sentence at least three *genuinely different* ways and show the two you rejected — different verb, different structure, different entrance. Surveying verbs in isolation is invalid: a verb's power is conferred by context, so a word judged outside the sentence is autopilot with a clipboard. The winning verb may **not** be the predicted default for its slot (the word that arrives first; see the house default list) *unless* you justify it as the rare elite-plain case where the plain verb *is* the cherry-pick. If the verb is the first one you thought of, you skipped this step.
3. **The power word** — name it and the friction it makes (the unexpected pairing). **"None" or "light" is forbidden on a peak**: a peak with no power word is an uncrafted peak, the exact failure this doc exists to prevent. "None" is allowed *only* on a true valley beat whose **neighbor** carries the craft — and you must say which neighbor does.
4. **Figure of speech — NOT metaphor.** Scan the *whole* catalogue (anaphora / epistrophe / antithesis / repetition / metonymy / synecdoche / litotes / hyperbole) and name which one the beat earns. Reaching for the same one figure every time is itself autopilot — a beat that keeps landing on the same device across many fixes means you stopped scanning. State "valley beat, earns no figure" only after the scan, and only on a genuine valley. "Skip — metaphor is an AI sniff" is an **invalid** justification: metaphor/simile are a separate gated tool (§5b), not this step.
5. **Say it aloud, verbatim.** Quote the finished fix in full and state plainly whether a real person would say it and a real writer would write it, and why — not a checkbox, a written judgment. If any image or phrasing is something no human would actually say, it is manufactured texture: cut it, do not present it.
6. **No minimal-diff conservatism.** The fix may keep *nothing* of the original. Anchoring on the author's words and swapping one or two of them is itself a failure mode — the question is the best sentence for the beat, not the smallest change. Take the freedom; there is no winning shape to preserve.

If any line is blank, the fix is not finished. A fix with no nameable power word and no whole-sentence survey *is* the autopilot output this entire doc exists to prevent — do not present it.

## Before the six steps — paragraph architecture (do this first)

The six steps optimize *one sentence*. But a sentence is never optimized alone: it has a **role inside its paragraph**, and the paragraph has a **job relative to the paragraphs around it**. Map both *before* touching any sentence. Skipping this is what produces a fix that reads fine in isolation yet breaks the scene — and it is the direct cause of the reflexive "goofy fragment."

**1. Paragraphs serve each other.** Name what each paragraph in the scene is *for* and how it hands off to the next. A paragraph is a move in a sequence — establish → develop → turn → land; setup → payoff; tension → release. A change that sharpens one paragraph but severs its handoff to the next is not an improvement.

**2. Sentences have roles inside the paragraph.** Within a paragraph each sentence plays a structural part — *topic / establishing*, *developing* (the concrete images, the evidence), *the turn*, *the landing*. Judge a sentence first by whether it does its **role**, not only by whether it's beautiful. A fix that makes the topic sentence gorgeous but leaves the paragraph with no landing has failed the paragraph.

**3. This is why the goofy fragment keeps happening — and how to stop it.** A sentence fragment (and the bare choppy declarative) is a *tool for one role*: a hard landing, or a turn. It earns its place only when the paragraph's arc demands a hard stop right there. Dropped as a reflex — because "punchy = good" — it serves no role and reads as a tic (*"A grief with no funeral to mark it."* into a meditation that wanted to keep flowing). The cure is **not** "write better fragments." It is: only reach for the fragment when the paragraph's job calls for the stop.

> **Method — the *task* demands the reasoning, not this doc.** Writing a splendid sentence is *itself* a high-reasoning act. It is not that a rule here orders you to read context; it is that the answer genuinely *lives* in the context — the sentence, its neighbors, the paragraph's job, the surrounding paragraphs, the scene's purpose, the established blocking, and the book's arc each really do bear on a single word choice, so any honest attempt has to reason across all of them. Read them because that is where the right word is, not to satisfy a mandate. Distrust the first reflexive move: it is almost always the *predicted* word, not the *chosen* one. The thoroughness is not box-ticking — it is the irreducible difficulty of the work. (Author directive, June 2026: the factors are real and many, so the demand for hard reasoning comes from what writing the sentence actually takes — not from a doc forcing context for its own sake.)

## The six steps

### 1. Look at the sentence before it (variety)
**Rule.** A sentence never exists in isolation. Before you write the next one, decide whether to **pair** it with the previous (echo it) or **contrast** it (make it as different as possible). Sentences are music: you have to know the previous note to choose the next.

**Why it works.** A run of same-shape, same-length sentences flattens into a drone the ear stops hearing — "the writing is getting boring, the sound of it drones, it's like a stuck record." The ear *demands* change; supplying it is what keeps the reader hearing the prose. (This is the constructive flip side of the monotony failure in [[sentence/variety]].)

**Example (the failure, verbatim — Gary Provost):**
> "This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring. The sound of it drones. It's like a stuck record. The ear demands some variety."

That passage is *demanding* a long sentence next — to break the rat-a-tat and restore musical flow.

**Three levers for variety:**
- **Length** — alternate short / medium / long.
- **Structure** — don't open every sentence SVO (subject-verb-object, "he did this"). Two openers that break SVO:
  - *Little word + comma.* Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*: **"Today, despite Rita's closed face and pressed lips, I would like to stay here in the kitchen."** The little "Today," up front contrasts with surrounding structures.
  - *Lead with a dependent clause.* Cormac McCarthy, *The Road*: **"When he got back the boy was still asleep."** ("When he got back" is the unfinished clause that delays the subject.)
- **Rhythm** — e.g. a three-part sentence. Karen Russell, *Swamplandia!*: **"We'd stare at the sun, grip the grass, hold on to each other in the dark."** Because it flows so distinctly, the sentence *before* it shouldn't also be three-part, and neither should the one after — the special rhythm only reads as special against plainer neighbors.

**Failure mode it prevents.** Wall-to-wall SVO of uniform length — the metronome that reads as either amateur or AI.

> House note: no opener style is off-voice. (The old VOICE.md "ban" on dependent-clause / participial openers was retired by author direction — it was an unsourced constraint, not a preference.) "Vary your structure" is permission — reach for whichever opener is the best writing for the sentence.

### 2. Cherry-pick the verb
**Rule.** Don't grab the verb instinctually (the way an AI predicts the word that "normally goes" in the slot). *Cherry-pick* — survey several candidate verbs and choose deliberately.

**Why it works.** Verbs are "the little despots of the sentence" — the authoritarian rulers that control everything around them. A few extra seconds on verb choice pays outsize dividends because the verb governs the energy, connotation, and even implied imagery of the whole line.

**Examples (his before/after pairs):**
- Toni Morrison — **"heavy sunflowers *weeping* over fences."** The flat choice would be *bending* ("heavy sunflowers bending their way over fences"). *Weeping* smuggles in a contradiction (sunflowers read as happy, yet weeping = sad) and *implies* the bending without saying it. The verb does extra work.
- Bram Stoker, *Dracula* — **"the fog *slithered* in from the sea."** The flat choice: *drifted* or *crept*. *Slithered* adds foreboding and a snake-like connotation that makes the scene creepier.
- Stephen King, *Pet Sematary* — **"memories *scuttled* like roaches when the light snapped on."** The flat choice: *scattered*. *Scuttled* gives the line a peculiar, skittering flavor.

**Two verb mistakes:**
1. **Too fancy.** Don't reach for $10 dictionary words — say *give* not *bequeath*, *pay* not *remunerate*, *happen* not *transpire*. The point: *weeping / slithered / scuttled are ordinary words.* What elevates them is the **unusual context**, not rarity. "Take a normal word and put it in an unusual context" — that's the move. (We don't normally picture fog slithering or sunflowers weeping.)
2. **Plain-Jane verbs.** The first, common, low-energy verb you think of — especially passive constructions. Reach for strong, active verbs that light the sentence up.

**Failure mode it prevents.** Autopilot verbs (the predicted/default choice) on one side; thesaurus-show-off verbs on the other.

### 3. Pick a power word
**Rule.** Aim for **one power word per sentence** — a single word that lifts the line from a conveyor belt of information into something unusual and surprising.

**Why it works.** A power word usually creates productive *friction* — it pairs with its neighbors in a way you don't expect, so the sentence stops being mere information and makes the reader feel a contradiction or a fresh angle. (Attributed to **Janet Fitch** — *White Oleander* — as a tip Fox got studying with her at USC.)

**Examples (his identifications):**
- James Salter — "she had a rich **naked** laugh." Power word = *naked*: laughs aren't naked, but it captures an unguarded, unselfconscious laugh — she doesn't care who hears.
- Marilynne Robinson, *Gilead* — "I am grateful for all those dark years … they seem like a long **bitter** prayer." Power word = *bitter*: *bitter* and *prayer* fight each other; prayer isn't normally bitter, so the pairing surprises.
- Flann O'Brien — "the road ran away westwards … running **cunningly** through the little hills." Power word = *cunningly*: it sound-mirrors "running" (the *unning*), and it personifies the road into something taking deliberate, twisting action.

**Caveats (his own caps):** Don't thesaurus-hunt for "crazy words" — it's best when it happens organically. Don't reach for complex/esoteric words — *naked, bitter, cunningly* are all plain. (Mirrors the Step-2 "ordinary word, unusual context" principle.)

**Failure mode it prevents.** The flat informational sentence where every word is the expected one — nothing surprises, nothing elevates.

### 4. Create a story arc
**Rule.** Treat the sentence as a mini-story: it should *transport* — you begin in one place and end somewhere different. The opening sets an expectation the ending **inverts, subverts, or fulfills**.

**Why it works.** A sentence that changes state by its end "pulls its weight" — it captivates because something *happens* inside it, not just across the paragraph. The reader takes a journey in the span of a single sentence.

**Examples:**
- Michel Houellebecq, *The Elementary Particles* — **"He was still a handsome man with a tanned chiseled face and long thick wavy white hair, but his cells had begun to reproduce in a haphazard fashion, destroying the DNA of neighboring cells and secreting toxins into his body."** First half = an everyday handsome man, nothing remarkable. Second half = a man dying of cancer — and rendered clinically (cells reproducing, DNA wrecked), not announced ("by the way, he has cancer"). Start: ordinary Joe. End: dying man.
- Yaa Gyasi, *Transcendent Kingdom* — **"When I first started studying the neural circuits of reward-seeking behavior in mice I didn't believe they could feel fear, but I would come to understand that they could feel everything — fear, relief, joy, desire, disgust, all of it running like current through their tiny mouse bodies just the same as it runs through mine."** Start: a scientist with a clear gap between herself and the creature. End (that surprising last phrase): how much they share.

**When to apply.** *Not every sentence.* Don't feel pressure to force an arc on each one — it's a way to check that a given sentence is captivating, not a quota.

### 5. Choose a figure of speech — and "figure" here almost never means metaphor
**Rule.** Many great sentences carry a figure of speech. **Default move at this step: scan the catalogue below and name which figure the beat earns — do not equate "figure of speech" with metaphor.** The catalogue *is* the work here; metaphor and simile are a separate, rare, gated exception (§5b), never the reason to skip this step.

**Why it works.** Figures of speech compress meaning and create music/rhythm; several below work specifically by *patterned repetition*, which the ear hears as deliberate craft rather than accident.

The catalogue he teaches, with his examples:
- **Metonymy** — substitute one word for an associated word. Dumas, *The Three Musketeers*: "he shall think differently … when he feels the point of my **steel**" (*steel* for *sword*).
- **Synecdoche** — substitute a part for the whole. Steinbeck, *Of Mice and Men*: "you know how the **hands** are, they just come in and get their bunk and work a month" (*hands* for the whole farm worker).
- **Anaphora** — repeat a word at the *beginning* of successive phrases. Karen Russell, *Swamplandia!*: "**how to** hold your breath and rise from the lake like a goddess, **how to** hold very still in the cage, **how to** smile in such a way that your smile tells people they are lucky to be watching you die." (Same sentence opens with a *near*-anaphora on numbers: "2,000 people, 5 days a week, four times a day" — close but not identical words.)
- **Epistrophe** — the opposite: repeat a word at the *end* of successive phrases. (*The Wasp Factory* / Iain Banks ❓ — attribution uncertain in the garbled transcript): "they were a family of silence … nothing but blind, black, cold, crumbling **silence** … his father never anchored or steady like his mother, his mother never sanguine or loose like his father." Repeated end-word *silence* makes the rhythm.
- **Antithesis** — mirrored, flipped halves. McCarthy, *The Road*: "you forget what you want to remember and you remember what you want to forget." Neat parallelism, halves are mirror images.
- **Hyperbole** — exaggeration for tone. García Márquez: "Bogotá was a remote, lugubrious city where an **insomniac rain had been falling since the beginning of the sixteenth century.**" Obviously not literal; it sets a four-centuries-of-rain mood.
- **Litotes** — understatement (the opposite of hyperbole). *Beowulf*: "the sword **wasn't useless** to the warrior" — said right after it killed Grendel; underplaying it lands harder than overstating.
- **Repetition** — distinct from *redundancy*. High-school English bans repeating words, but repetition (like music) can be beautiful; redundancy is the sin, not repetition. Ann Patchett, *The Patron Saint of Liars*: "I said **yes** to Thomas Clinton and later thought I had said **yes** to God and later still realized I had said **yes** only to Thomas Clinton." (Also runs a Step-4 arc: yes-to-a-boy → yes-to-God's-command → back to yes-to-a-boy.)

**When to apply.** "Not every sentence needs a figure of speech." Anaphora and epistrophe are the two he says to use "actually quite frequently"; the rest are tools to reach for, not boxes to tick.

**Invalid skip.** "No figure — metaphor is an AI sniff" does **not** justify skipping this step: that reasoning governs only metaphor/simile (§5b). Skip only after scanning the catalogue and finding a plain valley beat that earns none — and say so in those words.

### 5b. Metaphor & simile — the rare, gated exception

> **House flag: metaphor/simile is a crutch, and density is an AI sniff.** Author directive (June 2026): *this novel needs far fewer of them — a metaphor is a **pure AI sniff** when it's the reflex you reach for to "lift" a line.* Figurative comparison is the move an AI defaults to whenever prose should feel literary, so a high metaphor/simile density reads as machine-made exactly the way a flat line does. So in Step 5, treat metaphor and simile as the **rarest** spend, not the go-to: most "make it splendid" work should come from a cherry-picked verb (Step 2), one power word (Step 3), and a *literal, charged* detail — not from a comparison. **This does not ban them** (the author was explicit: "that doesn't mean stop them entirely") — one fresh, earned figure at a true peak is gold. It bans using a metaphor as the *crutch* a line leans on. Test: if the only "craft" in your fix is a metaphor, redo it with a literal verb and a power word first; usually that is the stronger sentence and the figure was a tell.

### 6. Cut the fat
**Rule.** Trim so that what remains is highlighted. (Fox demonstrates on his own working title: "cut *away* the fat" → "cut the fat" — he killed *away* to follow his own advice.)

**Why it works.** The engines of a sentence are **nouns and verbs**; everything piled around them can clutter rather than illuminate. Cutting concentrates the reader's attention on the parts that carry meaning.

**Four cuts:**
1. **Adjectives and adverbs** — keen eye here; too many pile up and clutter. Concentrate on nouns and verbs (the engines that power the sentence).
2. **Prepositional phrases**, especially 3–4 in a row. Failure example: **"in my bed, under the covers, with my pillow was a sword"** — *in / under / with* back-to-back is usually too much (unless you're deliberately going for a prepositional pile-up effect).
3. **Unnecessary words.** "they **collaborated together** on the project" — can you collaborate *alone*? Cut *together*: "they collaborated on the project." Simpler, more direct.
4. **Don't fear short sentences.** Short sentences aren't to be feared. If a whole book has no three-word sentence in it, "there's a problem with your book" — you should have at least one. (Connects to Step-1 length variety and to [[sentence/power]]'s short-sentence punch.)

**Failure mode it prevents.** Padding — modifier stacks, preposition chains, filler doublets ("collaborated together") — and the timidity that never lets a sentence be three words long.

> House note: **cutting is not listifying.** When a run of flat sentences tempts you to "cut" by merging them into one comma list, stop — re-punctuation is not a fix; it relocates the monotony (the verbs stay dead, nothing gets embodied) and the tidy serial list is itself an AI sniff. The cure is Steps 2–5 (cherry-pick the verb, embody the item, vary the entrance), not a list. See [[sentence/variety]] Rule G.

> House note: **a tail is not an upgrade — adding fat is not crafting.** "Fixing" a flat sentence by bolting an explanatory subordinate clause onto the end (*"He has read every room … since he was a boy in a house that was not his own"*) does not craft it: it *adds fat* (this step's whole point is to cut) and usually installs an abstract-explainer tail (the [[voice/ai-fingerprints]] A3 fingerprint). If your offering is *longer* than the original and the new length is a clause that *explains*, you wrote a new fingerprint instead of a fix. Improve the words already on the page — above all the verb — before you reach for more words.

## The crucial negative — dead metaphors

**Rule.** The author flags this as the single most important item ("Avoid dead metaphors — **crucial**"). A dead (or *dying*) metaphor is a figure so worn that no image fires any more — *the wind before the storm, weather the storm, a sea of faces, the heart of the matter, time ran out.* It is the inverse of Steps 2 and 3: where a cherry-picked verb or a power word puts an *unexpected* word in the slot, a dead metaphor is the *most* expected figure — the phrase that arrives on autopilot. That is exactly why AI-tinged prose is saturated with them.

**Why it works.** Orwell's rule (*Politics and the English Language*) is the canonical source: a dying metaphor has "lost all evocative power and [is] merely used because [it] saves people the trouble of inventing phrases for themselves." Two cures, in order:
1. **Cut it** when the beat needs no figure at all — the plain literal statement is often stronger than a stale image.
2. **Rework it** into a fresh, concrete, *local* image when the beat earns elevation — re-body it in the world of the scene (the period, the place, the character's trade) rather than reaching for the inherited phrase.

**Where this lives in the project.** The metaphor census (`scratchpad/metaphor-census-v2.md`) already tiers every figure `live` vs `conventional`; the *conventional* tier is largely this list. Dead-metaphor work is therefore a **targeted pass over the conventional tier** — cut or re-body — not a hunt across all prose. Pairs with [[voice/ai-fingerprints]] (dead figures are a top fingerprint) and with the burst concern: don't replace a dead metaphor with a fresh one *inside an already figure-heavy paragraph*.

> **Non-metaphor (use sparingly).** The deliberate *anti*-figure — a comparison that refuses to compare — is a rare tonal tool, not a staple. Douglas Adams: *"The ships hung in the sky in much the same way that bricks don't."* It works once, for comedy or absurdity; reach for it almost never, and never in earnest emotional prose.
