# Pro-Level Tips from Eight Authors

**Why it matters.** These eight techniques are the lever between *competent* prose (correct, robotic, dutiful) and *pro-level* prose. They are aimed at seasoned writers, not beginners — each is a calibrated dial, not an on/off rule. Most apply ~80% of the time, with named exceptions; the craft is knowing *when* to break them. Several attack the same failure mode this KB tracks elsewhere: prose that reads mechanical/AI because it is flat, over-detailed, over-decorated, or held at arm's length from the thing itself.

> **VOICE.md gate.** Three of these tips (burnt tongue, insistence, defamiliarization) actively invite warping, repeating, or estranging language. They are *permission*, not license. The manuscript's VOICE.md already constrains certain shapes; where it does, VOICE.md overrides "you can do anything." Do not invent VOICE.md rules to justify or block a tip — check the actual voiceprint.

## The eight tips

### 1. Burnt tongue — deliberately warp the sentence (Gordon Lish)
**Rule.** Write in "burnt tongue": deliberately warp your syntax so the prose sounds raw, strange, off, wrong, or weird. Screw up the sentence on purpose.
**Why it works.** Warped syntax *enacts* a character's emotional or psychological state — how a mind actually runs under stress, strong emotion, or when the speaker is from elsewhere or doesn't know English well. The wrongness mirrors the inner state instead of describing it. (Lish's own framing, per the transcript: "God only listens to those whose tongues are burnt." Warping also slows the reader, forcing them to read close rather than skim a surface of clichés.)
**How (three moves).** (a) Reorder the words / invert normal syntax. (b) Drop the articles ("the," "a"). (c) Write in fragments; break sentences off halfway.
**Example / exercise.** Write a paragraph where a character receives bad news. Draft it once in completely normal syntax. Then re-draft it with burnt tongue — fragments, dropped articles, sentences that stop halfway — and see whether the broken version better carries the emotional state.
**Failure mode / when.** This is a revision and emotional-register tool, not a default voice. Warp where the inner state earns it; constant warping just reads as noise, and on the page can read as the *opposite* of voice. (House note: Lish-style article-dropping can collide with VOICE.md and with brogue — coordinate, don't stack.)

### 2. Avoid "weak blonde prose" — the trap of the child voice (video creator's phrase; NOT verified as Nabokov's — see Provenance)
**Rule.** Don't constrain yourself to the vocabulary and thoughts of a child narrator unless you go in with full, eyes-open knowledge of what you give up. A six-year-old can't reach for sophisticated metaphor or analyze the world.
**Why it works.** A writer whose strength is ornate, perceptive prose (Nabokov's case) throws that strength away by limiting himself to how a seven- or eight-year-old talks. Choose the limitation deliberately or not at all.
**Counterpoint (Fox's addition, not Nabokov's).** Limitation can *create* creativity rather than squashing it, when the constraint is the point:
- *Room* (Emma Donoghue) — five-year-old Jack narrates; his childlike wonder makes an unbearable story (a mother trapped in a room) endurable. The mother narrating would be too much.
- *Flowers for Algernon* (Daniel Keyes) — Charlie starts unable to spell, gets brilliant, declines again; the pleasure *is* a narrator who can't narrate well, and the visible transformation.
- *The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time* (Mark Haddon) — Christopher's autistic way of seeing the world is the charm, not a deficit.
**Example / exercise.** Vocabulary-restriction test: write a full page using only words a five-year-old could use. If it comes easily, the constrained voice may genuinely be yours — go for it. If it's very hard, that voice is probably not right for you.
**Failure mode / when.** Picking a limited narrator by accident, then discovering mid-book all the metaphor, analysis, and range you've locked yourself out of.

### 3. Avoid abject naturalism — don't drown in everyday minutiae (Frank Conroy)
**Rule.** "Abject naturalism" is the listing of the minutiae of ordinary life — washing dishes, setting a table, weeding a garden. Don't get caught in the weeds of those details.
**Why it works.** Slow too far — a whole paragraph on suds and water-drips in the sink — and the reader gets bored fast. The detail is accurate but inert; it doesn't charge the scene with meaning.
**How (three options for the boring stretch).** (a) Skip it entirely — you don't owe the reader the dishwashing. (b) Demote it to a dependent clause: "After washing the dishes, he ___" then cut to the real action. (c) Summarize: "He washed the dishes," done, move on.
**Example.** Even *exciting* material can go abject: Peter F. Hamilton's *Pandora's Star* spends ~30 pages on a woman paragliding over a volcano — thrilling in principle, overwhelming in detail; a page or two would have done it.
**Exception.** High-literary writers make the abundance the *style* — Proust (*In Search of Lost Time*), Knausgård (*My Struggle*) immerse you by piling on detail. They pull it off as classics; your mileage will vary, so tamp it down by default.
**Exercise.** Compression: take a whole paragraph where something happens and compress it to a single sentence. You won't always keep the compressed version, but you'll see whether the long version was earning its length.
**Failure mode / when.** Reality-for-reality's-sake — documentary accuracy mistaken for storytelling.

### 4. Insistence, not repetition (Gertrude Stein)
**Rule.** Reframe deliberate repetition as *insistence*. Repeating something insists on its reality and emphasizes it — it is not the redundancy your English teacher warned about. (And do **not** "fix" repetition by thesaurus-swapping synonyms — that's the actually-bad advice.)
**Why it works.** Insistence enacts an emotional pattern the reader feels building. Stein's principle: each repeat carries fresh emphasis, so it is never truly the same — variation is allowed.
**Example.** *American Dirt* (Jeanine Cummins): a woman fears the cartel has killed her journalist husband, gets close, realizes it isn't him — "It was not him. It was not him… It's not him. It's not him." The repetition *is* the relief pouring out; the paragraph is not redundant, it's the experience.
**How much / must it be identical.** Roughly **three to five** repeats: more exhausts the reader, fewer fails to establish a pattern. It need not be the exact same words — insistence allows variation.
**Exercise.** Have a character repeat a word or phrase until the reader starts to doubt they're telling the truth. *The Red Badge of Courage* (Stephen Crane): the narrator keeps calling the soldiers who stayed and fought "imbeciles," and you realize he's justifying his own cowardice by insulting them.
**Failure mode / when.** Past five repeats it tips into exhaustion; under three it's just an accident, not a pattern. (House note: this is the *legitimate* sibling of the stylistic-tic problem — see [[device/recurring-device]] and the /tic pass. Insistence is patterned and earned; a tic is unconscious and metronomic.)

### 5. Defamiliarization — make the familiar strange (Viktor Shklovsky)
**Rule.** Take what's familiar to the reader and present it so it seems strange — make them see it as if for the first time.
**Why it works.** When we're familiar with something, we stop truly seeing it (Anaïs Nin). Defamiliarization wakes the reader up inside a world they thought they knew: be amazed, be surprised. (Like saying a word so many times — "plan, plan, plan" — that it detaches from its meaning.)
**Example.** Vonnegut calls New York City the "National Skyscraper Park" — that reframe makes you see the whole city fresh.
**The inverse move.** You can also make the *very strange* feel familiar — central to sci-fi/fantasy, where the author's job is to render a wild world understandable. *Dune*: an arid world, people harvesting spice and reclaiming their own internal liquids to survive the dryness — made familiar through storytelling and detail.
**Exercise.** Make the reader see a common object — a light switch — as if for the first time; make them wonder at it, find it a little miraculous.
**Failure mode / when.** Estrangement for its own sake reads as preciousness; the goal is renewed perception, not a riddle.

### 6. Kill filter words (John Gardner)
**Rule.** Cut the needless filtering of images through a character's perceiving consciousness — "he saw," "she heard," "she felt," "he noticed." Go straight to the object, the smell, the sound. (Gardner, *The Art of Fiction*, answering what common pitfalls writers face.)
**Why it works.** Filter words insert a veil between the reader and the actual thing, creating distance. Removed, the image hits unmediated.
**Example (filters added back, to hear the damage).**
- Fitzgerald, *The Great Gatsby*: "*I realized* we beat on, boats against the current…" — no; start at "So we beat on."
- Hemingway, *A Farewell to Arms*: "*He noticed* how the world breaks everyone…" — no; start at "The world breaks everyone."
- Austen, *Pride and Prejudice*: "*She believed* it is a truth universally acknowledged…" — no; start at "It is a truth universally acknowledged."
**Exceptions (the ~20%).** (a) **Unreliable / limited narration** — "I thought I heard footsteps" keeps us unsure whether it's real or imagined. (b) **Establishing POV** — "Sarah noticed the door was ajar" signals we're in Sarah's head, not an omniscient view.
**Failure mode / when.** Blanket-stripping every filter flattens unreliable narrators and muddies whose POV we're in. Apply ~80% of the time; keep the filter when the *act of perceiving* is itself the point.

### 7. Furnish the world on a budget — the salient detail (video says "Robin Wilson"; attribution unresolved — the principle is canonically John Gardner's, see Provenance)
**Rule.** You can't describe everything in a room. Pick this thing, this thing, and this thing — a few details that stand in for the whole.
**Why it works.** We're not making movies. A film captures a whole room in a second; do that in prose and you spend two or three pages and the reader throws the book across the room. The constraint forces selection, and selection is characterization.
**How (two techniques).** (a) **General impression** — name the *feeling* of the space: "it smelled like ___," or "the kind of room where you felt you might be tortured later." (b) **Single salient detail** — the one telling object: a bong on the dresser in a boy's bedroom characterizes the room (and the boy) instantly.
**Example.** Different boy's bedroom, one detail: a bong on the dresser. You decide whether it's the same or a different characterization — that's the point; the chosen detail does the work.
**Failure mode / when.** Inventory-style description — cataloguing every object — buries the salient detail and stalls the scene. (See [[setting/setting]].)

### 8. Kill purple prose (origin: Horace)
**Rule.** Cut the flowery, ornamental prose that doesn't serve the story. Not a modern term: Horace (*Ars Poetica*, ~1st c. BC) compared an overly ornate patch to sewing a big purple patch onto plain clothing — it stands out, and not in a good way.
**Why it works.** The problem isn't beautiful language or elegance — it's *self-indulgence*. Beautiful writing is fine when it serves the story and the reader; purple prose serves the writer's vanity, and it breaks the work's stylistic consistency.
**Example (deliberately hideous, then plainer, then the middle).**
- "The scintillating crystalline tears of ethereal sorrow cascaded melodiously down her alabaster cheeks like liquid diamonds." → "Tears rolled down her face." → or something poetic-but-not-straining in between.
- "The cerulean firmament was bejeweled with coruscating stellular orbs." → "The sky was filled with bright stars." → or a lightly poetic middle.
  (It's a gradient, not an either/or.)
**The three-question gut-check before you keep a fancy passage.**
1. If I deleted this whole thing — or trimmed the paragraph to one sentence — do I lose anything? If not, it's probably purple.
2. Does it match this character's voice and the story's tone? If it's consistent, it may simply *be* the voice, and it's right.
3. Am I trying to impress or to communicate? Impressing is about you; communicating is about the reader.
**Failure mode / when.** Question 2 is the saving clause — an ornate line that genuinely matches an ornate narrator is not purple. Strip on autopilot and you can flatten a legitimately lush voice. (House note: run question 2 against VOICE.md, not against a generic "plain is always better" reflex.)
