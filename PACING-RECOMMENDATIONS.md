# Chasing the Sun — Pacing Assessment

*Fresh read of the current Drive Doc (81,227 words: Prologue + 38 chapters + 5 interludes + Epilogue). This is a craft-based pacing pass — not a response to any score. (An outside tool's "74" is a generated number, not a real rubric; ignore it.) Every craft principle below is grounded in Alyssa Matesic's and John Fox's (Bookfox) actual published guidance, quoted and cited — not invented. Every claim about the book is anchored to a line in the current Doc. No edits have been made; this is diagnosis.*

---

## Craft basis (Matesic & Bookfox)

The whole assessment rests on one craft distinction — **scene vs. summary** — and on how each editor says to use it. Their words:

**John Fox / Bookfox**, on what summary is *for*:
- "What summary does is allow you to cover a larger portion of a person's life, and not get stuck in the dull parts of their life."
- "You can cover generations, rather than just a year in a person's life."
- "The pacing is much, much quicker [in summary]. Here in the space of a single paragraph we have the bombshell… and also his actual departure."
- On keeping summary alive: most writers "abandon specifics" when they start summarizing, but good summary keeps "concrete details, very authentic and memorable" — "simply use three sentences to cover an entire month."
- (Source: [Bookfox, "4 Ways to Write Summary in your Fiction (and avoid Scenes)"](https://thejohnfox.com/2022/05/how-to-write-summary-in-your-fiction-and-avoid-scenes/))

**Alyssa Matesic**, on where pace dies and what to do:
- **Identify slow scenes:** remove scenes not meaningfully contributing to plot — "if nothing of consequence occurs during their workday, you don't need to spend pages" on it. (She recommends *reverse outlining* to find them.)
- **Watch time jumps:** language like "the next two weeks passed by uneventfully" signals nothing is happening — compress it or fill the gap with subplot.
- **Slow down in consequential moments:** "slow down and show their emotional responses" during the dramatic beats.
- **Denouement:** give closure after the climax without "too long" a tail of characters' futures.
- **Governing principle:** "Every scene should contain only essential plot action; summarize or paraphrase non-critical details."
- On chapter endings: "every chapter ending gives the reader a choice whether to continue… or put it down"; "if the end of your chapter fizzles out… they're likely not going to continue"; but "I'm not talking here about ending every chapter on a dramatic cliffhanger… Often, subtler endings are even more effective" — emotional/inner-conflict endings count.
- On worldbuilding: the failure mode is "an info dump of nitty-gritty worldbuilding details we don't really need"; the test is "Does this directly complicate or contribute to the protagonist's journey?"; weave it "into scene action through what the characters see, hear, and do."
- (Sources: [Matesic, "How to Perfect Your Novel's Pacing in 5 Simple Steps"](https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/novel-pacing); [Matesic, "How to End a Chapter to Make Your Book Unputdownable"](https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/how-to-end-a-chapter); [Matesic, "7 Major Plot Mistakes To Avoid"](https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/7-plot-mistakes-to-avoid))

**Why this book is a special case of their rule.** This is a multi-decade, multi-generation saga — *exactly* what Bookfox says summary exists to handle ("cover generations… not get stuck in the dull parts"). So the deaths and births stay at scene-weight (Matesic: slow down in consequential moments), and the connective material between them should be **clean, specific summary** (Bookfox: three vivid sentences), not half-dramatized stretches. The diagnosis below is just that one rule applied chapter by chapter: scene where it's consequential, specific summary everywhere else, and nothing of consequence rendered twice.

## The test: load-bearing worldbuilding/character vs. true sag

"Doesn't move the plot" is **not** a reason to cut — worldbuilding and character development are *consequence*, and for historical fiction the world is load-bearing. Before flagging anything below, run each beat through three questions drawn from the sources:

- **Function (Matesic):** "Does this directly complicate or contribute to the protagonist's journey?" Her objection is specifically to "an info dump of nitty-gritty worldbuilding details we don't really need" — i.e. *front-loaded, standalone* exposition. Her remedy is to weave the world "into scene action through what the characters see, hear, and do." Woven through a character's agency → passes. A standalone descriptive block that pauses the story → fails.
- **Specificity (Bookfox):** vivid concrete detail earns its length even in telling ("very authentic and memorable"); blurred abstraction does not.
- **Payoff:** does it seed something that returns later? A beat that pays off downstream is doing a job now.

**If a beat passes any of these, the lever is line-level tightening *inside* the scene (Bookfox's sentence-level pacing), not deletion.** Only single-purpose, standalone, non-paying scene-setting is a candidate for the cut. This is the correction to an earlier version of this doc that mistook scene-dense character/world chapters for summary sag.

---

## On the "scene-to-summary ratio" — there is no magic number

Be wary of any pacing *number* (it's the same trap as the outside tool's "74"). **Neither Matesic nor Bookfox gives a target ratio**, and Bookfox argues the opposite of "more scene is better" — summary is *underused*, and some fine books "use summary for most of the book." So a high-summary ratio can be exactly right, especially for a generational saga.

The real best-practice metric is not a ratio but a **match**, found by **reverse outline** (Matesic's method):

1. List every unit of the book; tag each **scene** (dramatized) or **summary** (narrated).
2. Rate each unit's **consequence** — does it change the character or situation, or pay off later?
3. The only two errors are **length/consequence mismatches**: a *long dramatized scene on a low-consequence event* (over-rendered → compress to summary), and a *summary glossing a high-consequence event* (under-rendered → slow into scene).

That match — not a percentage — is the metric. The clearest example in this book is **Ch 37** (summary glossing the high-consequence thaw → dramatize it: the one beat that should be a scene is the one rendered in a time-jump). *Optional deliverable: a full reverse outline of all 38 chapters, each tagged scene/summary with a consequence rating, to map every place length and consequence diverge.*

## The one real pattern worth acting on

The book's set pieces — the deaths, the births, the deathbed X — are strong and, crucially, *varied*. George is slow and acoustic; William Jr. is fast and off-page; Joseph is a causal chain; Teresa goes out against the carollers; Mary Margaret is a count. That variation is doing exactly what it should.

Where momentum actually leaks is the **connective material between those scenes** — the settling sequences, the acquisition montages, the competence-inventories, the routine. This is a normal failure mode for a multi-decade family saga: the dramatized peaks are easy to write at full weight, and the valleys between them quietly fill up with *half-dramatized* material — too slow to be summary, too inconsequential to be scene. The fix is not to add tension to the valleys; it's to render the connective material *once* at scene-weight and let every reprise of it become clean, specific summary. (Matesic: cut scenes where "nothing of consequence occurs." Bookfox: that's what summary is *for* — "three sentences to cover an entire month," specifics intact.)

Three things follow from that: tighten the few genuine summary stretches (and the line-level fat inside long scenes), sharpen the four-stage land-buying arc so each stage reads as its own beat, and stop the one *truly* repeated image and the one stated theme that recur. Everything below is an instance of that.

## Where pace actually drags (current Doc, with evidence)

Two different problems live here. Ch 11 and Ch 20 are **long but scene-dense** (tighten the lines, keep the beats); Ch 34 is **short and already specific** (differentiate the arc stage, don't cut). Don't treat them the same — and note neither Ch 11 nor Ch 20 is the "summary pool" an earlier version of this doc claimed.

- **Chapter Eleven — Boston settling (lines 678–740, 2,675 words).** The longest early chapter and the first place a reader *could* feel the settling slow — but apply the test above before cutting. The beats are scene-dense and most earn their keep: the arrival, first mass at St. Stephen's, Mary's church-threshold prayer ("a good door to stand in," ~line 725), the market walk, and the wharf/apple errand (733–740). The **apple/wharf errand is character development with a downstream payoff** (the Letitia revisit) — it passes Matesic's function test; keep it. The **chapter's close** — "Her fingers smelled of apple for the rest of the day" (740) — is a quiet gestural ending in your voiceprint, and Matesic explicitly says "subtler endings are even more effective" than cliffhangers; **keep the apple close** (an earlier version of this doc wrongly suggested swapping it for the threshold beat — disregard that). The **market walk** stays if it's woven through Mary's agency (what she sees/does/decides); only any *standalone* descriptive line that pauses the story is a target. **The lever for Ch 11 is line-level tightening inside the kept scenes (Bookfox sentence-pacing), not deleting worldbuilding, character, or the close.** On a close read there is very little fluff; this is ~5–10% polish, optional, and if the chapter still feels long afterward the length is probably *correct* for a settling chapter — leave it. What "tighten the lines" actually means, with real examples:
  - **Cut a vague tell (let the image carry it):** "The smell of garlic drifted into the evening cabbage air. ~~The neighborhood was changing. Soon.~~" — the unreadable Italian grocer's sign already shows the change; the flat statement is redundant.
  - **Merge a beat stated twice:** in the church passage, drop "She stood in the doorway for a long moment" and keep the fuller "She stood at the threshold with her basket on her arm and looked at the crucifix…" — it's one action rendered twice.
  - **Compress travelogue connective, keep the reveal:** "~~After going south through the narrow streets, past the tenements, and storefronts with their hand-lettered signs, she noticed that the buildings thinned and~~ **She went south through the narrow streets until the buildings thinned and** the harbor opened up in front of her." — keeps the geography and the reveal, drops the inventory and the "she noticed that" filter.
  - None of these touch a beat, the worldbuilding, the character work, or the apple close. That is the point: in a scene-dense chapter the unit of work is the sentence, not the scene.

- **Chapter Twenty — the St. Paul "ascent" (lines 1292–1380, 3,060 words — the longest chapter in Part Three).** *Correction: an earlier version of this doc called this "90% summary-montage." That was a bad estimate and it's wrong.* On a full read it is roughly **70% dramatized scene** — the Globe-ad exchange, the schoolhouse board scene, the citizenship counter, the Bryant land-buy, the "barn first / house first" comedy with Mary, the "it feels magical" window, the pea-shelling apple-callback, Old Red. So it is the same case as Ch 11: **long but scene-dense — the lever is the sentence, not the scene.** The genuine line-level edits:
  - **Broken sentence — *fixed in Drive 2026-06-15:*** "The clerk behind a desk He had administered this oath…" → "The clerk sat behind a desk. He had administered this oath…"
  - **A point stated three times (citizenship beat):** belonging is asserted at the pen ("This was different. This was the line that said he belonged to a country") and again on the sidewalk ("the first time a country had written his name back"). Keep the rich middle (work/land/buried-children contradiction) and the sidewalk landing; cut the earlier assertion. Say it once.
  - **A paragraph that re-narrates the whole arc (Summit Avenue):** the "$25 → $500" line lands the ascent concretely (Bookfox-specific summary at its best); the following "He had looked up at that ridge… It had seemed like another country then" restates the chapter's arc in miniature — cut it.
  - **Continuity (not pacing):** naturalization stamped "October, 1878" sits after the "May 12, 1884" Globe ad and ~seven years into St. Paul — fix to 1884/1885.
  - **Untouchable anchors:** the pea-shelling scene (the Boston apple returning as Mary's life-philosophy) and the "it feels magical" window are real character payoffs that break the run of wins — leave them. If length ever forces a cut, the *citizenship* beat is the most expendable (arrival is already shown through the schoolhouse and the line of men at his door), but it's optional.

- **Chapter Thirty-Four — Grants Pass reinvention (lines 2393–2411, 700 words).** Already lean, and its summary is *already specific* (the "Ireland and Meade's Addition… Ireland." beat is excellent — keep it), so the lever is **not** compression. It is **Stage 4 of the displacement arc** (see the land-buying note below), and the work is making it read as its own stage, distinct from Stage 3:
  - **Stop announcing the theme twice:** the chapter is titled "Anesthesia" *and* opens "looked from the outside like another reinvention." Keep the first line; **retitle the chapter** — "Anesthesia" is Stage 3's word (Ch 23), and wearing it here flattens the progression. A Stage-4 title points at provision/distance (e.g. "Deeds in Their Names," "The Arc").
  - **Name what makes Oregon different (provision, not just speed):** the "different quality / Less strategy / More velocity" line frames the change as *pace*, which reads as more Stage 3. The new content is provision-from-a-distance because he believes he is a curse — he deeds lots into the children's names, not his own, and "resided in the center of that arc." Let the narration say what the deeds are doing.
  - **Untouchable anchor:** "He used to read her letters twice" is the one slowed, consequential moment (Matesic's rule) — the best line in the chapter.

## Land-buying as grief-displacement — a four-stage arc; protect it, don't cut it

**Correction to an earlier version of this doc, which said "cut to one." That was wrong.** Read end to end, the four land-buying beats are not a repeated note — they are a *progression* that accrues meaning, which is high-craft motif work. Each is a distinct emotional stage:

- **Stage 1 — Victory (Ch 20, line 1339):** "the dirt his own, bought with money his own hands had earned, and the boy at that window felt a long way off." The Dublin slum-boy now owns American ground. (Mary still alive.)
- **Stage 2 — Impotence (Ch 22, line 1448):** "none of it, not the house, not the business, not the money… could buy her one more week." What he built is exposed as powerless against death. The hinge.
- **Stage 3 — Anesthesia (Ch 23, line 1537):** "He needed to pour money into dirt because dirt did not die… filling his name with deeds, and he could not stop." Buying as a way not to feel.
- **Stage 4 — Provision / isolation (Ch 34, line 2396+):** can't paint anymore, so "More velocity"; lots deeded into Virginia's, Lottie's, Rose's names; "His Oregon holdings formed a rough arc around Grants Pass… William resided in the center of that arc." He gives them land because he believes he's a curse and can't safely give them himself.

The prose already threads the motif so it reads as one arc: "the boy at that window" (Stage 1) literally returns as "The boy from the tenements… filling his name with deeds" (Stage 3); "Ireland and Meade's Addition. Ireland." (Stage 4) is the same boy's origin surfacing in a deed. **Keep all four.** Cutting to one would collapse a deliberate arc into a single static image.

**The real lever (this is the actual "delivery" fix):**

1. **Compress the *generic* acquisition summary *between* the anchors, not the anchors.** The fatigue risk isn't the four stages — it's the routine "he bought more lots / the transfer closed in August of 1885" connective (heaviest in Ch 20) that washes around them and makes a reader feel "more land again." Thin that generic montage (Bookfox specific-summary fix) so each of the four stages stands out as its own beat. This *protects* the arc rather than diluting it.

2. **Differentiate Stage 4 from Stage 3.** Ch 34 is titled **"Anesthesia"** — but anesthesia is *Stage 3's* feeling (Ch 23). Stage 4's new content is provision-from-a-distance and self-isolation (deeds in the children's names; the arc with him alone at the dead center). Right now Ch 34 leads with "velocity," which re-treads Stage 3, and the provide/protect/curse meaning sits as subtext. Foreground the new stage: he deeds them *land* because he can't safely give them *himself*. Consider retitling Ch 34 so Stage 4 isn't labeled with Stage 3's word.

## The cycle isn't the problem — the sameness is

The birth → illness → death → X → move cycle is thematically intentional and earns its repetition. What tires a reader is when each turn runs at the *same length and intensity*. The principle: **each reprise of a recurring beat should be shorter than the last, and wrong in a new way.**

- The arrival-and-settle sequence gets a full survey in Dublin, Boston, St. Paul, and Grants Pass. The reader learns the pattern by Boston. Arrivals three and four can compress to a paragraph each and lean into the dread of the rerun rather than re-establishing it.
- The baby-milestone montages can run shorter the second time and trade wonder for foreboding — the reader already knows what milestones precede in this book.

## One repeated image to thin: the apron-on-the-hook

The empty-chair / apron tableau is post-death only and mostly well-deployed:

- **Ch 22, line 1497** — establishing: "Mary's chair was empty. Her apron hung on the hook by the stove. Her reading glasses rested on the windowsill where she had left them."
- **Ch 23, line 1528** — with a *new* beat (the refusal): "He had not let anyone move it. Letitia… tried gently to fold it away. He told her to leave it."
- **Ch 24, line 1708** — with a *new* beat (physical contact): "His sleeve brushed the fabric. He stopped and stood there with his hand on the towel and his arm against the apron."

Each of those does distinct work — establish, refuse, touch — and the interlude callback at 1549 ("He has just finished talking about the apron") is intentional frame work. **Keep all of those.**

The one genuine redundancy is **Ch 23, line 1542**: "The kitchen was quiet. Mary's chair was still empty. Her apron still hung on the hook by the stove." This restates line 1528 nearly verbatim, only fourteen lines later, inside the same chapter. Cut it, or vary it so it advances something. That's the single free improvement to the motif. (The reflective paragraph that follows at 1543 — "He just sat in the chair across from the empty one…" — is doing different, earned work; keep it.)

## Line-level tells — naming the meaning before the scene earns it

Standard subtraction. Each of these states a feeling the surrounding scene is already dramatizing:

- **Ch 28, line 1955 — the "happiness" essay:** "happiness is not a destination. It is a clearing in the woods… the woods have a memory, and they always grow back." This is a beautiful line, but it announces the theme *before* Mary Margaret's diagnosis (1966+) earns it. Consider moving it later so it lands as recognition rather than thesis — or letting the death scene imply it and cutting the statement.
- **Ch 31, line 2250 — "Lottie stumbled at the inspiring story around the compass."** This is the more important one. "Inspiring story" tells us she's persuaded, but the very next lines show her *still resisting* ("What do I tell our neighbors?… I think Oregon is not a cure. I think Oregon is you climbing another ladder"; "I hate you sometimes"). The narration and the dialogue contradict each other. Cut the editorial phrase and let her capitulation play as what it actually is — exhaustion, not inspiration. She's the one character who out-argues William; her surrender is the book's hinge and shouldn't be bought with a single told word.
- **Ch 7, line 551 — "She walked back to the quay with a spark in her step."** Lower priority and genuinely debatable: it sits *mid-scene* as Mary's interior turn, and the actual commitment is dramatized eight lines later ("I'll go, William," 557–562). It reads as a turning-point beat before the spoken declaration, so it's defensible. Only trim if you want the conversion to live entirely in the spoken line.

## Clocks — the rare *additions* worth the words

Most of this pass is subtraction, but two beats would convert existing inevitability into live suspense, which is usually a better use of words than the summary they'd offset:

- **Put Alex on the page (Ch 30–31).** The entire climax stake — "If I stay here, Alex will die" (line 2217) — is carried by secondhand report and William's projection. Alex is born healthy (2054), sits on Lottie's lap at dinner (2178, 2181), and is *talked about* ("She says you peek into Alex's room," 2200), but he never speaks, never shows a symptom, never appears in his own scene. The reader is asked to fear for a child they've never been alone with. One page — William in the doorway at night, counting the odds against this specific sleeping boy — converts asserted dread into dramatized dread. If you add one thing this pass, add this.
- **Re-arm Mary's ultimatum.** "I just can't bury another child. I just can't." (Ch 17, line 1088) is the loaded gun of the move to St. Paul, and then Part Three's prosperity never makes the reader feel it's still loaded. One held breath at a child's cough in Ch 19–20 keeps the threat live through the good years.

## Connective-tissue trims — calibrated, lowest-risk first

In rough priority. The first is a clear win; the rest are judgment calls, flagged honestly:

1. **Reconsider the off-page resolution in Ch 37 (line 2537):** "By the second week and many deep conversations, William's demeanor improved… he started to crack a few jokes." This is the inverse of Matesic's rule — the *most* consequential moment in Part Five (the thaw that the whole crisis has been building toward) is the one rendered in a time-jump summary ("by the second week… improved"), exactly the "passed by uneventfully" construction she flags, applied to the one beat that is anything but uneventful. Slow down here: dramatize one moment of the turn, or let Lottie leave not knowing it worked and put the thaw in Ch 38 where it can be earned. Highest-leverage single change in the back half.

2. **Trim (don't merge) Chapters 26–27 (lines 1810–1948, 4,729 words combined).** These read as deliberate parallels — Ch 26 is Lottie *building* family cohesion (the torn chore-chart, 1832, which she answers by re-pairing the children across family lines rather than hunting the culprit), Ch 27 is Mary's children *scattering* (Letitia's vows). Merging would flatten that reverb, so don't force it. But the connective filler between them is trimmable: the real-estate inventory and house tour (1853–1869) and the recap letter (1920–1927) can compress hard. *Keep* the Rose-painting beat (1873) — it plants Rose's gift before her own departure in Ch 30.

3. **Lottie's St. Paul routine (Ch 37, 2554–2564):** the ledger/competence inventory is methodical and carries real subtext (her numbers "accurate," William's "ambitious"). It's doing more work than a pure stall, so trim rather than gut — but the live beat is **Charlotte taking the pencil** (2574–2576, "a desk in Kansas folded into a kitchen in Minnesota"). Make sure the handoff isn't buried under the inventory that precedes it.

4. **Lower priority / optional:** the Ch 38 re-inventory of holdings (2601, "He still owned property…") is a light touch and now backdrop to the spiritual turn — leave it unless you're tightening hard. The Ch 38 doctor-refusal (2602–2603) is in a *different register* from the Ch 33 one (2380–2381) — tired wisdom vs. anger — so it earns its place; don't cut it for repetition's sake. And the Epilogue bequest percentages (2646) do **not** bury the folded-paper payoff — the paper comes *after*, as the final beat (2649–2657). On Matesic's denouement test (closure without "too long" a tail), the Epilogue passes: the percentages are short, in dialogue, and the climactic object lands last. Leave it.
