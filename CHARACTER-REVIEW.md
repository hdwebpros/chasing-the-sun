## Part 1 — The research: what modern historical fiction demands of characters

Twenty-four techniques were collected; these are the twelve with real leverage on this manuscript.

| # | Technique | Source |
|---|-----------|--------|
| R1 | **Invent inward, never against the record.** "From history, I know what they do, but I can't with any certainty know what they think or feel." The record constrains *deeds*; flaw, doubt, and pettiness live in *motive* — which the record can never contradict. For an ancestor-based protagonist, the license to darken him is precisely his interior. | Mantel, Reith Lecture 1 |
| R2 | **Period flaws are free characterization.** "A good novelist will have her characters operate within the ethical framework of their day — even if it shocks her readers." Son-preference, patriarchal rigidity, land-hunger, silence about feeling — likelier true of a 19th-century Irishman than modern likability, and they create friction without anachronism. | Mantel, Reith Lecture 2 |
| R3 | **Free the people from the archive** — write them "ignorant of their fates." The frame (old man looking back) may know; the inner chapters must not. No narration that quietly knows Oregon is coming. | Mantel, Reith Lecture 1 |
| R4 | **Love every character, especially minor ones.** Lee's "community narrative": even in a close-POV book, every spouse/child deserves at least one scene written in full sympathy with *their* agenda — where the protagonist is the obstacle in *their* story. | Min Jin Lee, Pachinko interviews |
| R5 | **The misbelief and the origin scene.** Every protagonist carries one flawed conclusion about how the world works, formed in a specific dramatizable moment; the plot exists to dismantle it. The misbelief must be *born on the page*, not pre-installed by narration. | Lisa Cron, *Story Genius* |
| R6 | **Lack of agency is a named rejection reason** — period constraint is no excuse. Pivotal beats must be decisions made *on the page*, with an alternative visibly refused; offstage choices "blunt the impact." | Anna Mazzola (agented novelist, summarizing editor feedback) |
| R7 | **Micro-tension: friction in every exchange, even between allies.** Mixed motives inside a character — resentment inside gratitude, ambition inside duty — generate line-by-line uncertainty without altering the true story. | Donald Maass, *Emotional Craft* |
| R8 | **Three likenesses, one opposition.** For each significant secondary: three ways they mirror the protagonist, one way they are exactly opposite. The opposition is their lever of conflict; what makes them matter is how they *change* him. | Maass, *The Fire in Fiction* |
| R9 | **The "juggler fact."** Towles's secondaries came alive via one concrete surplus detail exceeding their plot function (Andrey was a juggler). One skill, past, or obsession per character, surfacing twice, never explained. | Amor Towles, Gentleman in Moscow Q&A |
| R10 | **The frame needs its own arc, and the listener needs a stake.** The frame situation must change between first and last appearance (the dictation must *go* somewhere — the will altered mid-telling); the listener should resist, misunderstand, or want something. Interruption is characterization. | Frame-narrative craft consensus |
| R11 | **Large-cast discipline:** chart every character's own goal; merge or cut those with no role in the climax; represent groups through one or two spokespersons; every named character has a stake in their first scene. The will is a natural climax that *forces* every named heir to matter. | K.M. Weiland, large-casts rules |
| R12 | **Anachronism of mind is worse than anachronism of object.** New interiority must draw metaphor from scripture, land, weather, work, and folk belief — never psychology. (The book's trade-as-master-metaphor already obeys this; every fix below must too.) | Historical Novel Society / The History Quill |

Three more worth keeping in the back pocket: **R13** Whitehead's "stick to the truth, not the facts" (emotional truth outranks genealogy when fidelity blocks a needed scene); **R14** Towles's time-accordion (granular at identity hinges, compressed in stable years — the book already mostly does this, but see Gap W4); **R15** O'Farrell's move of writing the "headline" event from the household interior, at the level of hands and objects — which is this author's native register anyway.

**Editors' bottom line (Dutton, 2020s):** period difference outside, recognizable struggle inside — and the underheard perspectives (in an emigration saga, the women) are what acquisitions notice. The book's strongest character asset is exactly that inside/outside split; the women are where deepening pays twice.

## Part 2 — Scorecard: what the cast already delivers

Credit first — much of this is professional-grade and should be defended, not revised:

- **The want is the most legible and best-objectified element in the draft.** Sign → paper → deeds → will: one ladder, all objects (l. 138 → 253 → 1542 → 2604). The want *mutates legibly* per era — name-on-sign, name-via-son, name-as-fortress, name-as-bequest. This is the R5/Weiland skeleton done right at the Want level.
- **Mary is the standard the rest of the cast should be held to.** Independent, incompatible wants stated as such ("I can't live with change like this," l. 532); solo scenes without William (the quay self-conversion l. 540–553, "I am still here" l. 704, the apple l. 724–731); a self-narrated arc (l. 1337–1349); a death she controls. She passes every R4/R6 test.
- **Lottie is the book's second-best character** — solo-POV interiority no other secondary gets (the torn chart l. 1807, the hollow-chest foreclosure l. 2071–77, her own 25-line morning l. 2534–2556), the only adult who fights William twice and means it, and "I am not Mary" (l. 1815) is the single best two-way friction line in Part 4.
- **Frame-William is genuinely characterized apart from story-William:** the collar adjustment, the thumb on the knee (l. 20, echoed at l. 1629 — a lovely cross-era stitch), catastrophe delivered "the way you say a street address" (l. 1001). Hollis silently adopting William's posture (l. 2262) is the listener changed by the story — exactly R10.
- **Interiority deepens with age by design:** Dublin-William seen from outside, St. Paul adds the great compulsive runs ("He bought property like another man drank. For the brief absence of dread," l. 1840 — the best psychological sentence in the manuscript), Oregon's fall and breakdown are the most embodied sequences in the book. The narrator's one refusal of access (steerage, l. 622) is masterful.
- **The cheap-individuation toolkit exists and works:** Teresa's feathers (l. 876–887), Austin's water bucket and "More birds to eat" (l. 851, 927), Letitia's "Seems like a good door to stand in" (l. 716), Anetta's one perfect line (l. 1702), William Travers's bees (l. 1709–1727). The book *knows how* — it just didn't spend the beat everywhere.
- **The God-relationship is conducted bodily for sixty years** — sitting when others kneel (l. 684), painting God's house perfectly while despising the tenant (l. 1496) — the R12 ideal, until the very end (CR Gap 6).

**Do not touch:** Mary's death sequence (l. 1443–1480), the porch indictment (l. 2477–2498), Rose's Liffey painting (l. 1981–2015), the chore-chart redesign (l. 1819–1823), Letitia's deed-return (l. 1877), Mary Margaret's carriage-ride permission (l. 1651–1659), the "I hate you sometimes" / "I hate myself for loving you sometimes" echo (l. 2231/2511), the 100%-callback pair (l. 2222 → 2596, being re-phrased in LANGUAGE-PASS but staying a callback), and "Found ya."

## Part 3 — The gaps

### William (the protagonist layer)

**W1 — The lie is installed before it's earned (R5).**
"You are the kind of thing that ruins what it touches" is *narrated* at the sign-hanging (l. 140), when eighteen-year-old William has been discarded but has ruined nothing. Half the misbelief is earned (the parentage wound, l. 130–131); the ruin-half arrives as the author reaching forward. Cron's rule: the misbelief needs an origin scene — and the book already has the perfect one, the first X (l. 318–324).

**W2 — His runs are always vindicated, so his defining flaw never costs him (R1, R6).**
Every prior move improves the family's lot; he is "almost always right about commerce" (l. 397); Boston's graves are blamed on water; Mary retroactively blesses the whole itinerary (l. 1348, 1416). The reader is trained for 2,000 lines that William's runs pay off — then the epilogue confirms the abandonment paid off too ("Every last one of them," l. 2598). The abandonment reads tragic-logical instead of chosen-and-costly. Compounding it: the cruelest things he says are flagged but never billed — "Most were my sons." (l. 1266) costs one sentence of regret; "Our poor kids? They should be thankful." (l. 2230) costs nothing the scene registers. What's missing before 1900 is one run, one exchange, with a victim who isn't posthumously absolved.

**W3 — The curse's survivorship logic is never punctured.**
Hollis — professionally trained in causation — accepts post-hoc proof from a client (l. 2595–2598). The book doesn't need to refute William; ambiguity is right. But it currently *endorses* him.

**W4 — Interiority goes dark exactly when the arc most needs him from inside.**
Two relapses into opacity: the courtship (l. 1565–1780 — POV tilts to Lottie; the man deciding to love again after the lie crystallized gets one flicker, l. 1643) and chapters 36–38, where the recovery is narrated by other people ("William's demeanor improved… cracked a few jokes," l. 2513; "something shifted slowly like dawn," l. 2574). The book's deepest healing happens off-William.

**W5 — The book's deepest insight belongs only to the narrator.**
"He was not leaving them. He was the one being left behind" (l. 2257) — the hinge of the entire character — is narrated once on a train. Frame-William, who confesses everything else, never owns it to Hollis.

**W6 — The bravest act William ever performs is unstaged (R6).**
"Charlotte. Come to Oregon. I need you to come." (l. 2425) — the first time in 84 years he asks for help — is seen only from Lottie's end. The man who obsessively drafted and re-drafted advertisements (l. 1278, 2304) writes three lines with the pen scoring the paper; the contrast with his ad ritual *is* the content, and it's missing.

**W7 — Early defeat already exists, unexploited.**
CR Gap 7 asks for an invented early defeat; cheaper to make the existing one reverberate: Kennedy turning him away at the dock — "For the first time, William tried something physical and wasn't certain about it. He wanted to quit." (l. 735–742) — never echoes again. Relatedly, the warned-about anti-Irish cage is told three times (l. 503, 660, 672) and never once *shown happening to William*: he wins every bid, ad, and lot for fifty years.

**W8 — Leaving Dublin costs him nothing he touches.**
George and William Jr. lie in *unmarked* graves (l. 316, 461 "No stone"). The concrete consequence — with no stones, he could never find his sons again; the folded paper is the only marker that exists — is never stated at the gangway. The first run with a real price leaves the price implicit.

### The frame (Hollis)

**F1 — A prosecutor used as a stenographer (R10; confirms CR Gap 1/Phase 1.1 from the character side).**
Passive in interludes 1, 2, 4 (l. 385–393, 995–1011); detonates magnificently once ("Was the life you were building the thing that wore her out?" l. 1534); immediately disarms himself ("I don't think I have the right to debate you," l. 1548); finishes as an exposition machine (l. 2595, 2599 — already CR Gap 9).

**F2 — Two large dramatic ironies sit unexploited.**
(a) **The legal collision:** William is dictating a will naming as primary beneficiary a wife who has told the State of Minnesota she is his *widow* for 25 years ("When the census person comes, that is what you say. Legally we are still married, it will make the deeds work," l. 2228). Hollis, who notices everything, never notices the one fact squarely inside his professional competence. This is CR Gap 4's unfired census gun seen from the character side — the frame is the natural place it fires, and it would characterize both men at once. (b) **Why now?** William dictates in 1925 and dies in 1927 of an obstruction he declined surgery for (l. 2643–44) — a man with his history of refusing doctors, climbing those stairs *now*. The frame never lets us suspect he already knows something.

**F3 — Hollis's own stakes are planted and dropped (R10).**
The nine-year-old daughter (l. 1002) is one beat of empathy, then gone — a lawyer with a girl that age, transcribing a man who buried five children of hers. The cabinet of other men's last documents (l. 15) likewise: what makes him stop taking notes *for this one* is never dramatized.

### The abandoned half (the book's biggest cast failure)

**A1 — Alex is a premise, not a person (R4, R11).**
The son whose survival justifies the abandonment ("If I stay here, Alex will die," l. 2193), who receives the compass (l. 2226) and 60% of the estate (l. 2604), **never speaks one word in the entire book**. And the compass — haggled for on arrival day (l. 1133–1138), hidden 25+ years — never pays off: we never see Alex receive it, carry it, or stand in front of his father. The whole Oregon wager has no on-page payee; Alex's absence is the curse question's missing witness.

**A2 — Lottie is abandoned by the book before the end (R4).**
After her solo coda (l. 2534–2556, ending on "fix rail") she never appears in scene again; 18 years of marriage-by-ledger are silent; the epilogue disposes of her in a clause ("he will take care of Lottie," l. 2604). The widow-lie is *her* lie to live, surfaced once inside the porch speech (l. 2486), dramatized never. The book's most important live wire is cut 120 lines before the end.

**A3 — Charlotte is built for the reckoning and never used.**
Quietly the best late-book child: "I lied to my daughter. For you" (l. 2216), the disbelieved goodbye (l. 2248), the ledger handoff three-generation rhyme (l. 2541–2552), "When is he coming home?" every few weeks (l. 2545). William never directs a single beat at her — no letter, no line, nothing until the will. If CR Gap 5's staged reckoning is bought, Charlotte is already loaded.

### The rest of the cast

**C1 — Mary goes inert exactly where the middle sags (CR Gap 1).**
From the train window (l. 1123) to her illness (l. 1368) — ~250 lines — she offers zero resistance; her one St. Paul agency beat is negotiating *in William's favor* (l. 1248–54). The marriage's friction engine, the best engine in Parts 1–2, idles through the prosperity stretch. Hollis's later accusation ("She carried ten pregnancies in twenty years," l. 1526) indicts a cost no scene staged while it was being incurred.

**C2 — Mary Margaret: the problem is vector, not quantity.**
She has plenty of beats (brooch, sled, carriage-ride, "Do you love him?", the pot concession) — and every one vectors toward someone else's arc. Dead at 24 with no want, no refusal, no suitor of her own; even her deathbed request points away from herself ("Tell me about Ma," l. 1954). She dies as a mirror. CR Gap 10 asked for "one scene that is hers"; the character requirement is sharper: one scene where she wants something that isn't a family function.

**C3 — Doyle: the father-shaped hole.**
The book twice marks the inheritance ("He was Doyle now," l. 1294; the Oregon crew evaluations, l. 2299) and never closes the loop — William's own question ("Wondering if he'd be proud of me," l. 804) goes unanswered. The deeper, unremarked inheritance: Doyle's emotional grammar — approval as the smallest nod (l. 122) — becomes William's entire fathering language (l. 614, 1197, 2007). William parents like Doyle and never notices. Also: James & Mary Boog, the parents who reared him, are simply never mentioned in the emigration chapters (l. 563–601) — for a book about being discarded, William silently discards them.

**C4 — Letitia: the unused theological antagonist; her door and God's door never rhyme.**
She is the only living character positioned to argue the curse with William, and her one attempt is two sentences (l. 2174–2181). Meanwhile the book's first door (the boy in the rain at Doyle's — "In with ye, boy," l. 96–103), Letitia's door ("Seems like a good door to stand in," l. 716), and the final door ("it was still open," l. 2581) are the same image, and the text never lets them touch. Between Italy (l. 2368) and the ch. 38 epiphany she is silent — exactly the stretch CR Gap 6 says lacks runway.

**C5 — The children gradient (R11).**
The toolkit works where it was spent (Teresa, Austin, Letitia, Anetta, William Travers) and wasn't spent on: **John — zero traits, four-line death, and he is the *no sons left* death** (l. 983–992; CR Gap 7 confirmed and sharpened); Joseph — thumb-squeeze only (l. 782); Mary Travers / James / Clementine — roster entries (l. 1677); Virginia's "reading at four" stated, never staged (l. 2385).

**C6 — Named-once noise (B11/R11).**
Edward & Mollie Fitzgerald get two full names for handing over towels (l. 1730–33); Widow Brennan (l. 1025), Harlan (l. 2333), Dording (l. 866), landlord-Joseph (l. 221 — the third Joseph) earn nothing. (Exception: if Fitzgerald is a family Easter egg, keep and give them one half-beat.)

**C7 — The antagonist problem, diagnosed in the author's own line.**
"He wanted to hit something that could hit back. He wanted an enemy he could see" (l. 1424). Audit result: **William never loses a contested exchange with a human being in the entire book.** Doyle capitulates in one scene; Mary's "No" is resolved by her own off-page self-conversion; the clerk/foreman/landlord are one-beat props; the schoolhouse board folds in a paragraph; Hollis disarms himself; Lottie yields twice by absorbing the cost. The God-quarrel needs human proxies — Hollis, Lottie, Letitia — because weather and bacteria can't be cross-examined.

**C8 — The women pattern (Dutton's "underheard perspective").**
The wives get interiority; the daughters get gestures. Letitia and Rose escape only by *leaving the family* — the only daughters with self-directed lives are the ones who exit the plot to get them. Worth knowing as a pattern even where no single fix is owed.

## Part 4 — Improvement plan

Same rules as CRAFT-REVIEW: flag-and-suggest, author approves, `book-edit` applies to Drive, rebuild. Every fix below is an object/body/dialogue/withheld-gesture move per VOICE.md — no emotion labels, no "the way a…" similes, no triads. Costs: **beat** (1–4 lines) / **half-scene** / **scene**.

### Tier 1 — Fixes that serve character AND structure (do these with CRAFT-REVIEW Phase 1)

1. **Fire the compass; stage the witness (A1 + CR Gaps 4, 5).** One scene, late Part 5 or folded into the will afternoon: grown Alex in Grants Pass (the epilogue already places property and duty on him), the brass compass coming *out of Alex's pocket*, worn, the needle steadied. No speech about forgiveness — an object handed back or kept, a held beat, an exit on a physical action. The single highest-leverage character fix in the book: it gives the wager a payee, fires the loudest planted gun, and answers the curse question with evidence instead of testimony. **Cost: one scene.**
   - *Cheaper variant or companion (W-side):* a letter from eight-year-old Alex arriving in Oregon, child's hand, one question William cannot answer ("What are you painting?"); William answers the business half, files the letter in the deed drawer. The drawer beat shows what protection-by-absence actually does. **One scene.**

2. **Arm Hollis from interlude one; spring the legal collision (F1, F2a, W3; = CR Phase 1.1 + Gap 4, executed at character level).** Lawyer's questions with teeth from the first interlude (he already owns the instrument: "Longer." l. 1107). Then, in a late interlude or the epilogue, the collision: William names Lottie as beneficiary; Hollis's pen stops — the widow on the census and the wife in the will are the same woman, and he's the one who has to make the deeds work. William's flat disclosure, Hollis's professional silence. Also one beat of Hollis almost-objecting to the survivorship logic — pen lifted ("The water in Boston was also—"), William's look stopping him, Hollis writing something down instead. The reader gets to hold both readings of the curse; nothing is editorialized. **Cost: 4–6 beats across existing scenes.**

3. **Dramatize the census lie at Lottie's door (A2 + CR Gap 4).** The enumerator at the Dayton Avenue door; Lottie's steady voice saying "widow"; Charlotte's pencil stopping. No narration of feeling. Pays the unfired gun, gives the widow-lie to the woman who lives it, and loads the frame collision in fix 2. **Cost: one short scene.**

4. **Move the lie's birth to the first X (W1, R5).** Cut "ruins what it touches" from l. 140 (keep the discard-wound — that *is* earned); after the first X (l. 321–324), one beat: William looking at his own hand — the hand that wrote both the name and the mark — then closing it. The misbelief now has its origin scene. **Cost: one cut + one beat.**

5. **One run that costs; one exchange he loses (W2, W7, C1, C7).** Three small installations, any two suffice:
   - *Mary's St. Paul friction beat:* the lots scene (l. 1324–1331, she already wins "house first") or the price negotiation (l. 1248–54) re-pointed so William loses something visible and the loss isn't charming — the wearing-out Hollis later names, happening on the page. **One beat.**
   - *One shown nativist defeat:* Boston or early St. Paul, a bid lost with the reason said to his face or posted in a window; his response physical and small (he repaints his own sign that night). Gives the "quiet war" an actual enemy and the early earned defeat M7 asks for. **One beat–one short scene.**
   - *Bill the cruel lines:* "Most were my sons." (l. 1266) earns one consequence that lands — Mary's hands going back to the mending, the door of her attention closing for the rest of the scene. **One beat.**

6. **Stage the recovery inside William via the bird journal (W4 + CR Gaps 5, 6 — this is the *how* for CR Phase 1.5/1.6).** The man who counted foot traffic "between two and four o'clock" (l. 368) and coins into a tin now logs warbler arrival times — same method, same hands, new object, pointed at nothing sellable. One scene of the journal in use dramatizes the conversion of his signature strength without one abstract word. **Cost: one scene (riding the planned ch. 38 expansion).**

7. **Let the three doors rhyme (C4, W6-adjacent + CR Gap 6).** In the expanded ch. 38, in place of God's quoted reply (l. 2583, already slated for cutting): rain on the Oregon porch, William standing outside his own door the way he once stood on Stephen Street, the wait, then stepping through. Optionally carried by a human hand first: one letter from Letitia in the ch. 35–37 stretch, in her established letter-voice (l. 1893), one line about doors or light, unexplained; William files it, doesn't answer. **Cost: two beats.**

### Tier 2 — Cast debts (CRAFT-REVIEW Phase 2 companions)

8. **Lottie gets the last word she's owed (A2).** One beat in the epilogue frame: Hollis asks about the wife the will protects; William produces not the names-paper but something of hers — her handwriting on a ledger page, or the weekly letter rule (l. 2236) still running after 25 years, never once missed. Proof by object, not summary. **Cost: one beat–half-scene.**

9. **Charlotte gets one gesture from William (A3).** Her drawing (l. 2502) arriving in Oregon; what he does with it — pinned where the property maps are, or folded into the ledger — tells everything. **Cost: one beat.**

10. **Stage the three-line letter (W6).** Half-scene at the Oregon desk before ch. 36: no crossing out this time, no rehearsal — the contrast with the ad-writing ritual (l. 1278, 2304) is the content. End on the walk to the post office. **Cost: half-scene.**

11. **Mary Margaret's own scene (C2 + CR Gap 10).** In the 1891–92 stretch (between l. 1822 and l. 1931): she's offered something for herself — a match, a position, a household of her own — and turns it down with the brooch at her collar; only the reader sees what the refusal costs. Her deathbed bequest (l. 1951) then ends *her* line, not Lottie's coronation. **Cost: one scene.**

12. **Close the Doyle loop at "He was Doyle now" (C3 + CR Gap 10).** News of Doyle's death arriving in the St. Paul ascent, anchored to l. 1293–94 where William interviews men in the line he once stood in; one gesture — he checks a hire's boots before his hands, or sends money for a stone he'll never see. No eulogy. Plus one line for James & Mary Boog in the leaving-Dublin sequence — the man who chose the double-O dying before seeing it on a quay-corner sign. **Cost: two beats.**
