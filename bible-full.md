## Motif tracker: the name motif

- **Prologue:** Dominant motif of the chapter: Hollis warns 'a will is mostly names... I'll need all of them, even the ones you'd rather leave out'; 'Boog. That's an unusual name... Where does it come from?'; William: 'It doesn't come from anywhere... My mother chose the name William... my adopted father made up the spelling of the last name'; 'She chose my Catholic name herself. Weeks before I arrived'; 'a name no one wanted'; and 'You cannot write one name down without the rest of them crowding in.'
- **Chapter One:** Central motif of the chapter. "The more names he learned, the less he knew his own." Jon Murphy's name means "sea warrior" and destines him to cross the ocean. William asks "Pa, what name was I born into?" James explains the Scottish kin "go by B-o-a-g now" and that his father chose "new place, new spellin'. B-o-o-g." William asks "What does it mean to be a Boog, yer name?" — James: "It means nuttin'... maybe it'll mean 'famine survivor'." William vows "Then I'll put the meanin' in it meself," and prints WILLIAM BOOG "square letters, pressed hard" at the top of his stolen sheet.
- **Chapter Two:** Central at the birth: the midwife asks "Has his father got a name for him at all?" and the mother whispers "William... Just the name itself. Solid. A common name that wouldn't stand out. Safe. She wanted him to be ordinary. Ordinary boys lived." The surname arrives via secret adoption ("James would claim him for a son"), first used at "William Boog walked down the middle of the mess." Also the unnamed catastrophe: "There was no word for it that he knew" / "An Gorta Mór. The Great Starvation."
- **Chapter Three:** Central motif of the chapter. Doyle on craft: "Our name is on it, you know?" Then the sign: "BOOG — BOOTS & SHOES." — "His name. On something permanent. Not scratched into dirt, not scribbled in a church registry. This was a new name... But it was a name with no meaning. Maybe, Pa had said, it would come to mean famine survivor." And: "Born with a name that weighed nothing, fastened to nothing, he had painted it in clean block letters at last." Sign meant "for the weaver in Perthshire to read if word ever carried that far."
- **Chapter Four:** Strong. Mutual name-reveal: "It's Moran by the way, Mary Moran" / "He is James Boog. I'm William"; Mary's puzzled "Boog?"; William's grievance "Addin' nothin' to a name that means nothin'?"; his hope "a name that means nothin' here might mean somethin' wherever it's headed"; closing beat "William and Mary Boog now existed."
- **Chapter Five:** Strong throughout: William paints and hangs "Clean block letters on fresh board: W. BOOG — BOOTMAKER"; "His name, in full, above a proper door on a proper street... His."; he eats a loss "rather than put his name on a boot he could not stand behind"; at dusk "The last of the copper light caught the letters of his name above the door"; the sunset river turns "a color that had no name"; also the department stores "Dubliners call the Monster Houses"
- **Chapter Six:** Pervasive. William writes names in careful letters — "MARY," then "George" — echoing "a boy printing his name at the top of a stolen page," but "the first name on it was not his own." The doctor writes down "George Boog, eleven months." The X turns the name into "George X" — "his firstborn son was accounted for"; George's grave "held no stone marker," so the paper name is his only mark (chapter title: "The Smallest Mark"). Letitia is "Named for William's adopted sister's middle name, the only piece of her he could carry forward." The new shop gets a sign "twice the size of the old board: W. BOOG — BOOTMAKER."
- **Chapter Seven:** Dense: William Junior is "his namesake... two names crossed out"; William coins "Northampton trash" for English boots ("That's what it is and that's what I'll call it"), signs the ad "William Boog, 13 Aston's Quay"; the pub men christen him "Mr. Fancy Boots"; the chapter closes on the sign staying behind after they sell: "the name Boog would hang on, left behind as proof. A name from nothing, twice."
- **Chapter Eight:** Strong. William leaves his shop signs hanging: "The signs still hung above the door of number thirteen. He did not take them down" — recalling that on Stephen Street "he had reached up and touched each letter, his name fixed for the first time on something in the physical world." At the gangway the clerk asks "Names?" and Letitia answers "Letitia Boog. And this is Teresa" — the girls become "the first two entries on a manifest of eight hundred and sixty-three." William, caught in the shuffle, goes unrecorded: "The clerk had already moved on. William did not care about ledgers."
- **Chapter Nine:** The paper is a list of NAMES: "Seven names rode in his breast pocket"; William's night vigil — "He might have been reading names to the stars." Also the family-name vow: "no ground in America had ever buried a Boog."
- **Chapter Ten:** The clerk erases William's trade identity: William says "Bootmaker. Me own shop on the Liffey" and the clerk writes "Laborer it is"; William protests "I said bootmaker" and is overridden ("I heard you"). No beats on personal-name spelling or renaming.
- **Chapter Eleven:** Nearly absent as a motif; only incidental name usage — "The Boog family walked home," the dead son referred to as "William Junior" ("the night they buried William Junior"), and the exclusionary label "No Irish Need Apply" in the grocer's window
- **Chapter Twelve:** At the hide house the salesman waits, pencil up, "as if the rest of the name were still coming. The name of a firm" after William says "It's for me own shop. Boog. Bootmaker." — a lone man's name is not enough for industrial Boston. Kennedy "called names from a manifest" on Long Wharf. Mary is invoked full-name in "the domestic operations of Mary Boog," and she addresses him formally: "I wash these every night, William Boog." The old painter hums a bar he "never named."
- **Chapter Thirteen:** Mary names the newborn with a single word, "'Joseph,' she said"; William welcomes him by name, "Welcome to ye, Joseph"; the old-country name for London Bridge, "We used to call it High Gates"; Joseph dies in "the very country William had chased across the Atlantic to escape the verdict on his name"; the closing dread of "the thing crossing out these names."
- **Chapter Fourteen:** William uses Austin's full name at the deathbed: "I'll kill every bird in the world for ye, Austin James." Writing the X, William notes Austin was "the only one who could carry on his name." The foreman hails him by surname alone: "Boog, come down." Chapter closes naming the death dates formally: "Austin Boog died December 18, 1874. Teresa Boog died December 24, 1874."
- **Chapter Sixteen:** Strong. William introduces himself "'Boog,' he said... 'William Boog.'"; Flynn: "Never heard of a Boog at all"; William: "Most of the time I don't think anyone has. That'll change."; Murphy: "Isn't that the whole of why we crossed? To make something of our names?"; the Minnesota colony is christened — "A New Ireland, they're callin' it" (Bishop Ireland's own name doing double duty); landlord addresses him as "Mr. Boog."
- **Chapter Seventeen:** Strong. Bishop Ireland's name as irony: "A bishop out there buyin' up land for Catholic families. Ireland, he's called... A bishop named Ireland, sellin' us a new one." Mary's tally of dead children as names: "Mary carried the lighter trunk and counted every name not in it." The interlude turns on names in the will: "before you write another name, you should know the list is longer than what I've told you." Hollis addresses him as "Mr. Boog."
- **Chapter Eighteen:** One hit: William introduces himself at the Summit Avenue door — "The name is William. William Boog." — and the name buys him nothing; the homeowner answers with "You don't belong here." (Minor place-naming: "a ridge called Summit Avenue.")
- **Chapter Nineteen:** Heavy. Naming the baby: "'Rosellen,' Mary said... 'Rose,' William repeated." The paper's "three clean names. Mary Margaret. Letitia. Rose. The names that had no mark after them." Surname survival: "A daughter's children would answer to another man's name. If Boog was to outlive him, the paper needed a boy who lived." The shop sign: "Williams & Boog... His name on a building in America... the first time it meant ownership." The stillborn boy: "The boy never was assigned a name and the war had always been a war over names"; William says most were his sons and regrets it "because she had been counting children and his mind had gone to the name, which was the whole sickness in one breath"; he ends sitting with the thought "that the war might be built on something deeper than names."
- **Chapter Twenty:** Dense throughout: the shop window means pedestrians pass "a window with his name on it"; he reads "his own name in print... his name carried by someone else's type" in the Globe; he signs the naturalization form "Wm. A. Boog" — "His name, in his own hand, on something issued by a government that now claimed him as its own" — and the country "would keep his name beside theirs" (his buried children); he names the horse ("whom he named Old Red"); public naming: "Men called him Boog. Customers called him Old Reliable."
- **Chapter Twenty-One:** Light touches only: "the clean floor she had named" (Mary's naming of the house's virtue); the doctor formally addresses them "Mrs. Boog" and "Mr. Boog"
- **Chapter Twenty-Two:** Pervasive. Mary stops William with his name: "She had used his name quiet and final"; she calls him "William Boog" ("Ye didn't drag me anywhere, William Boog"). William's bitter inventory: "not the name he had hammered into this city... could buy her one more week." At the grave: "MARY at the top. Her name... The name without an X"; "the column of their children. He had carried every name on it for decades." After writing the X: "MARY X. The first name. The last X he ever wanted to write... now the name at the top carried the same mark as the children it sheltered."

## Motif tracker: doors

- **Name motif:** Dense. The paper is literally a list of names ('I just wanted to write the names down' — begun 'the night my first son was born in Dublin'); 'The names are in the will now.' A grandson is 'named after me, William Boog,' and William says 'A name like that can be a hard thing to carry. Maybe the compass helps him land somewhere good.' He signs his full name 'William Alexander Boog... clean and unhurried.' Lottie's paper erasure of his name: 'She told the census that she is a widow' — 'He had been dead on paper for years.' Alex pointedly withholds 'father,' saying 'Thank you, William.'
- **Doors:** Heavy threshold beats. William's answer to how he isn't broken: 'a door opened for me at the right time.' His 'eyes keep going to the door' as boots climb; 'Hollis opens the door' and Alex stands 'filling the doorway,' then 'comes through the door.' Leaving, Alex 'goes to the door. His hand is on the frame when he stops,' returns for the kiss, then 'walks to the door and stops one last time.' Finally William 'opens the door and steps onto the landing' and 'The door gently swings shut behind him' onto G Street. (Ties to Ch 38's open-door cliffhanger.)
