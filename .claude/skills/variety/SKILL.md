# variety

Pop one offending paragraph, fix it in the author's voice, get approval, ship it to
Drive, rebuild the epub. This skill exists because generic "vary your sentences"
advice keeps producing the same three self-inflicted wounds. The whole point is to
NOT do what training reflexively wants.

## What training wants you to do (DON'T)

These are the moves to actively resist — every one was made and rejected in the
session that produced this skill:

1. **Don't just subtract the tic.** Cutting `She / She / She` down to one `She` is
   *subtraction*, not a fix — the prose still doesn't move. **A fix isn't done when
   the tic is gone; it's done when an opener actually VARIES.**
2. **Don't collapse a run into a triad.** Folding three parallel sentences into one
   `X, Y, and Z` comma list trades one tell for another. Real parallelism here is
   **two blunt beats, or four** — never the smooth matched three.
3. **Don't protect a fragment-stack as "signature burstiness".** `Dry. Clean. No
   mold.` is three matched one-word fragments — that's *both* a triad *and* staccato,
   not voice. Two clipped fragments by a long sentence = voice; three matched = tic.
4. **Don't reach for the dependent-clause opener.** `Before she paid, she counted…`
   is Bookfox-approved in general but OFF-VOICE here. Use the prepositional/time-place
   opener instead (the Bookfox helper, below).
5. **Don't impose generic craft.** Judge "off-voice" against VOICE.md and the real
   Bookfox/Matesic sources, never training-flavored "good writing".
6. **Don't ship a long subjectless fragment.** When you dissolve a fragment-stack into
   an accumulation pile, it is tempting to write a 30–40 word run of comma-joined noun
   phrases and end it with a period — `The noise of all of it, the schedule that…,
   meals when…, the neighbors in and out.` That has NO main clause (no subject + finite
   verb) and is a run-on fragment: not a punchy beat, not a sentence, the worst of both
   worlds. The author's fragments are SHORT punchy beats (`William did not move.`); his
   *long* lines are accumulation **apposition hung off a real main clause**
   (`…still lean, lean from growing faster than food could follow`). So hang the pile
   off a clause that has a verb: `the house had a hum to it, the noise of it, the
   schedule that…, the neighbors in and out` — `house had a hum` is the subject+verb,
   the rest renames *the hum*. **After every rewrite, find the finite main verb. If the
   sentence carrying your pile has none, it is a fragment — rebuild it.**
7. **Don't grant a stack of matched shorts a "burstiness" pass because a long sentence
   sits nearby.** This was a real miss: `She closed her eyes. Her lips moved. The
   children followed. All except William.` got waved off as "healthy burst" because the
   paragraph opened with a longer sentence. Wrong. **Burstiness is ONE short beat set
   AGAINST an adjacent long piling one — the contrast is the whole point.** Four matched
   3–4 word declaratives in a row is staccato no matter what sits two sentences away. A
   long sentence *elsewhere in the paragraph* does not redeem a run of shorts; only a
   long sentence *adjacent to* the short redeems it. Count the run itself, not the
   paragraph's overall texture.
8. **Don't declare the paragraph done after fixing only the top-scored tic.** "ONE fix"
   means one surgical change shipped per offender — it does NOT mean "fix the worst tic
   and stop." A paragraph often carries two tics at once (a staccato stack upstream AND a
   He-run downstream, as line 693 did). Fixing the He-run while leaving the stack
   standing ships a paragraph that still metronomes. **Before presenting, re-scan the
   WHOLE paragraph for all three tics — not just the region you touched — and clear every
   one in the single proposed rewrite.**
9. **Don't rationalize a three-noun pile as "two appositive beats."** `…a path back, the
   deed in her name, a garden and a room that were hers alone` is a TRIAD — the ear hears
   `comma … comma … and` across three nouns (deed / garden / room) no matter how you nest
   the grammar to defend it. Nesting one item as a prepositional tail (`a room … with a
   garden behind it`) is the same move in a trench coat; it still reads as a list. **The
   triad test is acoustic, not grammatical: count the noun-slots a reader HEARS between the
   main clause and the final period. Three matched slots = triad, full stop.** Don't argue
   the structure on paper — say it out loud. If it lands like A, B, and C, it is. Cut to
   two real beats or build to four; when in doubt, ONE concrete appositive beats a clever
   pile. (Author, this session: *"explain how that doesn't read like a triad. Seems triad
   to me?"* — it did. I was defending a pile.)
10. **Don't propose cutting a concrete noun as "filler/redundant" until you've checked it
   isn't a motif.** My reflex twice steered toward deletion — "the deed just restates
   sentence 1, cut it" — but **deeds/property/ownership are a structural spine of this
   book** (the wall-of-ownership chapter, names on deeds, `Dirt stayed`). A detail that
   recurs across the manuscript is load-bearing, not padding; thinning a tic must never
   quietly amputate a recurring image. **Before recommending you DROP any concrete noun
   (deed, ledger, X, the paper, a name, the ladder, a window), grep the manuscript for it —
   if it appears in other scenes, it is spine: preserve it, restructure around it.** "Full
   cut" is almost always the wrong instinct here; the author's content is rarely the
   problem, the *rhythm* is. Tighten the rhythm, keep the thing.
11. **Don't stop hunting triads the moment you've decided a sentence is "signature."** Real
   miss, this session: I praised `hands that knew every angle of the chisel tip, how the
   grain worked, every trick the wood might try back` as "top-tier trade-grounded writing,
   keep" — and it was a same-opener triad (angle / grain / trick, two beats opening
   `every`) sitting in the sentence I'd waved through. The instant you label a line
   signature/keep, you stop reading it critically — that is exactly when the triad hides.
   **Run the acoustic triad test (item 9) on EVERY sentence in the paragraph: the keeps,
   the ones you're not touching, AND the new ones you just wrote. "I like this sentence" is
   not a triad exemption.** "Trade-grounded nouns" / "concrete craft detail" does NOT buy a
   three-beat list a pass — concrete *and* triadic is still a triad.
12. **Don't relabel a matched-three of `He`/`She` sentences as a "concrete-reversal that
   lands on a turn."** `He did not rush. He did not hesitate. He simply worked.` is a triad
   AND staccato AND a same-opener run — three matched sentences, all opening `He`. I
   defended it TWICE as signature because it "lands on a turn." It does not: the
   concrete-reversal keep lands on a **concrete CHOSEN thing** (`the scrappy, stubborn kind
   that grows between the cobblestones`). `He simply worked` is a generic activity, not a
   chosen object — so the run is a tell, not the keep. **Test for the concrete-reversal
   exemption: is the final beat a specific physical NOUN the author picked over the expected
   one? If the landing is an abstract activity/state (`simply worked`, `just sat there`,
   `kept going`), it is NOT concrete-reversal — it is a triad. Dissolve it.**
13. **Don't let a comma-list of three phrases off because the third is "a different
   grammatical type."** My own rewrite shipped a fresh triad: `He moved without hurry,
   without hesitation, the chisel sure in his hand` — I told myself it was "two negations
   plus an appositive tail," so not parallel. The reader's EAR doesn't parse grammar; it
   hears `A, B, C` — three comma-separated beats between the verb and the period. An
   absolute phrase / appositive / prepositional tail as the third slot is the same trench
   coat as item 9. **Count comma-separated beats acoustically, not by part of speech. Two
   beats then a tail can still land as three — if in doubt, split it into separate
   sentences.** And after writing ANY rewrite, re-read it adversarially as if someone else
   wrote it and you're hunting their triads — do not present until your own sentences pass
   the same test you'd apply to the original. (Author, this session, three rounds running:
   *"that is a triad why why just why"* — each time it was. I kept building new ones while
   fixing old ones.)
14. **When you keep producing triads, stop comma-piling — go to separate short sentences and
   an adverb opener.** The author's own escape hatch when a fix loops on triads: *"use an
   adverb, make a new sentence."* A one-clause sentence can't be a triad. The clean fix to
   #12/#13 above was `Gray-bearded and old, the man bent over his bench. Patiently, he set
   each cut. His hands never hesitated.` — discrete short sentences, one adverb-comma opener
   (`Patiently,`), zero three-beat lists. When the comma-list keeps betraying you, the
   answer is fewer commas, not cleverer nesting.

## STOP. Mandatory output checks before you present (the author keeps catching these)

These four failures shipped over and over in this pass and each one drew a sharp author
rebuke. Run this checklist on EVERY rewrite, out loud, before presenting. If any check
fails, REDO — do not present it.

1. **NO TWO ADJACENT SENTENCES MAY OPEN WITH THE SAME WORD in errand/action prose.** Three
   in a row anywhere is an automatic fail (the whole pass exists to kill it). *Two* in a row
   is allowed ONLY when it is deliberate anaphora doing real emotional work — paired refusal
   beats (`She did not shrug it off. She did not lean into it either.`), a grief-peak build,
   dialogue cadence. In plain narration two `He.`/`She.` openers back to back is laziness, not
   voice. Author, repeatedly: *"you STILL have two 'he' sentences after each other,"* *"there
   is soo much context there to start so many sentences without he."* **There is ALWAYS
   material in the surrounding paragraph to vary an opener — you are never forced into a
   repeat.** Tools, in order: (a) prepositional / time-place opener (`In her palm she counted
   the coins`, `Across the ocean…`, `Upstairs, her children slept…`); (b) object-fronting
   (`The joints he fit with the same patience…`); (c) change the grammatical subject (`Hers
   had been the first name…`, `The men on his payroll would have…`); (d) Bookfox adverb-comma
   (`Matter-of-factly, he recommended…`); (e) just COMBINE the two sentences into one
   (`He did not know that yet, only that the water was very blue.`). After writing, list the
   first word of every sentence in a column and look at it. Two the same touching? Redo.
   **And more broadly: actively AVOID `He`/`She`/`They`/`It` as sentence openers whenever
   the scene gives you anything else to lead with — place, time, the object, a named subject,
   a participial or adverbial phrase.** Pronoun openers are the default lazy choice; a
   paragraph that reads as a column of `He… She… He…` is a tell even when no two are
   adjacent. Author: *"your instructions should be clear to try and avoid he, she, they,
   sentence openers when possible."* Lead with a pronoun only when it is genuinely the
   strongest option (a deliberate refusal-beat or grief anaphora); otherwise vary it.
2. **A double-`and` sentence (`A and B and C`) IS a triad** — the ear hears three beats
   regardless of the conjunctions. Banned in your own rewrites, exactly like `A, B, and C`
   and three matched fragments. (The author's OWN original polysyndeton is his voice; leave
   it. The ban is on triads YOU create while fixing.) Author: *"a double 'and' sentence isn't
   very high level writing and also signifies a triad lol."*
3. **Every sentence must be a complete, clear thought you can read aloud without stumbling.**
   No garden-path opener (`With the same patience Doyle taught him he fit the joints` — reads
   as broken). No stranded verbless fragment where a real sentence belongs. The author wants
   full sentences with words and a finite verb — you have full flexibility to add/restructure
   to get there. Author: *"your second sentence is an incomplete thought lol."*
4. **CUT, don't re-punctuate.** Before reformatting a list, ask *is most of this even needed?*
   Kill redundant beats (an echo of the sentence beside it) and duplicate items (two beats that
   name the same thing). Land on the one beat that earns its place. Reformatting `X. Y. Z.`
   into `X, nor Y, nor Z` keeps the rule-of-three and reads junior — the author calls it
   "third-grade writing." See `feedback_triad_fix_cut_dont_repunctuate` in memory.

**Restraint (literary-historical standard).** Cut intensifiers and editorializers — `even`,
`just`, `simply`, `really`, `very`. The starker line is stronger and trusts the reader
(`They could not afford rings.` beats `They could not even afford rings.`). This is the
Mantel/Hemingway/Strunk-White discipline the genre's best practice rewards: state the hard
fact plainly and let it land; don't nudge the reader's feeling.

**Get creative — write like a bestselling historical novel, not a patched draft.** The goal
is prose a top editor passes, in HIS voice. Sweep the scene spatially or in time, re-body the
abstraction, find the sharper concrete noun. A two-`He` "fix" that merely deletes a tic is not
done; an opener that genuinely *varies* and a sentence that genuinely *reads better* is.

## You are REWRITING, not re-gluing (the core mandate)

The job is not to preserve every original noun and re-punctuate it. That is "semantic
surgery" — keeping `a schedule`, `a community`, `health and sickness` as abstract
nouns and just swapping the periods for commas. It produces unreadable noun-piles. The
author's note: *"You are not playing semantic surgeon, you are fixing bad writing by
replacing it with top-tier writing."*

- **You MAY introduce a few new words and concrete details** to shape a genuinely good
  sentence — as long as they are **true to the scene's established facts** (pull from
  the surrounding paragraphs; never invent plot) and **in voice**. Don't ransom the
  rewrite to the exact original vocabulary.
- **Re-body abstractions with the concrete thing underneath them (Matesic 2b/2c,
  `SENTENCE-VARIETY-LESSON.md`).** `Church` → *On Sundays they filled a pew.*
  `A community` → *the neighbors had learned all their names.* Replace concept-nouns
  with something the reader can see. This is the SAME instruction as VOICE.md's
  "re-route through the body, never strip."
- **Write for real sentence variety (Bookfox), not just tic-removal.** Vary length
  (short next to long-piling), vary openers (prepositional/time openers — his one
  on-voice opener fix). The result should read as prose a top editor would pass, not as
  a minimally-patched original.
- Still bounded by every hard constraint below (complete sentences, no triad, no
  staccato, no em dash, keeps preserved, Chicago typography). Better writing, in HIS
  voice — not generic "good writing," and not a museum restoration of the original nouns.

## The three target tics (detect, in priority order)

1. **Same-opener tic (HIGH).** 3+ sentences in a row opening with the same word,
   especially `He`/`She`. The dominant signal — chase these first.
2. **Staccato.** A stack of short sentence fragments trying to drive one thought home
   (`Dry. Clean. No mold.`). Three+ short beats in a row.
3. **Triads.** `A, B, and C` matched lists, OR three matched one-word fragments.

## The keep test (don't "fix" these)

- **Emotional peaks are signature, not tics.** Grief-ritual SVO runs (e.g. the death
  scenes), refusal beats (`He did not pour whiskey or sing or weep`), the screenplay-
  clipped parallel — leave them. Bookfox himself: repeated SVO has "music" when it's
  intentional. Only fix **errand prose** (a market trip, mailing a letter, walking a
  lot line) where the monotony does no emotional work.
- **Anaphora that lands on a withheld turn is a keep.** Three positives that build to
  a fourth concrete reversal (`She did not tell him to come home`) is not a triad —
  the turn earns the run. Don't flatten it.
- **Burstiness is healthy — but it is ONE short against an ADJACENT long, not a run.**
  *One* short sentence next to a long piling one is the voice; the contrast is the point.
  Three or four matched shorts in a row is staccato (tic #2), and a long sentence
  elsewhere in the paragraph does NOT buy them a pass — only a long sentence *touching*
  the short does. Count the consecutive-shorts run on its own; don't average it against
  the paragraph's overall texture. The tic is *unrelieved* sameness, not contrast.

## The Bookfox helper (the ONE on-voice opener fix)

From `SENTENCE-VARIETY-LESSON.md` Part 1. Of Bookfox's three named opener fixes, only
these are on-voice:

- **Prepositional / time-place opener** — ✅ primary tool. `In her palm she counted
  the coins`, `On the walk back she passed St. Stephen's`, `Outside, the valley was
  dark`. Drop it on the **worst SVO beat** in the run.
- **Adverb + comma opener** — ⚠️ sprinkle, never a staple. Source-honest split (don't
  blur these): **Bookfox endorses it** — it's one of his three named SVO-breakers, his
  own example `Carefully, he put the meat inside the crocodile's mouth`, and he puts **no
  frequency number on it** (his target is *unrelieved* SVO, not a count). The *"rarely"*
  is **this project's voiceprint overlay, not Bookfox** (VOICE.md: *"a single adverb-comma
  is fine, but it's not a strong part of your voiceprint; don't lean on it"*). So: allowed,
  but it's the weakest of the three levers — a fronted `-ly,` is conspicuous and a cluster
  reads amateur. Reach for the prepositional/place opener first; use the adverb-comma only
  when nothing else fits, and never two in a stretch. If asked "what does Bookfox say,"
  keep the line clear: he says *use it*; "rarely" is the voice refinement layered on top.
- **Dependent-clause opener** — ❌ NEVER (`When she…`, `Before she paid…`). Off-voice.

Other on-voice escape hatches (the author's own, not Bookfox's): fold the action into
the object (`pressing a few between her thumb and forefinger to find them dry`), change
the grammatical subject (`William had left her…`), comma-splice two beats into one.

## Hard constraints on every rewrite

- **No em dash.** VOICE.md flags them rare/off-voice. Check every fix.
- **No triad.** After the fix, recount parallel beats: exactly three matched → cut to
  two or build to four.
- **No staccato stack.** Dissolve three+ matched fragments into the flowing action.
- **Every rewritten sentence must be grammatically complete.** Find the finite main
  verb in each sentence you write. A long comma-pile of noun phrases with no
  subject+verb is a run-on fragment — hang the pile off a real main clause as
  apposition instead (`don't` item 6). Short punchy fragments (`Not today.`) are fine;
  a 30-word verbless pile is not.
- **Chicago typography.** Curly quotes + single spaces always; never inject straight
  quotes. (`reference_chicago_curly_standard`.) Curly apostrophes must match for
  find/replace.
- **At least one opener must actually vary.** If the only change was deletion, it's
  not done.

## Workflow

```
sync → scan/rank → pick (errand, not peak) → diagnose → ONE fix → PRESENT
   → wait for explicit approval → write Drive → rebuild epub → verify → mark done
```

### 1. Sync (always first — the cache goes stale)
```bash
bash .claude/skills/deai/sync.sh
```

### 2. Find the next offender
```bash
node .claude/skills/variety/bin/next.mjs        # top 6 ranked candidates
node .claude/skills/variety/bin/next.mjs 12      # more
```
Ranks errand-prose offenders by combined opener+staccato+triad severity and skips
anything already in `.deai/variety-done.json`. Apply the keep test by hand — the
scanner can't tell a grief ritual from a market trip. If the top hit is an emotional
peak, skip it and take the next.

### 3. Diagnose, out loud
Label what's tic vs what's signature/keep in the paragraph (bold the keeps). Name the
specific tic(s). This is how the author trusts the fix.

### 4. Propose ONE fix and STOP
**ALWAYS show the full BEFORE and AFTER paragraphs, in that order, as two labeled
blocks** — the author needs to read the offending paragraph in full against the rewrite
to judge how much it actually improves. Never present only the AFTER, and never show only
a snippet/diff of the changed sentence; quote the *entire* paragraph both times even when
the edit touches one sentence, so the rhythm is visible in context. Bold the changed span
in each block if it helps. Then add a short audit: which opener now varies (the Bookfox
helper used), beats recounted (no triad), no staccato stack, no em dash, keeps preserved.
Then **wait** — manuscript edits require explicit approval
(`feedback_book_edits_require_explicit_command`). Iterate on the author's pushback
before going near Drive.

### 5. On approval — write Drive (source of truth)
Use curly-quote-exact find/replace. The `find` is the ORIGINAL paragraph verbatim from
`read-doc`; `replace` is the approved rewrite.
```bash
node .claude/skills/book-edit/bin/read-doc.mjs > .deai/variety-doc.txt   # source the exact text
# build .deai/variety-edit.json = [{"find": "...original...", "replace": "...approved..."}]
node .claude/skills/book-edit/bin/edit-doc.mjs < .deai/variety-edit.json  # expect "occurrences": 1
```

### 6. Rebuild the served epub + refresh images
```bash
node scripts/build-epub-from-drive.mjs --promote
node scripts/extract-epub-images.mjs
```

### 7. Verify + mark done
```bash
node .claude/skills/book-edit/bin/read-doc.mjs > .deai/verify.txt
grep -c "…a unique phrase from the NEW text…" .deai/verify.txt   # expect 1
grep -c "…a unique phrase from the OLD text…" .deai/verify.txt   # expect 0
```
Then append the paragraph's 40-char head to `.deai/variety-done.json` so it never
resurfaces. Clean up scratch files (`.deai/variety-edit.json`, `variety-doc.txt`,
`verify.txt`).

## Done ledger

`.deai/variety-done.json` is a JSON array of 40-char paragraph heads (or `{ln, head}`
objects). `next.mjs` reads it to skip finished work. `.deai` is gitignored, so this is
a local working ledger — that's fine; it only prevents re-surfacing.
