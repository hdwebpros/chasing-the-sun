## Part 2 — Scorecard: where the novel already delivers

Credit first. These are not close calls — the manuscript *already embodies* a lot of what both channels teach:

- **The prologue passes every Matesic prologue rule (M2)** — one scene, short, differentiated, historical, and tension-loaded by the folded paper: *"Hollis does not know about this paper. He will, before the afternoon is done"* (l. 25). The three-questions test (M1) passes on page one: who is this man, why does "Boog" come from nowhere, what's on the paper.
- **The frame is load-bearing, not decorative.** The will-dictation *is* the plot engine; the paper teased at l. 25 is physically paid off at l. 2607–2621. That's Chekhov's Armory (B4) done right at novel scale.
- **Object-work is professional-grade:** the folded paper, the sign ritual, the X ritual, Mary Margaret's brooch — interiority carried by objects across decades. This is B7's bookending at book scale.
- **Dialogue (the author's claimed strength) is verified.** Only 3 adverb-tags in 80k words, 208 bare "said"s, beats doing tag work, and genuinely distinct voices: Doyle's needling Hiberno, Mary's dry fatalism (*"It goes down, William"*), Lottie's Kansas pragmatism, Hollis's measured formality. Bookfox's "dialogue ventriloquism" failure mode is absent.
- **Filter words are genuinely low** (`seemed` 10, `realized` 0 in 80k words) and **clichés are nearly absent** — the prose is built from objects and trades, exactly the B10/VOICE.md emotion-by-transfer model. George's death (l. 277–324) and the MARY X scene (l. 1459–1480) are case studies in lingering at the peak (B5).
- **Reader attachment runs on agency, not pity** (M7 adjacent): the boy in the rain with the hand-stitched scrap, letters learned upside down, the "quiet war" of the sign. A reader signs on at Doyle's door.
- **The want is explicit and early** (M3, M6): make the name mean something, chase the sun — stated by l. 138–179, well inside the first 10%.
- **The final button is elite.** *"Found ya"* (l. 2642) pays off l. 177 and l. 244 with zero editorializing — the exact opposite of M8's spoon-fed theme. The plain-register death notice after it is the right move. **Do not touch.**
- **Word count (~80k) is in band for adult historical (M13).** ✅

## Part 3 — The gaps

Ordered by leverage, each mapped to the advice it violates and the evidence in the draft.

### Gap 1 — The middle sags exactly the way the linked video describes (B1, M4, M5)
The stakes that open the book (survive, make the name mean something) are still the stakes in St. Paul — they never escalate, they just repeat in new cities. The opposition (disease, poverty, weather) has no agency, so for long stretches the only engine is dread. The two softest stretches:
- **Chapters 19–20 (l. 1163–1355):** ~190 lines of conflict-free accumulation — herbs, school, schoolhouse bid, citizenship, lots, house, horse. The stillborn (l. 1256–1270) is the lone shadow.
- **Chapter 30 (l. 2016–2150):** births-in-summary, foreclosure, telephone comedy — episodic drift until the ch. 31 panic attack snaps the spine back.

The book's true dramatic question — *is William himself the curse; can he ever stop running?* — is its tentpole, but it doesn't crystallize until l. 1542 (*"I am the curse"*). Bookfox's "change the desire mid-book" already *happens* (escape → build → outrun the curse → stop); it just isn't legible early enough to pull the reader through the prosperity chapters.

### Gap 2 — Chapters button instead of hook (B3 acceleration, M5)
Of 16 chapter endings sampled: **~12 close the lid restfully, ~4 hook.** The page-turning duty has been quietly outsourced to the Hollis interludes (*"the list of me children's names is longer,"* l. 1106 — superb), but the chapters themselves rarely contribute forward pull.

### Gap 3 — Scenes that exist because they happened, not because the story needs them (M5, B12)
True-story material that doesn't currently earn its place: Old Red's death (l. 1356–1365, labeled "Premonition" but foreshadows nothing specific), the telephone set piece (l. 2092–2134 — 40 delightful, stakes-free lines), the bell/shooting gallery (l. 1510–1511), the citizenship signing (l. 1296–1304), the third newspaper-ad scene (l. 2300–2313, with a duplicated punchline — *"Until I tell you to stop"* also at l. 1281). Bookfox's digression rule is the test: a disconnected chunk survives only if it echoes the theme or teaches William something.

### Gap 4 — A planted bombshell never fires (B4)
*"Tell the census I'm dead"* (l. 2228) is one of the most arresting lines in the book — raised once, never dramatized again. Chekhov violation on a gun this loud.

### Gap 5 — The climax is diffuse and the reckoning happens off-page (B5, M4)
Three climax candidates (Mary's death ~midpoint; the decision to abandon the family, l. 2186–2245 — the true structural climax; the desk breakdown, l. 2406–2420). What's missing is a **staged confrontation with the people he abandoned**: Alex never appears as a person; Charlotte's hurt is one beat (l. 2248); the 25-year "did the gamble pay off" question resolves in epilogue *dialogue summary* (l. 2595–2604) — told, not shown. Meanwhile 1907–1925 (~18 years) passes in ~25 lines (l. 2559–2583); Oregon, the thematic destination, gets the least scene-time of any setting.

### Gap 6 — The biggest arc in the book resolves in ten lines (B5, M8) — ✅ RESOLVED June 14
**Original diagnosis:** Sixty years of refusing God collapsed in a single paragraph **with a literal answering voice from heaven** (*"My child, I'm here as I've always been"*) — M8's "spoon-feeding" in its most literal possible form — where Mary's death got ten pages.
**Resolution (live in Drive doc, verified):** the quoted divine reply and the present-tense slip (*"He looks to the clouds and says"*) are cut. The arc is now dramatized across a full door sequence: William stands in the door as Mary stood in hers, asks *"What do you want with a bastard like me?"*, gets **no answering voice** — and instead *notices* the door that never latched (the one presence that never left), inverting his childhood curse. The child-of-God payoff lands as his own recognition (*"He was somebody's child after all. He always had been."*) plus the empty-seat reveal, not as narration. The epilogue's restrained *"a door opened for me at the right time"* now rhymes with an earned image rather than a spoon-fed one.

### Gap 7 — A handful of under-felt beats and emotion labels (B10, M10, VOICE.md's own toolkit)
- **John's death in four summary lines** (l. 989–992) — the death that leaves them with *no sons left*; one held beat of William's body would pay.
- *"He felt guilt and embarrassment"* (l. 457) — a bare label exactly where the VOICE.md toolkit demands body.
- *"Damn it Austin. Fight!" / "It's not supposed to be like this!"* (l. 931–933) — the withheld voice breaking into melodrama at a peak.
- Ch. 32: seven years alone in Oregon rendered as business actions with a single interior note (l. 2297).
- Early William is nearly frictionless (M7): his only pre-1865 defeat is the parentage revelation. One small, earned failure (a botched boot, a lost customer) would make the competence feel won.

### Gap 8 — Line-level metronomes (M9, B9)
Grep-verified across the draft:
- **~156 clipped "He [verb]" sentence-openers** ("He looked" ×51, "He stood" ×38, "He watched/walked/sat" ×19 each). The visual channel is saturated: `look*` 267, `watched` 114. Individually signature; in aggregate, late-book scenes blur into one camera move.
- **"Something" ×157** — a large subset is the unnamed-something emotion shim (*"something shifted in his expression,"* l. 102; *"something happened to his face,"* l. 1989). The withholding is his; the withheld thing should be a concrete gesture, not a pronoun.
- **"small" ×115** (incl. "the smallest" ×8 — known tic), **"quiet" ×97**.
- **"The lamp burned low" ×5 + "burned out" ×1, all between l. 1515–2535** — six lamp-endings in the final third.

### Gap 9 — Specific opening/ending blemishes (B3, M8)
- **Theme stated aloud on page 2:** Hollis's *"Your name turns into something that outlasts you"* (l. 23) editorializes the whole book before it starts.
- **Doubled birth beat:** l. 41 and l. 45 run the identical scream/brokers-pause/furniture-maker sequence twice — a drafting seam.
- **Chapter One's title ("Raw, furious, and unmistakably alive") spoils its own climax line** (l. 46).
- **Epilogue Hollis degrades into an interview machine:** *"you have to tell me, for the sake of your beneficiaries, if your gamble on your curse paid off"* (l. 2595) is an exposition prompt, not lawyer speech — M11's "as you know, Bob" in costume.
- Small: *"drop her gaze or drop her gaze"* (l. 148, typo); *"the kind of faces of children waiting for soup make"* (l. 75, broken grammar); the wrapped-around-her-finger + heart-strings stacked clichés (l. 1900); "Based on a True Story" on the title page (film convention — agents expect it in the query/author's note); grandfather-of-many (l. 2600) vs. *"nineteen grandchildren he never got to meet"* (l. 2646) reads as a contradiction without one clarifying beat.

### Gap 10 — Secondary-cast loose ends (B11)
Mary Margaret is thin until her (superb) death scene — she's owed one earlier scene that is *hers*. Doyle is unforgettable and then vanishes — a letter or news of his death closes the loop. Lottie's six children except William Travers, plus Joseph and John, are nearly birth-certificate entries; either give each one marker trait or name fewer of them.

### ⚠️ Where the YouTubers' advice should be *resisted*
Three of their rules conflict with the established voiceprint (VOICE.md), and the voice wins:
- **B9 (vary openers with dependent clauses):** thin the "He looked/stood" density, yes — but by folding the look into the object seen or varying the subject, *not* by adding subordinate-clause openers, which are off-voice. Burstiness and fragments stay.
- **Bookfox's "present tense is a beginner tell":** the present-tense intrusions in Ch. 4/7 are confirmed signature. Preserve.
- **M10 already grants this:** the book's strategic telling (*"He was in disbelief"* in grief) is sanctioned by VOICE.md. The flag is only for bare abstract labels with no grounding.

## Part 4 — Implementation plan

Sequenced macro → micro per B12/M12 ("repainting a car that has no engine"). All manuscript changes go through the established workflow: **flag-and-suggest, author approves, then `book-edit` applies to the Drive doc and rebuilds the epub.** Nothing below gets written into the book without an explicit go.

### Phase 1 — Structural (the engine) — highest leverage

1. **Surface the curse question early to tentpole the middle (Gap 1, B1).**
   The fix is not new events — it's making the existing dramatic question legible sooner. Concretely: activate Hollis in the first two interludes (l. 385–393, 995–1011), where he is currently a passive stenographer. His later turn to prosecutor (*"Was the life you were building the thing that wore her out?"* l. 1534) is the smartest move in the book — seed smaller versions of that pushback from interlude one, so every prosperity chapter is read under cross-examination. The reader then carries "is he the curse?" through St. Paul, and the conflict-free accumulation chapters acquire dread for free.
2. **Make each true-story set piece pay rent or cut it (Gap 3, M5).** Per scene: telephone set piece → keep, but give it a job (e.g., the voice on the wire as a thing that arrives without traveling — the antithesis of William's whole life; one beat does it). Old Red → either tie the premonition to something specific or fold to two lines. Citizenship signing, bell/shooting gallery, third ad scene → compress into adjacent chapters; kill the duplicated *"Until I tell you to stop"* punchline (keep the first, l. 1281).
3. **Fire the census gun (Gap 4, B4).** One scene or even one paid-off beat: the census taker actually comes to the St. Paul door, or the lie surfaces in the Lottie porch confrontation or the epilogue. Cheap to add, large payoff.
4. **Stage one on-page reckoning with the abandoned family (Gap 5).** The single biggest structural addition worth making: one scene — Alex or Charlotte, in Oregon or by letter that gets a scene's weight — between the breakdown (ch. 35) and the reconciliation arc. Lottie's porch indictment (l. 2477–2491) currently carries all of it alone.
5. ~~**Give chapter 38 runway (Gap 6).**~~ ✅ **Done (June 14).** God's quoted dialogue is cut and the spiritual turn is now a dramatized door sequence (recognition + curse-inversion + empty-seat reveal). See Gap 6 resolution above.
6. **Show one beat of the 18 elided years (Gap 5, M4)** — not a chapter; one scene of the Oregon stillness *working* (the bird journal again, or the grandchildren's letters), so "did the gamble pay off" is witnessed rather than testified.

### Phase 2 — Scene-level

7. **Convert 6–8 chapter buttons into hooks (Gap 2).** Candidates with cheap conversions: ch. 3, 6, 14, 15, 20, 23, 29. The mechanic the book already owns: end on the unanswered question or the object mid-motion instead of the object at rest. (Ch. 5's *"None of it enough"* and ch. 12's *"Don't tempt fate"* are the in-book models.)
8. **Pay the under-felt beats (Gap 7)** using the VOICE.md toolkit (body, object, withheld gesture — never a label): John's death gets a held beat; l. 457's "guilt and embarrassment" re-routes through the body; Austin's deathbed dialogue (l. 931–933) pulls back to the withheld register; ch. 32 gets two or three interior notes across the seven alone years.
9. **One early earned defeat for young William (Gap 7/M7)** — a botched boot or lost customer in the Doyle years, before 1865.
10. **Secondary-cast fixes (Gap 10):** one scene that belongs to Mary Margaret (Boston or St. Paul); one Doyle callback (a letter, or news of his death); marker traits or fewer names for the younger children.
11. **De-theatricalize the epilogue Q&A (Gap 9, M11):** rewrite Hollis's two interview-machine prompts (l. 2595, 2599) as lawyer questions with the same yield; fix the register wobbles (*"true 100% of the time,"* l. 2596; *"purely out of spite of my detractors,"* l. 1551).

### Phase 3 — Line-level passes (after Phases 1–2 settle, since they'll change the text)

12. **Run `/tic` passes** — the existing tooling is built for exactly M9's "hunt your pet phrases":
    - `the lamp burned low` (×5–6, deterministic, easy)
    - the **"something"-shim** (judge each against VOICE.md: withheld-but-concrete = keep, pronoun-evasion = re-body; expect ~40% thinning of 157)
    - **"He [looked/stood/watched]" opener density** (~156; thin by folding the look into the object or varying the subject — never by adding subordinate openers)
    - `small`/`the smallest` and `quiet` (lighter touch; "the smallest" is already a known tic)
13. **Surgical fixes via `book-edit`** (each needs explicit approval): the doubled birth beat (l. 41/45); Hollis's theme-speech on p. 2 (l. 23 — replace with a plainer lawyer's question); the Chapter One title (retitle so l. 46 keeps its surprise); the l. 148 typo; the l. 75 grammar; the l. 1900 stacked clichés; the grandfather/never-met contradiction (one clarifying beat); move "Based on a True Story" off the title page into front-/back-matter; move the website URL off the dedication page.

### Phase 4 — Validation (M14, B8)

14. **Reverse-outline check after Phase 1–2 edits:** one line per scene; verify every scene now answers to the central question and the middle has an escalation shape.
15. **Cold readers.** Both editors are emphatic that structure is invisible to the author (B8) and that you're done when feedback goes from story-level to tweaks (M14). After the passes above, 2–3 beta readers who read historical fiction, with three questions only: where did you skim, where were you confused, where did you cry.
16. **Don't over-revise the strengths.** The prologue, the X ritual, George's death, Mary's "No," the Hollis confrontation, the breakdown, and *"Found ya"* are done. Bookfox's warning cuts both ways: writers polish what's already good and miss the blind spots — the blind spots are listed above; the polished parts should be left alone.
