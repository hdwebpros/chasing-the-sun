# Variety — Diagnosing Sentence Monotony & Sentence-Level Tics

**Why it matters.** This is the *diagnostic* sentence lens: it detects flatness rather than building beauty. Where [[sentence/sentence-craft]] is constructive (how to make one sentence land) and [[sentence/power]] is scored (a rubric of features), this file is the eye that catches a page reading like a metronome — wall-to-wall SVO, every sentence opening on the same word, a pet phrase clustering, a triad climbing to a concept. The trap, though, is that every one of those shapes is *also* a signature when used on purpose. So the whole instrument is built around one judgment: signature or tic. **The author decides, per sentence; this lens only helps you see.**

> **The governing rule, before any fix.** The shape is not the tic. A fragment, a triad, a same-opener run — most of these are the author's voice. The tic is the **abstract version of the shape**: the run that climbs to a *concept* or *explains its own meaning* instead of landing on a chosen physical thing. So nearly every fix is **subtraction**: cut the abstract beat or the explainer tail, keep (or re-body) the concrete one. The de-AI proof case: *"despair"* became *"their eyes were sunken."* When in doubt, **keep.**

## The diagnostic principles

### 1. SVO monotony — the unrelieved metronome

**Rule.** Watch for runs of Subject-Verb-Object sentences of uniform length and shape. *Unrelieved* SVO dulls the reader; SVO itself is fine, and repeated on purpose it has music.

**Why it works.** Bookfox, verbatim: *"If you just write SVO sentences, they get very, very boring, and you will put all of your readers to sleep."* The ear registers change, not sameness; a uniform run flattens into a drone the reader stops hearing. But note what he does *not* say — he puts **no number on it** ("three in a row"), and he is explicit that intentional SVO has "a kind of music." His complaint is *unrelieved*, not *present*.

**Example (before / after).**
> Before: *He started in town. The first lot was on the southwest edge of downtown. He walked the raw clay with his stick and studied the survey stakes. He stood on the corner for twenty minutes watching foot traffic before he made up his mind.*

> After: *He started in town. The first lot sat on the southwest edge of downtown, the streets still being graded. On the corner, he stood for twenty minutes watching foot traffic, pricing it in his head, before he made up his mind.*

The fix fronts a place-phrase and folds two SVO beats into one. A dependent-clause or participial opener (*"Having studied the stakes, he…"*) would serve here just as well — **no opener style is off-voice.** Pick whichever is the best writing for the sentence; the only aim is to stop leaning on bare subject-led entrances.

**Failure mode / when.** Diagnose only the runs doing no job — errand prose, stage business, a survey of a low-stakes scene. Leave the peaks: a grief-ritual run of six "He [verb]" sentences after a death is the music, not the mistake. Diagnosis is locating *unrelieved* runs in flat passages, not flagging SVO on sight.

### 2. Opener pile-ups — the same word starting sentence after sentence

**Rule.** Watch for three or more consecutive sentences opening on the same subject word (*He… He… He…* / *She… She… She…* / *The… The…*). Split the deliberate rhetorical build (keep) from the accidental subject-stack (thin).

**Why it works.** Deliberate builds — runs opening on *Not / No / For / This was / The same* — are stacked-parallel signature and almost always land on a concrete payoff; keep them. The accidental metronome is where the same subject simply piles up because the writer never varied the entrance. The reader's ear clocks the repetition even when no single sentence is wrong; it is an *aggregate* tell, visible in the count more than in any one line.

**Example (before / after).**
> Before: *She cleaned the room. She organized the desk. She answered the letters. She paid the taxes early. She arranged for a neighbor to check the house…*

> After (vary an opener, cut redundant verbs): break the lockstep so at least one sentence enters differently, and drop the beats that merely echo.

**Failure mode / when.** A flat behavioral inventory in lockstep before the emotion lands is the tic. A withholding run that lands on a turn (*"He stood up. He did not hug her. He was not a man who did that easily."*) is signature. **House rule below governs the fix — do not collapse the run into a comma list, because that manufactures a triad.**

### 3. Pet phrases — the construction you love and don't notice

**Rule.** A pet phrase is *"a unique, descriptive turn of phrase that YOU love to use — and don't realize you're repeating"* (Matesic). The threshold is **clustering, not raw count**: *"If you say 'gold-flecked eyes' three times on one page, you need to vary the prose."*

**Why it works.** *"You won't catch these on a read-through because they sound natural to you, but your reader will notice… once they do, you've broken their immersion"* (Matesic). Her own field example: one author tagged nearly every character with *"[did X] with the [Y] of someone who [Z]"* — **13 times in the first few chapters.** That is the same "the way a…" simile shape a voiceprint already flags. Two independent sources, one conclusion.

**Example (before / after).**
> Before: *…and something happened to his face that he could not control.*

> After (the concrete version was already sitting in the next line; promote it): *…and his face moved before he could stop it. His cheeks pushed up toward his eyes.*

**Failure mode / when.** Keep the instances paid off in the same breath (a vague word followed immediately by its concrete image). Reword only where the phrase clusters or sits abstract. The method is deterministic: search every instance, reword the repeated ones substantially, do not touch the ones earning their place. The bar is "don't let it cluster," never "never use the word."

### 4. Stock gestures — the borrowed body language

**Rule.** Watch for the inherited gesture catalogue: *"letting out a breath they didn't know they were holding,"* feet *"padding down a hall,"* smiles that *"didn't reach their eyes."* These read as borrowed, not observed.

**Why it works.** *"These make prose sound amateurish and seasoned readers will pick up on it"* (Matesic). The fix is **not deletion**. Re-body the gesture with a fresher, more specific physical detail: instead of a held breath, *"the air hissed behind her teeth,"* or show the response through *"shoulders relaxing."* Describe the response *"in another way"* with fresher language — which is identical to the voiceprint instruction to re-route the feeling through the body, never strip it.

**Failure mode / when.** If a fix ever leaves *less* concrete physicality than the cliché it replaced, it has been misapplied. Re-body, never abstract.

### 5. Triad / rule-of-three overuse — the tic that fixing creates

**Rule.** A triad is acoustic: the reader *hears* three matched beats between a clause and a stop. Three shapes recur — `A, B, and C` (comma triad), three consecutive short fragments (`Dry. Clean. No mold.`), and three sentences on the same opener. Verb-sequence triads (*"He finished his section, moved the ladder, and finished the next"*) are natural compression — keep them. The tic is the **abstract / adjectival / noun-list triad**, especially one that climbs to a concept or caps a beat with a summary.

**Why it works (as a diagnostic).** A matched three reads as polished-but-junior precisely when nothing forced it to three — the writer reached for the symmetry, not the content. Severity ranks by sound: a **staccato** fragment triad (all three beats tiny) and a **same-opener** triad hit hardest; a plain comma triad is mildest and is usually natural prose. The two worst signals stack: staccato *and* same-opener at once is the top tier.

**Example (before / after).**
> Before: *the sky go on wide, deep, indifferent, and beautiful.*

> After: *the sky go on past the last rooftop with nothing to stop it.*

The climb to *"beautiful"* is the tic; one concrete image replaces the adjective stack.

**Failure mode / when.** A run of **four** is sanctioned; **three** is the trap — *two blunt beats, or four, never the smooth matched three.* A four-item list (*"A man. A bench. A valley. An empty seat."*) is not flagged; a four-adjective build is. The same-opener anaphora often earns its three positives only because it lands on a withheld fourth beat (*"She did not tell him to come home"*) — concrete-reversal, not a triad. Drop that fourth turn and the surviving three become a naked triad.

## The operating rules (house — non-negotiable)

These come from the author's own feedback and override generic "vary your sentences" advice. They are the load-bearing part of this file.

**A. A negation/contrast fragment is often the signature, not a tic.** The test: **concrete lands = keep; abstract dissolves = tic.** *"He did not scream. He ground his teeth."* lands concrete — keep. *"It was not grief. It was not fear. It was the animal awareness of…"* dissolves into a concept — thin. **Judge by ear, against the best-writing test, first.** Never blanket-thin a shape because it recurs; the count is a signal to *look*, not a quota to cut.

**B. Fixing a "He/She" pile-up must not create a triad.** Collapsing three clipped parallel sentences into one polished `X, Y, and Z` comma list trades one tell for another. Use **two beats or four**, never the matched three. **Recount the parallel beats after every fix.** If a rewrite leaves you with exactly three parallel items, drop the weakest to two or build to four.
> Before (the self-inflicted wound): *…telling him about Charlotte and the books, the snow, and Alex's questions.* (a matched three-beat list — a triad)
> After: *…telling him Charlotte had taken over the books and Alex was starting to ask questions.* (two beats — uneven, additive)

**C. Fix triads by cutting, not re-punctuating. Subtraction first.** *"nor since X, nor since Y"* is junior writing. First ask whether the beats are even needed: is one an echo of a sentence above it? Do two name the same place? Cut the redundant ones and land on the one that earns it — do not just re-punctuate the same three beats into a smoother list.
> Worked example: *"Load. Stroke. Wipe."* echoed the sensory sentence right above it, and the trailing *"Since Prince Street. Since the brownstones of Boston…"* named Boston twice. The fix dropped the echo and the duplicate waypoint and landed on the one that mattered (Dublin, the origin) — a cut, not a re-punctuation.

**D. Never introduce a triad while fixing something else.** This is the most common new damage. A fix that resolves an opener run or a staccato stack but leaves three matched beats has not improved the prose. Practice it in your own editorial prose too: when listing, prefer two or four.

**E. A fix isn't done when the tic is gone — it's done when an opener actually varies.** Cutting "She / She / She" down to one "She" is subtraction; it does not make the prose move. Vary the entrance on the worst beat — a prepositional or time/place phrase, a subordinate clause, a participial, an adverb, a conjunction, an inversion: whichever is the best writing. **Every opener style is fair game.** After every fix, check: did at least one sentence now *open* differently, or did I only delete?

**F. Vary the opener STYLE, and lean on bare subject-led far less.** *(Author rule, June 2026 — this is a positive target, not only a tic hunt.)* The bare subject-led entrance (*[Name]/[The]/[He] [verb]*) is the manuscript's default and is overused; use it **noticeably more sparingly**. The varied styles should be **more prominent** in the mix — prepositional / time-place (**P**), subordinate clause (**C**), conjunction (**J**), adverbial (**A**), participial (**G**), inversion (**I**). None is off-voice; reach for whichever is the best writing for the sentence.
- **The flag: four sentences in a row opening on the same style** — *any* style, not just subject-led — raise it and vary at least one. (Same-style monoculture in a *varied* style is also a flag; don't trade an S-wall for a P-wall.)
- **The aim is a richer mix of entrances,** with subject-led as one colour among several rather than the wall-to-wall default — *not* the elimination of subject-led prose. A genuine signature run (a grief cadence, a deliberate withholding build) is still the music; keep it. "Sparingly" governs the **default / errand** prose, where the bare entrance simply piled up because it was never varied.

> **Sequencing.** Per Matesic, this is the *last* pass, not the first: *"making every sentence sparkle is like repainting a car that has no engine."* Run it after structure is sound.
