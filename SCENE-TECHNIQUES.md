# Scene-Technique Pass — Chasing the Sun

Lens: John Matthew Fox / Bookfox's 8 scene techniques (`scenetranscript.md`).
Method: whole manuscript read in five sections by scout agents, each scene judged against the
8 techniques *and* `RYAN-HOUSE-STYLE.md`. Flag-and-suggest only. Line numbers are
`.deai/manuscript.txt` (synced from Drive, 81,206 words). Nothing here is written to Drive.

## The headline finding

The book already runs four of these techniques at a high level — **embody-emotion-in-an-object**
(the folded paper with the X's, the sign, the brooch, the deeds, the compass, the apron),
**unexpected-reaction** ("...that are still alive, anyhow"), **tonal-shift** (the Queenstown
departure, the carollers against the deathbed), and **dialogue-through-an-item** (the Hollis
pen, the hand-washing cloth). On the major beats the honest call is **protect, don't add.**

So the real opportunities split two ways:

1. **Subtraction at the seams** (the dominant pattern across all five sections). The prose keeps
   *enacting* a technique and then adding a sentence that *explains* it. Trust the object / the
   cut / the reaction; cut the gloss. This is the highest-yield, lowest-risk pass and it is pure
   house style.
2. **Addition in the low-stakes connective tissue** — exactly the scenes you named (bees, phone,
   land, the painting). A handful of minor scenes are talking-heads with no physical anchor, or
   minor characters with laundry-list intros, or flat "and then" transitions. These are safe to
   experiment on and are where a technique genuinely adds something.

The two genuinely *under-used* techniques are **one-shot description** (minor characters keep
getting 3–4 generic traits instead of one salient tell) and **the single second-person slip**
(the book's own pain could justify it exactly once — see the caution below).

## B) The named scenes you called out

- **Bees** — see #1 and #2 above. Both are subtractions; the scene already half-does the
  unexpected-reaction (a grieving, controlled man undone by helpless laughter while children
  scream). Just stop pre-explaining it.
- **Phone scene** — see #3. The object work is already strong (crank, earpiece, the shelf
  rattling); the only flat patch is the interior monologue justifying why he shouts.
- **Buying land scenes** — these are mostly **protect, not improve**. The deeds/dirt material is
  already the book's best embody-in-object work: the Carroll Avenue deed a daughter slides back
  (1900–1911), "Ireland and Meade's Addition" stopping him cold on the plat map (2401–2407), the
  wall-of-ownership winter-table beat (1870–1874). The one *add* is the wall-of-ownership theme
  is sometimes told twice; trim the trailing thesis where the deed already says it.
- **"Girls painting" scene** — there is no scene of the daughters painting *together*. The two
  things that exist: **Rose's small canvas of the Liffey** (2011–2021, Ch 29) — the book's single
  best object-beat, leave it alone except for thinning the explain-after paragraph (2019/2021) —
  and **Mary Margaret carrying paint cans** "with the same focus that a young William once had"
  (1052), a single flat sentence that *could* seed a brush-handed-down object thread but may be
  deliberate setup paid off elsewhere. Flagging it; not prescribing.

## D) Transitions worth juicing (high-expectation → hard-letdown / time-skip)

- **Castle Garden stamp → the gulls** (658–660, Ch 10): end on "Welcome to America / the stamp came
  down" and cut straight to the hustlers who "circled the dazed immigrants like gulls." Let the
  welcome get answered by con-men. (The "processed in under two minutes" line may be better kept;
  test, don't force.)
- **The goose feast → Teresa's labored breathing** (898→901, Ch 14): don't soften the warm scene;
  harden the *seam*. "The evenings returned to what they had been" is a cushion — a flatter cut from
  the loud warm table straight into the thick breathing weaponizes the warmth you just built.
- **The Irish Times ad → result** (421–442 / 480, Ch 7): the boast ("Let them come") currently cuts
  to a flat date-stamp; the payoff (the man with the pencil-circled clipping) is stranded 40 lines
  later. Pulling the result nearer makes the bravado land against its consequence. (Watch the birth
  chronology if reordering.)
- **The crying-barn banter → the fall** (2355→2362, Ch 32→33): the "permission was on a timer"
  stinger already juices this. Just make sure no flat connective line softens the irony of cutting
  from peak confidence to "found a wet rung and did not hold." Probably already working.
- **Selling the Evergreen house** (1861–1863, Ch 26): already a near-model (the ledger and the
  clerk's pen carry the loss). Leave it; do not gild.

## E) The second-person slip — spend it ONCE, if at all

The brief nominates the book's worst pain for a one-time dissociative slip into "you." Every scout
flagged a candidate; the consensus is **the device is risky and the book is committed third
person, so it can be spent at most once across the whole novel, if ever.** Note the book *already*
uses second person in the Ch 9 opening ("the smell was the first thing you noticed") and Ch 11, so
it is not virgin.

Ranked candidates if you ever use it:
1. **Mary's death montage** (1487–1491, Ch 22) — "she saw it all at once." The highest pay, and the
   most defensible single site. If you use it nowhere else, here.
2. **The fall, the inner snap** (2362–2372, Ch 33) — one clause as the body stops obeying. But this
   spends it on *physical* pain, and risks reading as a tic so near the Ch 35 breakdown.
3. **The unnamed stillborn boy** (1294–1295, Ch 19) — weaker than Mary's death; don't spend it here.

Every scout's recommendation: most likely **leave all three flat.** This is the one technique where
the right answer is probably restraint.

## F) Protect — do not "improve" these (they already do the technique)

Embody-in-object, working at master level: the folded-paper / X motif (260–265, 1503–1511,
2652–2666), the brooch arc (1153 → 1976–1982 → 2274 → 2487–2490), the Carroll Ave deed sliding back
(1900–1911), Rose's Liffey canvas (2011–2021), the hand-washing cloth and reappearing bandage
(758–767), the will + compass in the Epilogue (2652–2700). Unexpected-reaction done right:
"...that are still alive, anyhow" (501–503), Mary's "No" and her catalogue of yeses (538–545),
Rose's birth "Not dramatically. Just two tears" (1255–1262). Tonal shift done right: the birth and
wedding undercuts (41–65, 194–208), the Queenstown departure (601–611), the ascites diagnosis cut
(1403→1405). Dialogue-through-item done right: the Hollis pen across the confrontation (1549–1592),
the paint-chip-as-argument schoolhouse bid (1309–1317), Lottie's ring and brooch catching light
through her porch reckoning (2500–2518). Trickery: the Hollis-frame "Longer." withheld reveal, and
the compass-decoy at the kitchen table (2247–2252).

Leave the deaths plain. The scouts agreed the child deaths (Austin 929–945, Teresa 953–959, Joseph
825–837) and the breakdown (2433–2447) win by restraint; applying a technique for its own sake
would overwork grief that already lands.

## One cross-cutting craft note

If you do only one thing from this document: run the **subtraction pass in section C**. It is the
same fix the de-AI and house-style work already favors (cut the explain-after-show), it touches a
dozen scenes, and it sharpens every technique the book already owns without risking the motifs. The
*additive* work in section A is the fun, low-stakes experimentation — the bees, the phone, the
hawk, the ad — and is where a brand-new technique actually buys you something.
